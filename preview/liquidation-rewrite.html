Which Type of Liquidation Applies to You?

Work out which of these sounds closest to your situation:

  • You want to close the company yourself — Creditors' Voluntary Liquidation (CVL). You appoint the IP. You control the timing. This is the focus of this page.
  • A creditor has filed a petition against you — Compulsory liquidation. Court-ordered. The Official Receiver takes control, not you. Covered below.
  • You're not sure if the company is insolvent — Take the 30-second insolvency test to get a clearer picture.

If your company is solvent and can pay all debts in full, you need an MVL (Members' Voluntary Liquidation) instead. That's a different process with different rules, covered in our dedicated MVL guide.

If the business itself is viable but carrying legacy debt, a Company Voluntary Arrangement (CVA) may be more appropriate than liquidation. Liquidation ends the company permanently.

How Company Liquidation Works

Liquidation is the legal process that ends a limited company by turning its assets into cash and distributing it to creditors. A licensed insolvency practitioner takes control, settles debts in a legally defined order, and investigates how the company was run, including director conduct.

Once appointed, the IP manages everything: collecting debts owed to the company, selling assets, making employees redundant (if still employed), and paying creditors in the order set by law. Secured creditors are paid first, then preferential creditors (employees owed wages and holiday pay), then unsecured creditors. In practice, unsecured creditors often receive little or nothing.

Directors must cooperate fully: hand over books and records, attend meetings if required, and answer questions about transactions going back years. The IP will file a report on your conduct with the Insolvency Service. This isn't a background process — it requires your active involvement.

Of roughly 25,000 companies entering liquidation in 2024-25, the Insolvency Service secured just 1,037 disqualification orders. Those were overwhelmingly for serious misconduct, not for directors who acted responsibly.

Creditors' Voluntary Liquidation (CVL)

A CVL is the most common form of insolvent liquidation. In 2024-25, CVLs accounted for more than 75% of all company insolvencies in England and Wales, according to the Insolvency Service's quarterly statistics.

How the process works:

  1. You decide the company can't continue
  2. You appoint a licensed insolvency practitioner
  3. The shareholders pass a resolution to wind up the company
  4. The IP takes control and creditors approve (or replace) the appointment
  5. The IP realises assets, investigates conduct, and distributes to creditors

Why most directors choose this route: you choose the IP, you control the timing, and you've demonstrated responsible conduct by acting when you recognised insolvency. Directors who act early and cooperate tend to receive cleaner conduct reports.

What happens to employees: if the company still has staff when it enters CVL, the IP will make them redundant. Employees can claim statutory redundancy pay, unpaid wages, and holiday pay from the government's Redundancy Payments Service. This applies to directors too, if they were genuinely employed (on payroll, with a contract of employment).

The cost question: a CVL typically costs £4,000 to £6,000 plus VAT in practitioner fees. If the company has assets, fees come from those. If it doesn't, directors usually need to fund it personally. However, the director's own redundancy claim can offset or cover the cost entirely. Many directors assume they can't afford a CVL, then find redundancy pays for it.

Timeline: the initial process from board meeting to the start of liquidation typically takes 2 to 4 weeks. The full process, including asset realisation and final distribution, usually takes 12 to 18 months for straightforward cases.

Compulsory Liquidation: When a Creditor Forces It

Compulsory liquidation is court-ordered. It starts when a creditor, most commonly HMRC, files a winding-up petition at the High Court.

What happens and when:

  • Petition filed: a creditor applies to the court to wind up your company
  • Gazette publication: the petition is advertised in the London Gazette
  • Bank accounts frozen: most banks freeze accounts within 24 hours of the Gazette notice
  • Official Receiver appointed: they take control, investigate director conduct, and manage the liquidation

The Official Receiver doesn't work for you. Their job is to protect creditors and investigate how the company ended up here. The process is slower, more expensive, and more invasive than a voluntary route. You lose all control over timing and costs.

HMRC now accounts for roughly 60% of all winding-up petitions. The COVID-era enforcement pause is over. Directors who assumed tax debts could be pushed down the road are finding that HMRC is now petitioning faster and with less warning than at any point in the last five years.

The Gazette publication: once a petition is filed, it's advertised in the London Gazette. Most banks monitor the Gazette automatically and freeze company accounts within 24 hours. That means no payroll, no supplier payments, no trading. For many directors, the bank freeze is the moment the business effectively stops.

What Directors Risk in Liquidation

The company is a legal entity, but the consequences can reach you personally. Here are the specific areas of risk:

  • Wrongful trading: continuing to trade when you knew, or should have known, the company couldn't avoid insolvency. The court can order personal contributions to creditor losses.
  • Preference payments: paying yourself, family, or connected creditors ahead of others in the run-up to liquidation. These can be clawed back.
  • Transactions at undervalue: selling company assets below market value, particularly to connected parties.
  • Overdrawn director's loan: if you owe money to the company (DLA in debit), the liquidator will pursue repayment. This is one of the most common and most avoidable issues.
  • Bounce Back Loan misuse: if BBL funds weren't used for the business's economic benefit, the liquidator will investigate. Disqualification periods for BBL misuse have ranged from 7 to 15 years. Our guide on BBL default consequences covers this in detail.

✅ Do

  • Seek advice from a licensed insolvency practitioner as soon as you suspect the company is insolvent
  • Keep accurate, up-to-date financial records from today onwards
  • Treat all creditors equally — do not pay family, yourself, or connected parties ahead of HMRC or suppliers
  • Document every decision and the reasoning behind it
  • Cooperate fully with the IP once appointed

❌ Don't

  • Continue trading and taking on new credit if there's no realistic prospect of repaying it
  • Pay yourself preferentially from company funds once you suspect insolvency
  • Transfer assets to family, friends, or connected companies at below market value
  • Destroy, conceal, or falsify company records
  • Attempt to dissolve the company to avoid dealing with creditors

Directors who sought advice early, documented their decisions, and cooperated with the process come through cleanest.

How Much Liquidation Costs

  • CVL (insolvent): £4,000 to £6,000 + VAT. Directors may offset via a redundancy claim.
  • Compulsory liquidation: £3,480+ upfront (court fee £1,880 + Official Receiver deposit £1,600), plus ongoing costs you don't control. Almost always more expensive overall than a voluntary route.

Costs depend on the number of creditors, asset complexity, whether employees need redundancy, and whether investigations are required. A single-director company with no assets sits at the lower end. A company with stock, debtors, property, and employees will be higher.

When Liquidation Isn't the Right Route

Liquidation ends the company permanently. If one of these applies, a different route may be more appropriate:

  • The company is solvent — you need an MVL, not a CVL. Different process, different rules, different tax treatment.
  • The business is viable but carrying legacy debt — a Company Voluntary Arrangement (CVA) lets the company continue trading while repaying creditors over 3 to 5 years. Requires 75% creditor approval by value.
  • The business has significant assets, employees, or contracts worth saving — Administration may protect more value than immediate liquidation. Rarely proportionate for micro-businesses.
  • The debts are manageable with a credible plan — direct negotiation with creditors, including Time to Pay arrangements with HMRC, can sometimes avoid formal insolvency. Only works if you act before creditors escalate.

Strike-off is not an alternative to liquidation for insolvent companies. You can only strike off a company with no outstanding debts, no recent trading, and no legal proceedings. If any of those conditions are unmet, don't file a DS01.

Company Liquidation FAQs

Can I Liquidate My Company Without an Insolvency Practitioner?

No. Both CVLs and MVLs require a licensed insolvency practitioner to act as liquidator. The IP is regulated by a recognised professional body (IPA, ICAEW, ICAS, or the Insolvency Service) and has legal obligations to creditors and the Insolvency Service. If someone offers to "liquidate" your company without being a licensed IP, walk away.

What Happens If I Keep Trading While Insolvent?

You risk a wrongful trading claim under Section 214 of the Insolvency Act 1986. The court can order you to contribute personally to the company's debts. The test isn't whether you intended to harm creditors, but whether a reasonably diligent person in your position would have concluded there was no reasonable prospect of avoiding insolvent liquidation.

Will Liquidation Automatically Disqualify Me?

No. Liquidation triggers an investigation into conduct, not an automatic ban. The IP reports to the Insolvency Service, who decide whether to pursue disqualification. If you acted responsibly, kept records, and cooperated, disqualification is unlikely. Where it does happen, periods range from 2 to 15 years depending on the severity of misconduct.

How Long Does a CVL Take?

The initial process, from board meeting to the start of liquidation, typically takes 2 to 4 weeks. The full liquidation, including asset realisation and distribution, usually takes 12 to 18 months for straightforward cases. More complex cases with disputed assets or ongoing investigations can take longer.

Can I Start Another Company After Liquidation?

Yes, unless you've been disqualified. Section 216 of the Insolvency Act restricts you from using the same or a similar company name for 5 years without court permission. If you're planning to continue a similar business, get advice on the naming restrictions before setting anything up.

What Happens to Bounce Back Loans in Liquidation?

The BBL is treated like any other unsecured creditor claim. The government guarantee means the lender gets repaid by the government, not by you personally, as long as the loan was used for the company's economic benefit. If the liquidator finds the funds were misused, you could face a misfeasance claim and disqualification.

Can I Just Strike Off the Company Instead?

Only if the company has no outstanding debts, hasn't traded recently, and isn't subject to legal proceedings. If any of those conditions are unmet, creditors can object to the strike-off and HMRC can restore the company to the register. Using dissolution to avoid dealing with debts is one of a common mistake, and it can lead to investigation.

Who Pays If the Company Has No Assets?

The directors fund the CVL, often through their personal redundancy claims. Statutory redundancy, unpaid wages, and holiday pay are paid by the government's Redundancy Payments Service, not from the company's funds. Some IPs also offer payment plans. If no funding source exists and no creditor will petition, the company may remain dormant until Companies House eventually strikes it off, but that isn't a clean outcome if debts remain.

Will My Personal Credit Score Be Affected?

Company liquidation doesn't appear on your personal credit file directly. However, personal guarantees survive liquidation. If you guaranteed bank loans, commercial leases, or credit lines, the creditor can pursue you personally, and any default will affect your credit score. Check your loan agreements and lease contracts before the company enters liquidation.

Can a Liquidator Challenge Past Transactions?

Yes. Preference payments and transactions at undervalue can be challenged if made within 2 years of liquidation starting. The look-back period is longer for connected parties than arm's-length creditors. If you've made payments to family, yourself, or related companies in the run-up to liquidation, discuss this with an IP before the process begins.

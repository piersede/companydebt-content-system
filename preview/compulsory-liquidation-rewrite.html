What compulsory liquidation means

Compulsory liquidation is the forced closure of a company by court order. It is not something a director chooses. It happens because a creditor, most commonly HMRC, has asked the court to wind the company up.

Once a winding-up order is granted, the Official Receiver (a government-appointed officer) takes control of the company. Directors lose the power to manage the business, access company bank accounts, or dispose of company assets. The company stops trading and its assets are collected, sold, and distributed to creditors.

The legal basis sits in the Insolvency Act 1986. The court can order compulsory liquidation if it is satisfied the company cannot pay its debts, under section 122(1)(f).

The distinction that matters: in a creditors' voluntary liquidation (CVL), directors initiate the process and choose the insolvency practitioner. In compulsory liquidation, the creditor forces it and the Official Receiver is appointed. That difference affects cost, speed, director scrutiny, and whether employees can claim statutory redundancy pay through the normal route.

Why this matters right now

Compulsory liquidation is not a background legal process. It triggers immediate, practical consequences that affect the director personally and the company operationally.

  • Bank account freeze. Banks routinely freeze company accounts when they become aware of a winding-up petition, often after the Gazette notice is published. This can happen before the court hearing, halting payroll, supplier payments, and all outgoing transactions overnight.
  • Gazette publication. The petition is advertised in The London Gazette at least 7 business days before the hearing. This is a public record. Customers, suppliers, landlords, and lenders can see it.
  • Loss of director control. Once the winding-up order is made, the directors have no authority to act on behalf of the company. The Official Receiver manages everything from that point.
  • Director investigation. The Official Receiver is required to investigate the conduct of directors. If wrongful or fraudulent trading is found, directors can face personal liability, disqualification for up to 15 years, or both.
  • Employee impact. Employees are automatically dismissed when a winding-up order is made. Redundancy claims go through the Redundancy Payments Service rather than the employer.

The practical issue is timing. In our experience, once the petition is advertised, the damage to the company's reputation and banking relationships has already started, regardless of the court outcome.

What happens if the petition is ignored

Doing nothing is the most damaging option. If a winding-up petition is served and no response is filed, the court will almost certainly grant the winding-up order.

  • No defence filed: the court treats the debt as undisputed. The order is made on paper or at a short hearing. There is no second chance.
  • Bank account frozen before hearing: most UK banks freeze accounts on sight of the Gazette notice. Directors often find this out when payroll fails or a supplier payment bounces.
  • Official Receiver appointed: the company is placed into compulsory liquidation immediately. Directors must cooperate with the OR's investigation, hand over all company records, and attend interviews if required.
  • Director conduct scrutiny intensifies: ignoring a petition does not look like an oversight. The OR and the Insolvency Service will look closely at whether directors continued trading while knowing the company was insolvent.

The window to respond is short. Between service of the petition and the hearing, directors have 8 to 10 weeks in most cases. But the real pressure point is the Gazette advertisement, which triggers the bank freeze. That happens much sooner.

Immediate steps to take

If you have received a winding-up petition or a statutory demand, these are the actions available to you. The right step depends on whether the debt is disputed, whether the company can pay, and how much time remains.

  1. Check the deadline. A statutory demand gives 21 days. A winding-up petition hearing is typically 8 to 10 weeks from issue. Both deadlines are hard.
  2. Decide whether the debt is genuinely disputed. If you have a legitimate reason to challenge the debt (the amount is wrong, the goods were defective, the invoice was already paid), you may be able to apply to have the petition dismissed or restrained. The court can do this if the debt is disputed on substantial grounds.
  3. Pay the debt in full. If the company can pay the petitioning creditor, paying the full amount owed (including legal costs) before the hearing will usually result in the petition being dismissed.
  4. Negotiate a settlement. Creditors sometimes agree to withdraw the petition if a payment plan or partial settlement is offered. This needs to happen quickly and in writing.
  5. Consider a voluntary route. If the company cannot pay its debts but wants to close on its own terms, a creditors' voluntary liquidation (CVL) may be available. This allows directors to choose the insolvency practitioner and typically results in less scrutiny than compulsory liquidation.
  6. Get licensed advice immediately. A licensed insolvency practitioner can assess the position, identify which options are still open, and act on the same day if needed.

Delaying past the Gazette notice point makes every option harder. The bank freeze alone can make it impossible to fund a settlement or pay for a voluntary process.

Legal grounds: who can petition and on what basis

Compulsory liquidation is governed by the Insolvency Act 1986. The key sections are:

  • Section 122(1)(f): the court may order winding up if the company is unable to pay its debts.
  • Section 123: defines what "unable to pay its debts" means. The two main tests are the cash-flow test (the company cannot pay debts as they fall due) and the balance-sheet test (liabilities exceed assets).
  • Section 124: sets out who can present a winding-up petition. This includes creditors, the company itself, directors, shareholders, the Secretary of State, and certain regulatory bodies.

In practice, HMRC is the petitioner in the majority of compulsory liquidation cases. HMRC petitions have risen sharply since the end of the COVID-era enforcement pause, and HMRC now accounts for around 60% of all winding-up petitions. The minimum debt threshold for a winding-up petition is £750, set by s.123(1)(a) of the Insolvency Act 1986. In practice, most petitions involve debts of several thousand pounds or more, but the legal threshold is low.

A creditor does not need to prove the company is balance-sheet insolvent. Failure to pay a debt of £750 or more within 21 days of a statutory demand is enough to establish a presumption of inability to pay under the Act.

The court process

The winding-up process follows a fixed legal sequence. Knowing the stages helps directors identify where they still have options.

  1. Statutory demand served. The creditor sends a formal demand for payment. The company has 21 days to pay. If the debt is not paid or disputed, the creditor can issue a petition.
  2. Winding-up petition filed. The petition is filed at court. The petitioner must serve it on the company at its registered office.
  3. Gazette advertisement. The petition must be advertised in The London Gazette at least 7 business days before the hearing. This is the trigger point for bank freezes and reputational damage.
  4. Court hearing. The court hears the petition, typically 8 to 10 weeks after issue. Possible outcomes:
    • Winding-up order made (company is placed into compulsory liquidation)
    • Petition dismissed (debt paid or legitimately disputed)
    • Petition adjourned (more time given, often for payment negotiations)
    • Petition stayed (if a voluntary arrangement is proposed)
  5. Official Receiver appointed. If the order is made, the Official Receiver becomes liquidator. A private insolvency practitioner may later be appointed if creditors request it.
  6. Investigation and asset realisation. The OR investigates director conduct, collects company assets, and distributes proceeds to creditors in the statutory order of priority.

The entire process from petition to final dissolution can take 12 months or longer. For directors, the critical period is the weeks between the petition being filed and the hearing date. That is when we see the most options still available.

What directors should avoid doing

Once a company is facing a winding-up petition, certain actions by directors will make the situation significantly worse. Some carry personal liability. Others are criminal offences.

  • Do not pay one creditor ahead of others to reduce pressure. Paying a connected creditor (a director's loan account, a family member's invoice, a related company) when the company is insolvent is a preference under s.239 of the Insolvency Act 1986. The liquidator can reverse the payment and pursue the director personally.
  • Do not move or sell company assets below value. Transactions at an undervalue (s.238 IA 1986) can be reversed. Transferring stock, vehicles, equipment, or property to connected parties while the company is insolvent will be investigated.
  • Do not continue trading if there is no reasonable prospect of paying creditors. Wrongful trading under s.214 IA 1986 can result in the director being ordered to contribute personally to the company's debts. The test is whether a reasonably diligent person would have concluded there was no reasonable prospect of avoiding insolvent liquidation.
  • Do not ignore the Official Receiver. Directors are legally required to cooperate with the OR, provide company records, attend interviews, and complete a questionnaire. Non-cooperation can lead to arrest, contempt of court, and a longer disqualification period.
  • Do not destroy company records. Destroying or concealing company books and records is a criminal offence under s.209 and s.210 IA 1986.

The theme is straightforward: actions that protect the director at the expense of creditors will be scrutinised and, where found, penalised. We advise directors to take no unilateral action with company assets once a petition is in play.

What this means for directors, employees, and bank accounts

For directors

Directors are automatically removed from their management role when the winding-up order is made. They must cooperate with the Official Receiver's investigation into the company's affairs and their own conduct as directors.

If the investigation reveals wrongful trading, fraudulent trading, or misfeasance, directors may face:

  • Personal liability for company debts (wrongful trading claims under s.214)
  • Disqualification from acting as a director for between 2 and 15 years
  • Criminal prosecution in cases of fraud or deliberate concealment

Personal guarantees (commonly given for commercial leases, bank lending, or HMRC time-to-pay arrangements) survive liquidation. If a director has personally guaranteed a debt, the creditor can pursue the director directly after the company enters liquidation. Read more about director exposure during liquidation.

For employees

Employees are automatically dismissed on the date of the winding-up order. They become preferential creditors for certain claims (up to 4 months' unpaid wages, capped at £800 per employee, plus accrued holiday pay).

Redundancy pay, notice pay, and unpaid wages beyond the preferential cap are claimed through the government's Redundancy Payments Service (part of the Insolvency Service), not from the company directly. This process typically takes 3 to 6 weeks after the claim is submitted.

For bank accounts

Company bank accounts are frozen either when the bank becomes aware of the petition (usually via the Gazette notice) or when the winding-up order is made. Any disposition of company property after the petition is presented is void unless the court orders otherwise (s.127 IA 1986).

This means payments made from the company account after the petition date, including wages, supplier payments, and rent, may need to be returned to the liquidator. Directors should seek a validation order from the court if they need to continue making essential payments before the hearing.

Alternatives that may still be available

Even after a winding-up petition has been filed, some alternatives may remain open. Which ones depend on the company's financial position, how much time is left before the hearing, and whether the petitioning creditor will agree.

  • Pay the debt. If the company or a third party can pay the full petitioning debt plus the creditor's legal costs, the petition will usually be dismissed. This is the most direct route but requires available cash.
  • Creditors' Voluntary Liquidation (CVL). If the company is insolvent and closure is inevitable, directors can initiate a CVL before the winding-up order is made. A CVL allows directors to choose the insolvency practitioner, typically involves less scrutiny than compulsory liquidation, and may preserve employee access to statutory redundancy pay through the normal route.
  • Company Voluntary Arrangement (CVA). A CVA is a formal agreement to repay creditors over time, supervised by an insolvency practitioner. It requires 75% creditor approval by value. If accepted, the petition is usually stayed. This route suits companies that are viable but temporarily unable to pay. It is not available if the company has no future income to fund the arrangement.
  • Administration. If the company has a viable business that could be rescued or sold as a going concern, administration may be appropriate. An administrator is appointed to manage the company and can block further action by creditors, including winding-up petitions. This route requires a realistic rescue plan and is usually more expensive than a CVL.

The window for these alternatives narrows as the process advances. Before the Gazette notice, most options are still available. After the bank freeze, funding any of them becomes the primary obstacle.

Not every company in this position qualifies for every route. We help directors identify which alternatives are realistic based on the company's actual financial position, not which ones sound appealing in theory.

Frequently asked questions

Can a winding-up petition be stopped?

Yes, in several ways. The debt can be paid in full (including the petitioner's costs), the petition can be dismissed if the debt is genuinely disputed on substantial grounds, or the court can adjourn or stay the petition if a voluntary arrangement or other proposal is made. The earlier the director acts, the more options remain. Once the winding-up order is made, it can only be rescinded in limited circumstances.

How quickly does the bank account freeze?

Most UK banks freeze company accounts as soon as they become aware of a winding-up petition, typically when the Gazette notice is published. This can happen 7 or more business days before the court hearing. Some banks act even earlier if they receive the petition directly. The freeze happens without warning and applies to all funds in the account.

Will I lose my house?

Compulsory liquidation of a limited company does not automatically affect personal assets, including the director's home. However, if the director has given a personal guarantee for any company debt (common with bank loans, commercial leases, and some HMRC time-to-pay arrangements), the creditor can pursue the director personally. If a personally guaranteed debt cannot be paid, the home could be at risk. This is a question for early legal advice, not something to guess about.

Is compulsory liquidation the same as bankruptcy?

No. Compulsory liquidation applies to companies. Bankruptcy applies to individuals. A director whose company is wound up is not personally bankrupt unless they separately fail to pay personal debts (including any triggered by personal guarantees). The two processes are legally distinct, though one can follow the other in some circumstances.

What happens to employees?

Employees are automatically dismissed on the date the winding-up order is made. They can claim redundancy pay, unpaid wages (up to 8 weeks, capped at the statutory weekly limit), notice pay, and accrued holiday pay through the government's Redundancy Payments Service. Claims are typically processed within 3 to 6 weeks. Employees do not need the employer's cooperation to make a claim.

Can I still be a director of another company?

Being a director of a company that goes into compulsory liquidation does not automatically trigger disqualification. However, the Official Receiver will investigate the director's conduct. If the investigation finds unfit conduct (wrongful trading, preferences, breach of duty), the Insolvency Service can apply for a disqualification order, banning the individual from acting as a director for between 2 and 15 years. Until any investigation is concluded, holding other directorships is not prevented, but taking advice on the risk is strongly recommended.

What does the Official Receiver do?

The Official Receiver is a civil servant employed by the Insolvency Service. After a winding-up order, the OR takes control of the company, investigates the conduct of directors, collects and realises company assets, adjudicates creditor claims, and distributes proceeds in the statutory order of priority. The OR may also report to the Secretary of State on whether directors should be disqualified. Directors are legally required to cooperate fully with the OR.

How much does compulsory liquidation cost?

The petitioning creditor pays the court fee (currently £2,167, which includes a £1,600 deposit to the Official Receiver and a £567 court fee). [HUMAN CONFIRMATION NEEDED: verify current compulsory liquidation court fee and OR deposit amounts] The director does not pay these costs directly, but the company's assets are used to cover the OR's costs and expenses before any distribution to creditors. If the company has no assets, the OR's costs are met from the deposit.

Can I convert a compulsory liquidation to a CVL?

Once a winding-up order has been made, it is not possible to convert to a CVL. The CVL route is only available before the order is granted. This is one of the main reasons directors are advised to act quickly after receiving a petition: if voluntary liquidation is a better outcome, it must be initiated before the court hearing, not after.

What happens to company debts after compulsory liquidation?

Company debts are dealt with through the liquidation process. The liquidator distributes available assets to creditors in the statutory priority order: secured creditors, preferential creditors (employees for certain claims), unsecured creditors with a prescribed part allocation, then remaining unsecured creditors. Any debts not covered by available assets are written off when the company is dissolved. Personal guarantees, however, are not extinguished by company liquidation and remain enforceable against the director personally.

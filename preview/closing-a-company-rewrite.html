Closing a Limited Company: The Routes, the Costs, and How to Choose

Closing a limited company is not one process. It is a choice between several routes, and the right one depends almost entirely on whether the company can pay what it owes.

Directors who pick the wrong route risk personal liability, unnecessary cost, or having the process reversed months later. Directors who pick the right one close the company cleanly and move on.

This page covers the four main closure routes, explains how solvency determines which ones are available to you, and walks through the costs, timescales, and trade-offs of each. If you are not sure where your company sits, start with the insolvency test.

The Main Routes for Closing a Limited Company

There are four routes used to close the vast majority of UK limited companies. Two are for solvent companies. Two are for insolvent ones.

  • Strike-off (voluntary dissolution) — the cheapest and simplest route, but only available if the company has no debts it cannot pay, no assets worth distributing, and has stopped trading.
  • Members' Voluntary Liquidation (MVL) — a formal liquidation for solvent companies with assets to distribute, typically used to extract funds tax-efficiently.
  • Creditors' Voluntary Liquidation (CVL) — the standard route for insolvent companies where the directors choose to close voluntarily rather than wait for creditors to act.
  • Compulsory liquidation — forced closure by a creditor (usually HMRC) through a court petition. The directors do not control this process.

The first decision you need to make is whether your company is solvent or insolvent. Everything follows from that.

Solvent or Insolvent: The First Decision

Before choosing a route, you need to answer one question: can the company pay all of its debts?

UK insolvency law uses two tests:

  • Cash-flow test — Can the company pay its debts as they fall due? If invoices, HMRC liabilities, or loan repayments are overdue and there is no realistic way to pay them in the normal course of business, the company fails this test.
  • Balance-sheet test — Are the company's total liabilities greater than its total assets? Include everything: HMRC debts, Bounce Back Loans, trade creditors, director loan accounts, and any contingent liabilities such as personal guarantees.

Failing either test means the company is insolvent. That rules out strike-off (in most cases) and MVL, and points you toward a CVL or, if creditors act first, compulsory liquidation.

If you are unsure, the Company Debt insolvency test takes five minutes and gives you a clear answer. Getting this wrong is the most common and most expensive mistake directors make when closing a company.

Strike-Off: When It Works and When It Does Not

Strike-off is the simplest way to close a company. You file a DS01 form with Companies House, the company name is published in the Gazette, and if nobody objects within two months, the company is dissolved.

Eligibility

  • The company must not have traded or sold stock in the last three months.
  • It must not have changed its name in the last three months.
  • There must be no outstanding debts the company cannot pay.
  • It must not be subject to a CVA, administrative order, or other formal insolvency procedure.
  • All employees must have been formally terminated.

Process summary

  • File DS01 with Companies House (fee: £10 online).
  • Notify all creditors, employees, shareholders, and pension trustees.
  • Submit final accounts and a Company Tax Return to HMRC.
  • Wait for the Gazette notice and two-month objection window.
  • If no objections, the company is dissolved.

Cost

The filing fee is £10. Accountancy fees for final accounts and tax returns vary but typically run between £300 and £800. No insolvency practitioner is needed.

The risk directors underestimate

If a creditor objects during the Gazette notice period, the strike-off is suspended. HMRC objects frequently when there are unpaid Corporation Tax, VAT, or PAYE liabilities. A Bounce Back Loan lender can also object. If the company is struck off while debts remain, creditors can apply to restore it to the register for up to six years, and the directors face potential personal liability for the debts. This is why strike-off is not a shortcut for companies that owe money. Read more in our full guide to strike-off and dissolution.

MVL: For Solvent Companies with Assets to Distribute

A Members' Voluntary Liquidation is a formal liquidation process for companies that can pay all their debts in full within 12 months. It is most commonly used when a solvent company has significant retained profits or assets and the directors want to extract those funds as a capital distribution rather than as dividends.

Why directors choose MVL over strike-off

Capital distributions through an MVL qualify for Business Asset Disposal Relief (formerly Entrepreneurs' Relief), meaning the first £1 million is taxed at 10% rather than at income tax rates. For a company sitting on £50,000 or more in retained profits, the tax saving typically outweighs the liquidation costs.

Process summary

  • Directors sign a Declaration of Solvency confirming all debts can be paid within 12 months.
  • Shareholders pass a special resolution to wind up the company.
  • A licensed insolvency practitioner is appointed as liquidator.
  • The liquidator settles all debts, distributes remaining assets to shareholders, and files final returns.
  • The company is dissolved.

Cost

IP fees for an MVL typically range from £2,000 to £5,000 plus VAT, depending on the complexity of the company's affairs. This is on top of accountancy fees for final accounts.

The critical condition

The Declaration of Solvency is a legal statement. If the company turns out to be insolvent, the directors who signed it face personal liability and potential disqualification proceedings. Do not sign a Declaration of Solvency unless you are certain the company can pay every debt in full. If there is any doubt, a CVL is the safer route. See our detailed MVL guide for the full process.

CVL: For Companies That Cannot Pay Their Debts

A Creditors' Voluntary Liquidation is the standard route for closing an insolvent company when the directors decide to act rather than wait for creditors to force the issue. It is the most common formal insolvency procedure for small and medium-sized companies in England and Wales.

Why a CVL rather than just walking away

Walking away from an insolvent company does not make the debts disappear. Creditors can still petition to wind the company up compulsorily, HMRC can pursue the directors for unpaid PAYE and VAT, and the Insolvency Service can investigate director conduct going back years. A CVL puts the closure into a controlled, legal framework and demonstrates that the directors acted responsibly.

Process summary

  • Directors appoint a licensed insolvency practitioner.
  • The IP prepares a Statement of Affairs.
  • Shareholders pass a resolution to wind up.
  • A decision procedure is held for creditors.
  • The IP realises company assets and distributes proceeds to creditors in statutory order.
  • The company is dissolved.

Director redundancy

Directors who are employees of the company (on payroll, with an employment contract) may be eligible to claim redundancy pay from the Redundancy Payments Service. This can be worth up to £21,000 and is often used to offset the cost of the liquidation itself. Not every director qualifies. The eligibility criteria are specific, and getting them wrong means a rejected claim. If you think this applies to you, ask the IP to assess eligibility before committing to a route.

Cost

CVL costs typically start from around £4,000 to £6,000 plus VAT. The total depends on the size and complexity of the company's affairs, the number of creditors, and whether assets need to be realised. Some IPs offer fixed-fee packages for straightforward cases.

Compulsory Liquidation: When a Creditor Forces the Issue

Compulsory liquidation is not a route you choose. It happens when a creditor (most often HMRC) petitions the court to wind up the company because a debt has not been paid.

Once a winding-up petition is presented, the company's bank accounts are typically frozen. The court appoints the Official Receiver to take control of the company's affairs. The directors lose all authority over the business, its assets, and its closure process.

The cost to the company is significantly higher than a voluntary route, and the directors face mandatory conduct investigation by the Official Receiver.

If you have received a winding-up petition or a statutory demand, acting before the court hearing is critical. A CVL started before the petition is heard gives the directors more control, typically results in a better outcome for creditors, and avoids the Official Receiver. How to Choose the Right Route

Start with solvency. Everything else follows.

If the company can pay all its debts

  • No significant assets to distribute? Strike-off is the cheapest and fastest option. File the DS01, notify creditors, and wait for dissolution.
  • Retained profits above £25,000? An MVL almost certainly saves more in tax than it costs in IP fees. The capital distribution route via Business Asset Disposal Relief is the main reason directors choose MVL over strike-off.
  • Retained profits under £25,000? You may be able to take a capital distribution via strike-off without an MVL, but take advice on the tax position first. The £25,000 threshold is not a hard legal rule for every situation.

If the company cannot pay all its debts

  • No creditor has petitioned yet? A CVL gives you control. You choose the insolvency practitioner, you set the timeline, and the process demonstrates responsible director conduct. If you qualify for director redundancy, the payout can offset most or all of the cost.
  • A creditor has already petitioned? You may still be able to start a CVL before the hearing date, but you need to act immediately. Call an insolvency practitioner today, not next week. See our compulsory liquidation guide for the timeline and defence options.
  • The company might be rescuable? If the business is viable but carrying legacy debt, a CVA (Company Voluntary Arrangement) or administration may be worth exploring before committing to closure. These are rescue routes, not closure routes, and they require a fundamentally different conversation with an IP.

If you are not sure about solvency

Do not guess. An insolvent company dissolved via strike-off can be restored to the register. A Declaration of Solvency signed for an MVL when the company is actually insolvent creates personal liability. Take the insolvency test or speak to a licensed insolvency practitioner before committing to any route.

Common Mistakes When Closing a Company

  • Using strike-off to avoid paying creditors. Creditors (especially HMRC) object to strike-off applications when debts are outstanding. Even if the company is dissolved, it can be restored to the register for up to six years. Strike-off is for companies with no debts, not for companies trying to escape them.
  • Signing a Declaration of Solvency without proper due diligence. This is a legal statement. If the company later turns out to be insolvent, the directors who signed it face personal liability. Check every liability, including contingent ones, before signing.
  • Waiting too long to start a CVL. The longer an insolvent company continues trading, the greater the risk of wrongful trading claims against the directors. Once you know the company cannot pay its debts, delaying the decision does not improve the outcome. It worsens it.
  • Not claiming director redundancy. Many directors do not realise they may qualify for redundancy pay if they are on the company payroll. This is not automatic and must be claimed through the correct process, but it can be worth up to £21,000 and often covers the cost of the CVL.
  • Choosing the cheapest route rather than the correct one. Strike-off costs £10. A CVL costs thousands. But using strike-off when the company is insolvent can result in personal liability, restoration to the register, and higher costs later. The correct route is the one that matches the company's financial position, not the one with the lowest upfront fee.
  • Ignoring HMRC correspondence. HMRC is the most common petitioning creditor for compulsory liquidation. Statutory demands and winding-up petitions have strict response deadlines. Missing them removes options that were available days earlier.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I close a company with debts?

Yes, but not through strike-off. If the company has debts it cannot pay, the appropriate route is usually a Creditors' Voluntary Liquidation (CVL). This is a formal insolvency process where a licensed insolvency practitioner takes control of the closure, realises any assets, and distributes proceeds to creditors. The company is dissolved at the end of the process, and the debts are written off against the company, not the directors personally (unless personal guarantees are involved). See our CVL guide for the full process.

How much does it cost to close a limited company?

It depends entirely on the route. Strike-off costs £10 in filing fees plus accountant costs for final returns. An MVL typically costs £2,000 to £5,000 plus VAT in insolvency practitioner fees. A CVL typically costs £4,000 to £6,000 plus VAT. Compulsory liquidation costs the company £10,000 or more and is not controlled by the directors. If you are eligible for director redundancy through a CVL, the payout (up to £21,000) can offset most or all of the cost.

How long does it take to close a company?

Strike-off takes three to six months from filing to dissolution. An MVL takes six to twelve months, sometimes longer if assets are complex. A CVL typically completes the main process within three to six months, though the formal dissolution can take longer. Compulsory liquidation is the slowest route, often taking one to three years because the Official Receiver works across a large caseload.

What is the difference between strike-off and liquidation?

Strike-off is an administrative process through Companies House. It is cheap and simple but only works for solvent companies with no significant assets. Liquidation (whether MVL, CVL, or compulsory) is a formal legal process overseen by a licensed insolvency practitioner. It handles debts, distributes assets, and provides legal closure. The key difference: strike-off does not deal with debts or creditors. If the company owes money, strike-off is not the right route. See our liquidation overview for a full comparison.

What happens to company debts when a company is dissolved?

Debts owed by the company are written off against the company when it is dissolved through a formal liquidation process (MVL, CVL, or compulsory). However, any debts with personal guarantees survive the company's closure and remain the director's personal responsibility. HMRC can also pursue directors personally for unpaid PAYE and National Insurance in some circumstances. If the company is dissolved via strike-off while debts remain unpaid, creditors can apply to restore it to the Companies House register.

Am I personally liable for company debts?

In most cases, a director's liability is limited to the amount unpaid on their shares. But there are important exceptions. Personal guarantees (common with bank loans, leases, and Bounce Back Loans) make the director personally liable for that specific debt regardless of the company's status. Directors can also face personal liability for wrongful trading if they allowed the company to continue trading while insolvent, or for misfeasance if company assets were misused. A properly conducted CVL reduces, but does not eliminate, the risk of personal claims.

Can I claim redundancy if I am a director?

Potentially, yes. If you are on the company payroll with an employment contract, pay yourself through PAYE, and work regular hours for the company, you may qualify for redundancy pay from the Redundancy Payments Service when the company enters liquidation. The maximum statutory redundancy payout is currently around £21,000, depending on age and length of service. Not every director qualifies. The criteria relate to your employment status, not your directorship. Ask your insolvency practitioner to assess your eligibility before choosing a closure route.

Can HMRC object to my company being struck off?

Yes. HMRC is the most common objector to strike-off applications. If the company has outstanding Corporation Tax, VAT, or PAYE liabilities, HMRC will typically object during the two-month Gazette notice period. This suspends the strike-off process. The company remains on the register, the debts remain, and the directors are back where they started, except that HMRC now knows the company was trying to close without paying. If you suspect HMRC is owed money, do not file for strike-off until you have confirmed the position.

What happens if I do nothing and just leave the company dormant?

The company remains on the Companies House register, and you remain a director with ongoing legal obligations. You must still file annual accounts, a confirmation statement, and Company Tax Returns. Failure to file results in penalties and, eventually, Companies House may strike the company off the register. But creditors can still pursue the company and its directors during that time. HMRC debts accrue interest and penalties. Leaving an insolvent company dormant does not make the debts go away. It typically makes the situation worse and more expensive to resolve later.

Should I speak to an insolvency practitioner before choosing a route?

If there is any possibility the company is insolvent, yes. An initial consultation with a licensed insolvency practitioner is typically free, confidential, and without obligation. The IP can assess the company's financial position, confirm which routes are available, and explain the implications of each. For solvent companies with no debts, your accountant can usually handle the strike-off or refer you for an MVL. For anything involving unpaid debts, creditor pressure, or uncertainty about solvency, professional insolvency advice is not optional. It is how you avoid the mistakes listed above.

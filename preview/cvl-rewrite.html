Closing an Insolvent Company Through a CVL

If the company cannot pay its debts and continuing to trade is no longer viable, a Creditors' Voluntary Liquidation (CVL) is the standard route for an orderly shutdown. Directors initiate the process, a licensed insolvency practitioner takes control of asset realisation, and the company is dissolved once creditors have been dealt with.

This page covers the full CVL process: who qualifies, what it costs, how long it takes, and what directors face during and after the procedure. It is written for directors who have accepted that closure is necessary and need to understand the mechanics before instructing a practitioner.

Delay matters. Trading while insolvent exposes directors to personal liability under the Insolvency Act 1986, and HMRC escalation (including winding-up petitions) can remove the option of a voluntary process entirely. The sooner a CVL is started, the more control directors retain over the outcome.

When a CVL Is Used

A CVL applies when the company is insolvent and the directors choose to wind it up voluntarily rather than waiting for a creditor to force the issue.

Insolvency has two statutory tests under s.123 of the Insolvency Act 1986:

  • Cash-flow test: the company cannot pay its debts as they fall due. This includes tax liabilities, supplier invoices, loan repayments, and rent.
  • Balance-sheet test: the company's total liabilities exceed its total assets, including contingent and prospective liabilities.

Failing either test is enough. Most CVLs are triggered by the cash-flow test, often after months of mounting HMRC arrears, failed payment plans, or creditor pressure that makes continued trading unsustainable. For a detailed breakdown of both tests, see the insolvency test guide.

Common triggers include HMRC Time to Pay refusals, bounce-back loan defaults, loss of a major customer, or a statutory demand that cannot be paid within 21 days. If a winding-up petition has already been filed, a CVL may still be possible but the window is narrow. Directors facing an active petition should seek immediate advice.

Who Can Use a CVL

Any UK limited company that is insolvent can enter a CVL, provided the directors and shareholders follow the statutory procedure.

The key threshold is a shareholder vote. Under s.84(1)(b) of the Insolvency Act 1986, the shareholders must pass a special resolution to wind up the company voluntarily. That requires at least 75% of voting shareholders to approve.

In practice, most companies entering a CVL are owner-managed, so the director and majority shareholder are the same person. The vote is a formality. Where there are multiple shareholders who disagree, the 75% threshold becomes a real constraint, and directors may need to negotiate or consider alternative routes.

There is no minimum debt level, no minimum number of creditors, and no requirement that the company has traded for a specific period. Dormant companies with outstanding liabilities can also enter a CVL, though strike-off may be more appropriate if debts are negligible and no creditor is likely to object.

How the CVL Process Works Step by Step

The CVL follows a fixed statutory sequence. Directors cannot skip steps or change the order.

1. Board decision and insolvency practitioner appointment

The directors hold a board meeting and resolve that the company cannot continue trading due to its liabilities. At this stage, an insolvency practitioner (IP) is engaged. The IP must be licensed by a recognised professional body (IPA, ICAEW, ICAS, or the Insolvency Service). The IP will assess the company's position, prepare the necessary documents, and advise on timing.

2. Shareholder resolution

The shareholders pass a special resolution (75% majority) to wind up the company voluntarily. Under the Insolvency Rules 2016, this can be done by written resolution rather than a physical meeting, which speeds up the process for owner-managed companies.

3. Creditor notification and decision procedure

Within 14 days of the shareholder resolution, creditors must be given notice of the liquidation and invited to participate in a decision procedure. Since the Insolvency Rules 2016 replaced the old creditor meetings, this is typically handled by correspondence (a "deemed consent" procedure). Creditors can request a physical meeting, but this is uncommon in small-company CVLs.

Creditors have the right to appoint a different liquidator if they choose, though in the majority of cases the IP proposed by the directors is confirmed.

4. Statement of affairs

The directors must prepare a statement of affairs: a verified document listing all assets, liabilities, and creditor details. This is sworn by the directors and filed with the liquidator. Inaccuracies or omissions in the statement of affairs can trigger investigation, so accuracy matters.

5. Liquidator takes office

Once appointed, the liquidator takes full control. Directors lose their powers. The liquidator's role is to realise assets, investigate the company's affairs, adjudicate creditor claims, distribute funds, and report on director conduct.

What Documents and Decisions Are Involved

Directors need to prepare or provide the following during the CVL process:

  • Board minutes recording the decision that the company is insolvent and should enter liquidation
  • Special resolution of shareholders (75% majority) to wind up the company
  • Statement of affairs listing all assets, liabilities, creditors (with addresses and amounts owed), and any security held by creditors
  • Director questionnaires covering the company's trading history, reasons for insolvency, and any transactions that may require investigation
  • Company books and records including management accounts, bank statements, VAT returns, and HMRC correspondence

The IP handles statutory filings with Companies House and the Insolvency Service, including the notice of appointment and the Forms 4.25 and 600 series under the Insolvency Rules 2016.

Directors should expect to spend several hours compiling information. Incomplete records slow the process and increase costs, because the liquidator must reconstruct the company's financial position from bank statements and third-party records.

How Long a CVL Takes

From first contact with an IP to the liquidator taking office, the typical timeline is 10 to 21 days. That covers the board meeting, shareholder resolution, creditor notification period, and formal appointment.

The full liquidation, from appointment to dissolution, depends on asset complexity. A simple case with no significant assets, no property, and straightforward creditor claims can be finalised in 6 to 12 months. Cases involving property sales, ongoing litigation, book debt collection, or HMRC disputes can take 12 to 24 months or longer.

The statutory minimum before dissolution is approximately 3 months after the final account is sent to creditors and filed with Companies House. The company is then dissolved by the Registrar.

What a CVL Costs

CVL costs typically fall between 4,000 and 6,000 pounds plus VAT for a straightforward case with limited assets. More complex cases, especially those involving property, contested claims, or director conduct issues, cost more because the liquidator's time increases.

The fee covers the IP's statutory duties: creditor notifications, asset realisation, investigations, reporting, and final dissolution. Disbursements (bonding, advertising, Companies House fees) are separate and typically add 500 to 1,000 pounds.

Redundancy funding: how directors can offset the cost

Directors who are also employees of the company (with a PAYE employment contract and a minimum of two years' continuous service) may be eligible to claim statutory redundancy pay from the Redundancy Payments Service (now part of the Insolvency Service). The claim is made after the liquidator is appointed.

Statutory redundancy pay is calculated using age, length of service, and weekly pay (capped at the statutory weekly limit). For directors with long service, the payment can be several thousand pounds, which in many cases covers or exceeds the cost of the CVL itself.

This is a legitimate entitlement under the Employment Rights Act 1996, not a loophole. The liquidator must confirm the director's employment status, and HMRC will check that the employment was genuine. Directors paid solely through dividends with no employment contract do not qualify.

What Happens Once the Process Starts

Once the liquidator is appointed, the company stops trading (if it has not already) and the liquidator takes control of all assets.

Asset realisation

The liquidator sells assets (equipment, vehicles, stock, intellectual property, debtor balances) and collects any money owed to the company. Property with secured charges is dealt with according to the security holder's rights. Unsecured assets are sold and the proceeds distributed to creditors in the statutory order: fixed charge holders, preferential creditors (employees and certain HMRC claims), floating charge holders, then unsecured creditors.

Creditor claims and distributions

Creditors submit proof of debt. The liquidator adjudicates each claim and, if there are sufficient funds, makes distributions according to the statutory hierarchy. In many small-company CVLs, unsecured creditors receive little or nothing because preferential claims and liquidation costs absorb the available funds.

Director conduct report

The liquidator is required to file a report on director conduct with the Insolvency Service (under s.7A of the Company Directors Disqualification Act 1986). This report covers whether directors traded responsibly, kept adequate records, filed statutory returns, and acted in creditors' interests once insolvency was known or suspected.

The conduct report is not optional. Every CVL generates one. Directors who cooperated fully, ceased trading at an appropriate time, and maintained proper records have little to fear from this step. For more detail on what investigators look for, see the director during liquidation guide.

What Happens at the End of a CVL

Once all assets have been realised, distributions made, and investigations concluded, the liquidator files a final account with Companies House and sends notice to all creditors.

Three months after that filing, the company is automatically dissolved. It ceases to exist as a legal entity. The company name becomes available for re-use (subject to restrictions under the Insolvency Act 1986 s.216, which prevents directors from re-using a company name or a similar name for five years without court permission).

After dissolution, directors are free to start a new company, provided they have not been disqualified. Any personal guarantees given by directors survive the liquidation and remain enforceable by the creditor who holds them. A CVL does not discharge personal guarantees.

Director Risks and Investigations

A CVL triggers mandatory scrutiny of director conduct. Understanding the specific risks helps directors prepare and reduces unnecessary anxiety.

Wrongful trading

Under s.214 of the Insolvency Act 1986, directors can be held personally liable if they continued trading after the point at which they knew (or should have known) that the company could not avoid insolvent liquidation. The test is objective: what would a reasonably diligent person in the director's position have concluded?

The practical defence is to show that once insolvency became apparent, the director took steps to minimise losses to creditors. Seeking professional advice early and acting on it is the clearest evidence of that.

Disqualification

The Company Directors Disqualification Act 1986 gives the Insolvency Service the power to seek a disqualification order (banning a director from holding office for 2 to 15 years) based on the liquidator's conduct report. Common grounds include allowing HMRC debts to accumulate while paying other creditors, failing to maintain adequate accounting records, and continuing to trade at creditors' expense.

Insolvency Service data shows that the majority of CVLs do not result in disqualification proceedings, but the risk is real for directors who traded irresponsibly or failed to engage with the process.

Director liability agreements (DLAs)

In some cases, the liquidator may seek a contribution from directors through a formal claim or negotiated settlement, particularly where preferences (paying one creditor ahead of others in the run-up to liquidation) or transactions at undervalue have occurred. These claims are funded from the liquidation estate or through litigation funding.

Alternatives to a CVL

A CVL is not the only option for an insolvent company. The right route depends on whether rescue is realistic, whether creditors are already forcing the issue, and the company's financial position.

  • Compulsory liquidation: If a creditor (usually HMRC) files a winding-up petition and the company does not respond, the court can order compulsory winding up. Directors lose all control, the Official Receiver is appointed, and the process is more intrusive and less predictable than a CVL. See the compulsory liquidation guide.
  • Company Voluntary Arrangement (CVA): If the business is viable but burdened by debt, a CVA allows the company to agree a repayment plan with creditors and continue trading. This requires 75% creditor approval and a realistic repayment proposal. It is a rescue tool, not a closure tool. See the CVA guide.
  • Administration: Provides a statutory moratorium (protection from creditor action) while a rescue plan is developed. Suitable for larger businesses with ongoing revenue and realisable assets. More expensive and complex than a CVL. See the insolvency overview for how administration fits into the wider framework.
  • Strike-off (dissolution): Costs 10 pounds and is available for companies with no significant debts, no active creditor claims, and no assets to distribute. Not appropriate for insolvent companies because creditors can object and HMRC can restore the company to the register. See the strike-off guide for eligibility and risks.

If the company is solvent and directors want a tax-efficient extraction of remaining funds, a Members' Voluntary Liquidation (MVL) is the relevant procedure, not a CVL.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can directors choose the insolvency practitioner in a CVL?

Directors propose the IP, but creditors have the right to appoint a different one during the decision procedure. In practice, creditors rarely exercise this right in small-company CVLs, so the directors' choice usually stands.

Do directors need to attend a creditors' meeting?

Physical meetings are no longer mandatory. The Insolvency Rules 2016 replaced them with a "decision procedure" that can be conducted by correspondence (deemed consent). Creditors can request a meeting, but this is uncommon. Directors may need to answer questions from the liquidator or creditors in writing.

What happens to employees in a CVL?

Employees are made redundant when the liquidator is appointed (or when trading ceases, if earlier). They can claim statutory redundancy pay, unpaid wages (up to 8 weeks), holiday pay, and notice pay from the Redundancy Payments Service. These are preferential claims under the Insolvency Act 1986.

Can HMRC block a CVL?

HMRC cannot block a CVL. The decision to enter a CVL is made by the shareholders, not creditors. However, if HMRC has already filed a winding-up petition, the court process may overtake the CVL unless the petition is withdrawn or the CVL is commenced before the hearing date.

Does a CVL affect the director's credit rating?

A CVL does not directly appear on a director's personal credit file. However, any personal guarantees that are called in, or personal insolvency that follows (bankruptcy or an IVA), will affect the director's credit rating. Director disqualification is a public record held by the Insolvency Service.

What if the company has no assets at all?

A CVL can proceed even if the company has no realisable assets. The IP's fees must still be paid, typically upfront by the directors. This is one reason redundancy funding is relevant: it can provide the funds to cover liquidation costs.

Can a CVL be reversed once started?

Once the liquidator is appointed, the process is extremely difficult to reverse. The court can stay or terminate a winding up under s.112 of the Insolvency Act 1986, but this requires an application supported by evidence that the company is in fact solvent. In practice, reversal almost never happens.

What happens to company bank accounts?

The liquidator takes control of all company bank accounts on appointment. Any remaining balances become part of the liquidation estate. Directors cannot withdraw funds after the liquidator's appointment. Banks typically freeze accounts as soon as they are notified of the liquidation.

Are bounce-back loans treated differently in a CVL?

Bounce-back loans are unsecured debts and rank alongside other unsecured creditors. The government guarantee protects the lender, not the borrower. Directors are not personally liable for the loan unless they gave a personal guarantee or the loan was misused (which can trigger separate investigation). The liquidator will review how the loan funds were applied.

How soon after a CVL can a director start a new company?

Immediately, unless the director has been disqualified. The restriction under s.216 of the Insolvency Act 1986 applies to the company name (or a similar name), not to directorship itself. A disqualified director cannot act as a director of any company for the duration of the disqualification (2 to 15 years).

What to Do Now

The right next step depends on how immediate the pressure is.

  • If the company is still trading but clearly insolvent: speak to a licensed insolvency practitioner this week. Early engagement protects against wrongful trading risk and preserves the option of a controlled CVL rather than forced compulsory liquidation.
  • If a winding-up petition has been filed or a statutory demand received: act within days, not weeks. A CVL can still be started, but only if the petition hearing has not yet taken place. Delay past this point removes the voluntary option entirely.
  • If trading has already stopped and debts remain: the company still needs a formal closure route. Leaving it dormant with unpaid creditors does not remove the liabilities or the risk of compulsory action. A CVL resolves the position definitively.

### Q6: HMRC enforcement change since COVID forbearance ended?

HMRC's enforcement stance has significantly hardened since the end of COVID-related forbearance measures (such as the 'Time to Pay' arrangements and temporary restrictions on WUPs that expired in early 2022).

*   **Increased Activity:** HMRC has actively resumed and increased its use of formal enforcement tools, including winding-up petitions and statutory demands, to recover tax arrears. This shift became evident from late 2022 and has continued through 2024 and 2025.
*   **Focus Areas:** There is a strong focus on recovering outstanding VAT, PAYE, and Corporation Tax debts, which accrued during the pandemic.
*   **Proactive Approach:** HMRC is now more proactive in pursuing businesses that are not engaging or adhering to payment plans, often moving to enforcement actions more quickly than during the forbearance period.
*   **Economic Context:** This increased enforcement is partly driven by the need to recover national debt and partly by the broader economic challenges, which have left more businesses unable to meet their tax obligations.

### Q8: Most common grounds for disqualification?

The most common grounds for director disqualification consistently include:

1.  **Failure to pay tax / HMRC arrears:** Deliberate non-payment or mismanagement of company finances leading to significant tax liabilities is a very frequent reason.
2.  **Trading to the detriment of creditors (wrongful trading):** Continuing to trade when a company is demonstrably insolvent, thereby worsening the position of creditors.
3.  **Lack of commercial probity / Misconduct:** This is a broad category covering various forms of dishonest or unethical behaviour, including:
    *   Misuse of company funds/assets.
    *   Failure to keep adequate accounting records.
    *   Making preferential payments to certain creditors.
    *   Abuse of government support schemes (e.g., Bounce Back Loans, CBILS).
4.  **Failure to cooperate with insolvency practitioners:** Obstructing or failing to provide information to liquidators or administrators.

### Q11: Typical CVL timeline first meeting to dissolution?

A typical CVL timeline from the first meeting of creditors/members (or deemed consent) to dissolution is approximately **12-24 months**, though it can be shorter or significantly longer depending on complexity.

1.  **Decision & IP Appointment:** 1-2 weeks (from initial contact to formal appointment)
2.  **Creditors' Voluntary Liquidation Commencement:** Day of the deemed consent or virtual meeting (the day the liquidation officially begins).
3.  **Initial Administration (Asset Realisation, Claims Processing):** 3-12 months (highly variable)
4.  **Final Report to Creditors/Members:** Once all assets are realised, claims adjudicated, and distributions made.
5.  **Liquidator Seeks Release:** After the final report, the liquidator applies for release (usually deemed release after 3 months).
6.  **Company Dissolution:** The company is automatically dissolved **3 months** after the liquidator files the final return with the Registrar of Companies and the Insolvency Service.

### Q12: Director redundancy in CVL? Eligibility? Max statutory redundancy 2025-2026?

Yes, directors can be eligible for redundancy pay in a CVL, provided they meet certain criteria:

*   **Eligibility:**
    1.  **Employee Status:** The director must have a written or implied contract of employment, not merely be a director. They must have been genuinely performing a role of an employee (e.g., sales director, finance director) in addition to their directorial duties.
    2.  **Minimum Service:** At least 2 years' continuous service.
    3.  **Remuneration:** Must have been paid a salary (via PAYE) for their employee role.
    4.  **Working Hours:** Must have worked at least 16 hours per week.
    5.  **Loss of Employment:** Their employment must have been terminated due to the company's insolvency.

*   **Maximum Statutory Redundancy (2025-2026):**
    *   For dismissals on or after 6 April 2025, the maximum amount of a week's pay used to calculate redundancy is **£643**.
    *   This figure is multiplied by the director's age and length of service (up to 20 years).

### Q14: What happens to bank accounts when WUP in Gazette? How fast do banks freeze?

When a winding-up petition is advertised in the London Gazette, it signals the impending formal liquidation of a company.

*   **Bank Account Freezing:** Banks are very sensitive to winding-up petitions. While a WUP does not *automatically* freeze accounts the moment it is Gazetted, most banks have sophisticated systems to monitor the Gazette and will typically **freeze company bank accounts very quickly** once they become aware of a petition.
*   **Speed:** This can happen within **hours or a day or two** of the Gazette publication, and sometimes even before if the bank is otherwise made aware of the petition (e.g., direct notification from a creditor or the court).
*   **Why Freeze?** Once a WUP is presented, any disposition of the company's property (including bank payments) made after the date of the petition can be voided by the court if a winding-up order is subsequently made (s.127 Insolvency Act 1986). Banks freeze accounts to protect themselves from this risk and to prevent assets from being dissipated before a liquidator is appointed.
*   **Consequence:** Freezing of accounts can severely cripple a business, preventing it from making or receiving payments, paying staff, or continuing to trade.

### Q15: Compulsory timeline vs CVL?

**Compulsory Liquidation Timeline (longer and less predictable):**

1.  **Statutory Demand (Optional):** 21 days for response.
2.  **Petition Presentation:** Creditor files WUP with court.
3.  **Petition Hearing:** Typically 6-10 weeks after presentation.
4.  **Gazette Advertisement:** After the petition is presented, it's advertised, usually around 7 days before the hearing.
5.  **Winding-Up Order:** If the court grants the order.
6.  **Official Receiver (OR) Appointment:** The OR becomes liquidator initially.
7.  **Investigation & Administration:** OR/Appointed IP takes over, investigates, secures assets, deals with creditors. This phase can take **12 months to several years**, depending on complexity and investigations.
8.  **Dissolution:** After administration is complete, the company is dissolved.

**Creditors' Voluntary Liquidation (CVL) Timeline (quicker and more controlled by directors):**

1.  **Director Decision & IP Instruction:** 1-2 weeks.
2.  **Preparation of Documentation:** 1-2 weeks.
3.  **Deemed Consent/Virtual Meeting:** Liquidation commences.
4.  **Liquidator Takes Control:** Immediately after commencement.
5.  **Asset Realisation & Creditor Claims:** This phase is typically **6-18 months**, often quicker than compulsory if assets are few and claims straightforward.
6.  **Final Report & Liquidator Release:** Once complete.
7.  **Dissolution:** 3 months after the final return is filed.

**Summary Comparison:**

*   **Control:** CVL is director-led and controlled; Compulsory is court-driven and often hostile.
*   **Speed:** CVL is significantly faster to commence and often to conclude.
*   **Cost:** CVL often incurs lower professional fees (as it's streamlined); Compulsory has fixed court and OR deposit costs, plus IP fees, and can be prolonged by court processes.
*   **Publicity:** Both lead to Gazette notices, but a WUP for compulsory liquidation can have a more damaging immediate impact on reputation and banking.

### Q17: IS policy on investigating dissolved company directors? Director Disqualification (Dissolved Companies) Act impact?

*   **IS Policy:** The Insolvency Service (IS) actively investigates directors of dissolved companies. Its policy is to scrutinise the conduct of directors whose companies have been dissolved, particularly where there is evidence of misconduct, abuse of government support schemes, or wrongful trading.
*   **Director Disqualification (Dissolved Companies) Act 2021 Impact:** This Act, which came into force on **15 February 2022**, significantly enhanced the IS's powers.
    *   **Prior to the Act:** The IS generally needed a company to be in liquidation or administration to investigate directors' conduct. Once a company was dissolved, investigations were extremely difficult or impossible without a court order to restore the company.
    *   **Impact:** The Act now allows the IS to conduct investigations into directors of dissolved companies and, if appropriate, seek a director disqualification order, without the need to restore the company to the register. This has closed a loophole that allowed some directors to escape scrutiny by dissolving their companies.
    *   **Focus:** The powers are particularly used to target directors who dissolved companies to avoid paying creditors (including HMRC) or who abused pandemic-related loan schemes (e.g., Bounce Back Loans) and then dissolved their companies.

**Source URL:**
*   [GOV.UK, The Rating (Coronavirus) and Directors Disqualification (Dissolved Companies) Act 2021](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/new-powers-to-tackle-directors-who-dissolve-companies-to-avoid-paying-their-debts) (Announcement of the Act coming into force.)
*   [GOV.UK, Investigation of dissolved companies](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/investigation-of-dissolved-companies) (IS guidance on the use of these powers.)

### Q18: FCA/IS warnings about unregulated insolvency advice firms 2024-2025?

Yes, both the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) and the Insolvency Service (IS) have issued warnings about unregulated insolvency advice firms in 2024-2025.

*   **Nature of Warnings:** These warnings highlight that many firms offering "debt advice," "liquidation advice," or "business rescue" services are unregulated and often charge exorbitant upfront fees for services that either don't materialise, are poorly executed, or lead to worse outcomes for businesses and directors.
*   **FCA's Role:** While the FCA doesn't directly regulate insolvency practitioners (which are regulated by professional bodies), it warns consumers and businesses about firms offering *financial advice* that might steer them towards unsuitable or fraudulent insolvency solutions. The FCA maintains a register of authorised firms and alerts about unauthorised firms.
*   **IS's Role:** The IS (and Recognised Professional Bodies like ICAEW, R3) regularly advise businesses to only engage with **licensed insolvency practitioners (IPs)**. They publish guidance on how to check if an IP is licensed and warn against firms that promise unrealistic outcomes or demand large upfront fees for "assessments" or "introductions."
*   **Common Tactics:** These unregulated firms often target vulnerable directors, offering to "write off" debt, hide assets, or guarantee certain outcomes, which is misleading and potentially fraudulent.

### Q19: Statutory interest rate for MVL declaration of solvency?

In an MVL, a Declaration of Solvency asserts the company can pay its debts in full within 12 months. The relevant statutory interest rate applies to debts that are outstanding and need to be paid in the liquidation.

*   The statutory interest rate for unsecured debts in insolvency, if provided for by statute, is **8% per annum**. This rate is specified in Section 189 of the Insolvency Act 1986 and Rule 14.23 of the Insolvency (England and Wales) Rules 2016.
*   This rate applies from the date the company went into liquidation until the debt is paid. It ensures that creditors whose claims are delayed receive some compensation, provided there are sufficient funds after payment of the principal amount and higher priority debts.

### Q20: Average time statutory demand to WUP filed?

There isn't an official "average time" statistic published by the Insolvency Service for the period between a statutory demand and a WUP filing, as it's a pre-insolvency process managed by creditors. However, based on common practice and legal requirements:

*   **Statutory Demand Expiry:** A statutory demand gives the debtor **21 days** to either pay the debt, reach a settlement, or apply to the court to have the demand set aside.
*   **WUP Filing:** If the debt remains unpaid and no application to set aside is made, the creditor can file a winding-up petition (WUP) **immediately after the 21-day period expires**.
*   **Typical Delay:** In practice, creditors (especially HMRC) might wait a **few days to a few weeks** after the 21-day period before actually filing the petition, allowing for administrative processing or a final attempt at engagement.
*   **Therefore:** The period from the service of a statutory demand to the filing of a WUP is typically **between 3 weeks (21 days) and 6-8 weeks**.

# Comparison Governance

Hard rules for competitor comparisons, Company Debt mentions, frameworks, and pricing transparency.

## 1. Comparison table risk rules

### Every negative claim is high-risk

Any competitor claim in a table using language such as:
- "No"
- "Not native"
- "Partial"
- "Limited"
- "Requires workaround"
- "Email only"
- "US servers"
- "Portal-dependent"

must be treated as high-risk. A single incorrect negative claim in a comparison table can destroy credibility with any reader who knows the competitor product.

### Verification requirements

Each negative claim must be one of:
1. Recently verified against current product documentation (date noted)
2. Checked in-product (date noted)
3. Marked as documentation-based and needing confirmation: "[Verify: based on documentation review, March 2026]"

### Mandatory table metadata

Every comparison table must include:
- **Verification date:** "Comparison data last verified: [month year]"
- **Basis of comparison:** "Based on: public documentation review / product testing / both"
- **Caution note:** "Competitor features change frequently. Verify current capabilities before making decisions."
- **Scope note:** State what the table covers and what it does not. Do not imply exhaustive coverage.

### Prose-level negative claims

Negative capability claims about competitors in running prose (not just tables) require the same verification treatment as table entries: verified from current documentation with date, or flagged with `[VERIFY]`. Do not infer feature absence from product origin or market (e.g., "US-built" does not prove "no MTD audit trail").

### What the table must not do

- Must not omit a competitor's genuine strength to favour Company Debt
- Must not present estimated or inferred data as confirmed
- Must not present a comparison as exhaustive without stating scope limits
- Must not state competitor limitations as fact without verifiable evidence
- Must not infer feature absence from product origin, market focus, or company headquarters

## 2. Anti-self-serving framework rule

If an article includes any of the following:
- buyer framework
- category framework
- shortlist framework
- comparison framework
- evaluation checklist
- decision matrix

then it must include at least one meaningful dimension where Company Debt is not the strongest fit.

### Dimensions where Company Debt is typically weaker

- Practice management breadth
- Billing and invoicing
- E-signatures
- Internal workflow depth
- Team coordination and job management
- Mature ecosystem or install base
- Breadth of feature set
- Portal functionality for digitally confident clients
- CRM and pipeline management
- Time tracking

### What "meaningful" means

The balancing dimension must be one that matters to a real buyer. It cannot be trivial, obscure, or irrelevant to the decision context.

**Bad balance:** "Company Debt does not have a mobile app for Windows Phone." (Irrelevant.)

**Good balance:** "For practices that need billing, e-signatures, and internal workflow management in one platform, TaxDome or Karbon provides broader coverage. Company Debt handles one layer: outbound client chasing."

### Detection test

Would a competitor's product manager read the framework and consider it fair? If not, it needs revision.

### Failure this prevents

Self-serving frameworks. When the author's product wins on every dimension, the framework is not an evaluation. It is a sales page. Readers detect this. It damages long-term credibility.

## 3. Company Debt mention rules

### No abrupt product insertion

Company Debt must not appear as a sudden pivot in an otherwise neutral article. The reader should never feel that an editorial piece was a Trojan horse for a product pitch.

### How Company Debt should be introduced

Where Company Debt appears in a competitor review, category article, or comparison piece, it should usually be preceded by one of:

1. **Category distinction:** "This is a practice management tool. Document collection is a different category."
2. **Problem-layer distinction:** "The coordination problem and the chasing problem are different. Karbon solves the first. Tools like Company Debt solve the second."
3. **Buyer-fit distinction:** "If your primary frustration is internal workflow confusion, a work management platform is the right tool. If your primary frustration is clients not sending documents, a chasing tool is what you need."
4. **Workflow-stage distinction:** "Billing sits downstream of document collection. These are different stages of the engagement workflow."

### Balancing requirement

If Company Debt is mentioned as stronger in one layer or dimension, the article must also state:
- Where Company Debt is not a replacement for the competitor
- Where the competitor remains stronger
- What Company Debt does not do

**Bad:** "Company Debt handles multi-channel chasing across email, SMS, WhatsApp, and voice. Unlike TaxDome, it goes and gets the documents."

This implies Company Debt is simply better. It does not acknowledge what it lacks.

**Good:** "Company Debt handles one layer: outbound client chasing across email, SMS, WhatsApp, and voice. It does not provide practice management, billing, e-signatures, or internal workflow tools. For practices that need both, using Company Debt alongside a practice management platform is a common pattern."

### Company Debt disclosure

Where Company Debt is compared against competitors, include a disclosure: "Company Debt is our product. This review is written from that perspective. We have tried to be accurate and fair about both strengths and limitations."

## 4. Pricing transparency rules

### Do not criticise pricing without numbers

If a competitor's pricing is criticised as expensive, steep, or a significant commitment, the actual figure, range, or pricing model must be stated if it can be verified.

**Bad:** "Karbon's per-seat pricing represents a significant annual commitment for small practices."

**Good:** "Karbon's per-seat pricing is not publicly listed. Request a quote for current rates. At two to three seats, based on comparable per-seat tools in the category, the annual cost is likely to exceed what a micro-practice would spend on a flat-rate chasing subscription."

### If pricing cannot be verified

Say so clearly: "Current pricing was not publicly available at the time of writing."

Do not use vague substitutes like "steep", "premium", or "significant commitment" as the only price signal.

A "could not verify" note does not license subsequent vague criticism. If pricing cannot be verified, state that fact and stop. Do not follow with "significant commitment", "steep", or equivalent language in the same passage.

### Company Debt pricing transparency

Where Company Debt is framed as lower cost or flatter cost, state:
- The basis of the comparison (flat-rate vs per-seat, for example)
- The limits of the comparison (at what headcount does the comparison flip?)
- What is included and excluded

### When pricing is central

If cost is central to the article's argument, the reader must be given enough information to make their own judgement. Vague framing is not enough.

## 5. Diagnostic-buyer framing for commercial software articles

Commercial software articles must be written as diagnostic buying tools, not descriptive software summaries.

Rules:
- Default framing is problem-first, not vendor-first
- Every article must clarify what problem the tool solves and what problem it does not solve
- Every relevant article must distinguish adjacent categories where confusion is likely
- Feature breadth must not be treated as equivalent to fit. A tool that does many things does not necessarily solve the reader's specific problem
- Where useful, state that a tool improves visibility over a problem rather than removes the workload itself (e.g., "Karbon gives visibility into job status. It does not chase clients for documents.")

### Category-clarity enforcement

When adjacent tools are commonly conflated, the article must explicitly distinguish the relevant categories. Failure to resolve category confusion is a material decision-quality failure.

Categories that commonly require explicit separation:
- workflow management vs client chasing
- portal-based collection vs outbound chasing
- OCR / receipt processing vs document acquisition
- all-in-one practice management vs specialist tools
- internal visibility tools vs external client-engagement tools

Mechanically flag any article that discusses tools spanning these categories without explicitly stating which category each tool belongs to and what problem each category solves.

## 6. Universal Company Debt mention rule

The Company Debt mention rules in §3 apply in **every article type**, not only comparison or category pages.

If Company Debt is mentioned in a workflow article, problem article, opinion post, or explainer, it must still:
1. Be introduced through a relevant problem-layer, workflow-stage, or buyer-fit distinction
2. Include a balancing line stating where Company Debt is not a replacement or not the strongest fit
3. Not appear as a sudden product insertion after neutral editorial analysis

The only exception: if Company Debt is the explicit subject of the article (e.g., a Company Debt product update or Company Debt-specific guide), the introduction rule does not apply, but the balancing requirement still does.

4. Not serve as the article's conclusion unless the article is explicitly about Company Debt. An article that ends on Company Debt product features reads as a sales funnel, not editorial content.
5. Include the Company Debt disclosure ("Company Debt is our product") wherever Company Debt is mentioned, in any article type.

**Detection test:** Read the article as a reader who does not work at Company Debt. Does the Company Debt mention feel earned by the preceding analysis? Or does it feel inserted? If inserted, it fails regardless of the article type.

## 7. Comparison governance checklist

Before finalising any comparison or competitor content:

- [ ] Page mode declared: independent-style or vendor-perspective (§8)
- [ ] Trust-first rule satisfied: analysis useful even without conversion (§9)
- [ ] Category frame tested for neutrality (§10)
- [ ] House product includes best-fit, not-a-fit, and required conditions (§11)
- [ ] Disclosure architecture complete: top-of-page, product-specific, footer (§12)
- [ ] Opener uses conditional verdict, not winner-first (§12a)
- [ ] No bracket-stacked proof after verdict language (§12b)
- [ ] Winner language appears at most twice (intro + conclusion) (§12b)
- [ ] Product sections vary visually, not forced into identical scaffold (§12b)
- [ ] Generic intensifiers cashed out into consequence (§12b)
- [ ] Every negative capability claim is verified with date, or flagged for confirmation
- [ ] Comparison table includes verification date, basis, caution note, and scope note
- [ ] No competitor strength omitted to favour Company Debt
- [ ] Any framework includes at least one dimension where Company Debt is weaker
- [ ] Company Debt introduced through category/problem/buyer-fit distinction, not abruptly
- [ ] Balancing statement included where Company Debt is mentioned as stronger
- [ ] Pricing criticism backed by actual figures or "could not verify" note
- [ ] Company Debt disclosure included where Company Debt is compared
- [ ] A competitor's product manager would consider the comparison fair
- [ ] If Company Debt is mentioned in a non-comparison article, the universal mention rule (§6) is satisfied

## 8. Commercial comparison page mode

### Mode declaration

Every comparison, alternatives, and decision-stage page must declare one mode before drafting:

- **Independent-style comparison:** The publisher has no product in the category. The analysis is impartial by default. Disclosure is minimal.
- **Vendor-perspective comparison:** The publisher sells a product in the category. The analysis is commercially interested. Trust architecture must be explicit.

If the publisher sells a product in the category, default to **vendor-perspective comparison** unless a higher-layer instruction explicitly allows neutral treatment.

### Rules

- The system must not mix independent-review posture with vendor-led persuasion
- If the house product appears, the page must explicitly preserve real reader choice
- Mode must be visible to the review layer — not buried in metadata

### Fail conditions

- **FAIL** if the article sounds neutral on the surface but structurally funnels toward the house product
- **FAIL** if the page implies independence it has not earned

## 9. Trust-first rule for vendor-authored content

### Core principle

Trustworthiness is the gating factor for vendor-authored comparison content. Strong structure, expertise, and depth do not compensate for weak trust design.

### Rules

- Commercial intent must be explicit early
- Analysis must remain useful even if the reader never converts
- The house product must be one valid path, not the inevitable endpoint

### Fail conditions

- **FAIL** if the article helps mainly by steering the reader toward the house product
- **FAIL** if the page preserves only one commercially convenient path

## 10. Category-framing neutrality

### Rules

- Before drafting, test whether the article's conceptual frame would still be considered fair by a reasonable independent reviewer
- If a conceptual distinction advantages the house product, the system must:
  - justify the distinction
  - show where it does not apply
  - state at least one scenario where another tool is better
- Category framing is editorial judgement unless independently verified

### Fail conditions

- **FAIL** if the article defines the problem only in the terms most favourable to the publisher
- **FAIL** if a binary is introduced that conveniently makes the house product the only logical winner

---

## 11. House-product containment

### Rules

The house product must always include:
- **Best fit:** who should use it and under what conditions
- **Not a fit:** who should not use it and why
- **Conditions required for adoption to make sense:** what must be true about the practice for this tool to deliver value

The article must remain coherent and useful if the house-product section is removed. Product mention must follow problem definition and decision framing, not create them. Product-specific disclosure appears at first house-product mention.

### Fail conditions

- **FAIL** if the article collapses conceptually without the house product
- **FAIL** if the house product appears without explicit limiting conditions

---

## 12. Disclosure architecture

### Three-tier disclosure model

1. **Top-of-page disclosure:** Declares perspective early without foregrounding an unknown brand unnecessarily
2. **Product-specific disclosure:** Appears at first house-product mention in the analysis
3. **Footer disclosure:** Reinforcement, not the primary trust signal

### Approved top-of-page disclosure pattern

> This article was written by the Company Debt team and reflects our assessment of the options covered, based on [evidence basis].

### Disallowed top-of-page disclosure pattern

Editorial lines that force the reader to process the house product before it matters.

### Fail conditions

- **FAIL** if trust context is incomplete before substantive comparison begins
- **FAIL** if disclosure is buried below the analysis
- **FAIL** if disclosure foregrounds the house product too early for a cold reader

## 12a. Comparison page opener discipline

### Winner-first opener ban

A comparison page must not begin with:
- "The best X is Y"
- "Y is the only tool here that..."
- any opener that combines ranking, exclusivity, compliance reassurance, and brand positioning in one move

### Required opener sequence

1. Identify the real buyer problem or category split
2. Explain why that split matters in practice
3. State scope and method
4. Give a conditional verdict (narrowed by use case)
5. Disclosure if relevant

### Conditional verdict rule

A comparison page may use strong verdict language only when narrowed by use case.

**Correct shape:** "For firms whose main bottleneck is X, Y is the strongest fit in this shortlist."

**Incorrect shape:** "The best X is Y."

### Fail conditions

- **FAIL** if the page opens with a winner-first sentence
- **FAIL** if the verdict is universal rather than conditional

---

## 12b. Comparison page prose discipline

### Bracket-stacked proof ban

Do not allow verdict language followed by stacked product proof in brackets.

**Bad:** "For firms whose main bottleneck is X, Company Debt is the strongest fit in this shortlist (from £99/month, UK data residency, multi-channel chasing across email, SMS and WhatsApp)."

**Better:** "For firms whose main bottleneck is X, Company Debt is the strongest fit in this shortlist." Then bring the evidence later in prose.

Intro verdict language must be narrow and restrained. Evidence follows in subsequent paragraphs.

### Repeated verdict phrasing limit

Phrases like "strongest fit", "best overall", "best choice", "strongest option" must not be repeated throughout the page.

Rule:
- One main verdict in the intro
- One final verdict in the conclusion
- Avoid restating winner language inside each product section

**FAIL** if winner language appears more than twice in total (once in intro verdict, once in conclusion).

### Template visibility rule

Comparison pages must not read like the same machine pattern repeated for each product.

Structural aids (Best for / Not for / Key facts) are acceptable. But:
- Visible variation across product sections is required
- Section logic should fit the product rather than forcing symmetry
- Lead with what is distinctive about each product, not the same scaffold

**Preferred shapes by product type:**
- Chase-focused tool: lead with the real-world pain of repeated chasing, then workflow consequences, then limits
- Client-experience tool: lead with why a smart firm would be drawn to the experience, then explain friction
- All-in-one platform: lead with tool-sprawl relief and consolidation appeal, then explain what it does not solve
- Adjacent-category tool: lead with the category misunderstanding, then explain why it remains valuable anyway

### Generic intensifier ban (comparison-specific)

Reduce or remove in comparison pages:
- genuinely cleaner
- genuinely reduces
- real value
- solid
- cleaner than most competitors
- practical rather than theoretical

unless immediately cashed out into concrete consequence. (Extends `09-voice-governance.md` §5.)

### Fail conditions

- **FAIL** if all product sections visibly expose the same machine-like structure
- **FAIL** if winner language is repeated more than twice
- **FAIL** if bracket-stacked proof follows verdict language in the intro
- **FAIL** if generic intensifiers appear without concrete consequence

## 13. Alternatives page governance

*(Previously §8. Renumbered after governance layer additions.)*

### Purpose

Alternatives pages serve a reader who has a specific incumbent tool and is trying to decide whether to replace it, augment it, or change how they use it. They are not catalogue pages. They must earn each product mention through decision logic, not list vendors as if summarising a category.

### Required framing before any product is named

Before naming any alternative, the article must establish:

1. **Why readers are looking for an alternative** — the specific switching trigger, not a generic description of the category
2. **What job they are actually trying to solve** — the underlying problem, separated from the surface-level dissatisfaction with the incumbent
3. **Whether they need replacement, augmentation, or process change** — these are different answers and require different recommendations

### Classification requirement

Every "alternative" discussed must be classified into exactly one of:

- **Direct replacement** — replaces the incumbent's core function entirely
- **Adjacent specialist add-on** — fills a gap the incumbent category does not address; does not replace it
- **Remain on incumbent and change process** — the underlying problem is solvable without switching

The classification must be stated explicitly before the product is described.

### Augmentation rule

If augmentation (adding a specialist layer alongside the incumbent) is a legitimate solution path for the reader, the article **must** present this path before discussing vendor options. Omitting augmentation when it is a valid answer is a framing failure that harms readers making real purchasing decisions.

### House product placement

Company Debt must not appear in an alternatives article until the replacement-vs-augmentation distinction has been made explicit. Company Debt is an adjacent specialist add-on in most alternatives contexts, not a direct replacement for practice management software. This distinction must be stated clearly before Company Debt is introduced.

### Category integrity

If the article discusses a workflow problem that the incumbent category does not fundamentally solve (e.g., outbound client chasing in a practice management tool), the article must say so directly. Do not allow adjacent-category products to appear as if they are direct replacements unless they actually are.

### Fail conditions

- **FAIL** if article structure follows the pattern: incumbent intro → vendor summaries → Company Debt CTA
- **FAIL** if augmentation is a valid buyer path but is not presented before vendor options
- **FAIL** if the replacement-vs-augmentation distinction is present but appears below the first screen of content
- **FAIL** if any product is mentioned before the decision frame (why switching, what job, which path) is established
- **FAIL** if the house product appears without explicit "not the right fit if…" conditions

### Alternatives page governance checklist

- [ ] Switching trigger stated before any product is named
- [ ] Underlying job separated from surface dissatisfaction
- [ ] Replacement / augmentation / process-change paths all considered
- [ ] Each alternative classified into one of the three paths
- [ ] Augmentation presented as a path where it is genuinely valid
- [ ] Company Debt introduced only after category distinction is explicit
- [ ] Company Debt includes at least one "not the right fit if…" condition
- [ ] Article does not end with Company Debt as the natural conclusion

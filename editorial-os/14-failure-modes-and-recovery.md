# Failure Modes and Recovery

A reference library of recurring content failures, how to detect them, and how to fix or remove them.

Every failure mode here has been observed in Company Debt content. These are not hypothetical risks. They are patterns that recur unless actively prevented.

---

## 1. Synthetic first person

**What it looks like:**
"I think Karbon is genuinely good at what it is designed to do."
"From what I have seen, portal adoption rates often settle below vendor projections."
"I would not call this a dealbreaker, but it is worth noting."

**Why it is a problem:**
First person is being used to simulate authorship rather than express genuine editorial judgement. It sounds like AI performing the role of a human writer. Real human editors do not preface every opinion with "I think."

**How to detect it:**
Search for "I think", "I believe", "in my view", "I would say", "from what I have seen", "I find", "I should be clear." If the sentence works without the first-person prefix, the prefix is filler.

**How to fix it:**
Remove the first-person scaffolding. State the judgement directly.

Before: "I think the per-seat pricing is steep for micro-practices."
After: "The per-seat pricing is steep for micro-practices with two or three staff."

**When to remove instead of rewrite:**
If the sentence without the first-person prefix says nothing useful, delete the entire sentence.

---

## 2. Founder drift

**What it looks like:**
"When we built Company Debt, we wanted to solve the chasing problem."
"The problem we set out to solve was..."
"I did not build Company Debt as a practice management tool."

**Why it is a problem:**
The writer is not the founder. Claiming founder authority is false and damages credibility if discovered.

**How to detect it:**
Search for "we built", "I run", "we started", "we created", "our founding", "set out to", "I did not build." Any phrasing that implies personal ownership of the product or company.

**How to fix it:**
Rewrite from the editorial team perspective.

Before: "We built Company Debt to solve the chasing problem."
After: "Company Debt is a document collection tool focused on outbound client chasing."

**When to remove instead of rewrite:**
If the founder-drift sentence is there purely to establish authority, remove it. The content should establish authority through the quality of the analysis, not through claimed identity.

---

## 3. Padded evaluation

**What it looks like:**
"Karbon is genuinely good at what it is designed to do."
"The email triage is useful, particularly for practices receiving high volumes of client communication."
"TaxDome's pipeline view is well-executed."

**Why it is a problem:**
These sentences sound like evaluation but deliver no information. The reader learns nothing specific. Padded evaluation is a substitute for actual analysis.

**How to detect it:**
Ask: "What exactly is good, useful, or well-executed, and for whom?" If the sentence does not answer both questions, it is padded.

**How to fix it:**
Replace with specific claims about what the feature does, who benefits, and what the limits are.

Before: "The email triage is useful."
After: "The email triage attaches incoming client emails to the relevant job, saving time spent sorting and forwarding. It does not chase clients who have not replied."

**When to remove instead of rewrite:**
If you cannot specify what is good about it, the sentence does not belong in the article.

---

## 4. Hedged unsupported claim

**What it looks like:**
"Portal adoption rates tend to settle below vendor projections."
"From what I have seen, multi-channel chasing often produces better results."
"Clients typically cannot distinguish automated messages from manual ones."

**Why it is a problem:**
The hedge makes the claim feel safer but does not make it true. If the claim carries the argument, the hedge is not enough. The reader has no way to verify, challenge, or build on the claim.

**How to detect it:**
Identify the load-bearing claims in the article. For each, ask: "Is this supported by a named source, labelled as judgement, or flagged for confirmation?" If the only support is a hedge phrase, it fails.

**How to fix it:**
Apply one of the four evidence treatments: verify, label as judgement, flag for human confirmation, or remove.

**When to remove instead of rewrite:**
If the claim cannot be sourced, labelled, or flagged in a way that adds value, delete it. An article without the claim is stronger than an article with an unsupported one.

## 5. Abrupt Company Debt pivot

**What it looks like:**
An article spends 800 words neutrally reviewing a competitor or category, then suddenly says: "Company Debt handles this differently. We provide multi-channel chasing across email, SMS, WhatsApp, and voice."

**Why it is a problem:**
The reader feels ambushed. The neutral analysis was a setup for a product pitch. This damages the credibility of everything that came before it.

**How to detect it:**
Read the article as a reader who does not work at Company Debt. Mark where Company Debt first appears. Does it feel earned by the preceding analysis, or does it feel inserted?

**How to fix it:**
Introduce Company Debt through a category distinction, problem-layer distinction, or buyer-fit distinction before the mention.

Before: "[800 words about TaxDome] ... Company Debt handles this differently."
After: "[Analysis of TaxDome's chasing layer] ... The coordination problem and the chasing problem are different problems. TaxDome solves the first. Tools focused specifically on outbound document collection, including Company Debt, solve the second."

**When to remove instead of rewrite:**
If the Company Debt mention does not add decision value to the article, remove it entirely. Not every article needs to mention Company Debt.

## 6. Self-serving framework

**What it looks like:**
A comparison framework where the evaluation criteria are chosen so that Company Debt wins on every dimension. The criteria emphasise multi-channel chasing, UK compliance, and flat-rate pricing while omitting practice management breadth, billing, e-signatures, and ecosystem maturity.

**Why it is a problem:**
Readers notice when a framework is rigged. It damages trust in the entire evaluation, not just the Company Debt section.

**How to detect it:**
Check whether Company Debt wins on every dimension. If it does, the framework is self-serving.

**How to fix it:**
Add at least one meaningful dimension where Company Debt is not the strongest fit. Common options: practice management depth, billing, e-signatures, internal workflow, mature ecosystem.

**When to remove instead of rewrite:**
If the framework cannot be made credible with balancing dimensions, remove the framework and replace it with direct editorial judgement about fit.

---

## 7. Pricing criticism without numbers

**What it looks like:**
"Karbon's per-seat pricing represents a significant annual commitment for small practices."
"The cost feels steep relative to the value a team that size typically derives."

**Why it is a problem:**
The reader cannot judge whether the pricing is actually steep without knowing what it is. Vague pricing criticism is opinion dressed as analysis.

**How to detect it:**
Search for "steep", "significant commitment", "premium pricing", "expensive" in relation to competitors. Is an actual price, range, or model stated? If not, it fails.

**How to fix it:**
Add the actual pricing, state the pricing model, or explicitly note that pricing could not be verified.

Before: "Karbon's per-seat pricing represents a significant annual commitment."
After: "Karbon's per-seat pricing is not publicly listed. Request a current quote. At two to three seats, based on comparable per-seat tools, expect the annual cost to exceed a flat-rate chasing subscription."

---

## 8. Decorative sourcing

**What it looks like:**
"Industry research from Gartner suggests that SMS open rates exceed 90%."
"Studies have shown that multi-channel communication produces better results."

**Why it is a problem:**
The source is mentioned to create the appearance of evidence without actually verifying the specific claim. The Gartner research may not say what is being claimed, or may not apply to the context.

**How to detect it:**
For each cited source, ask: "Does this source actually support this specific claim in this specific context?" If you cannot confirm, it is decorative.

**How to fix it:**
Either verify and fully attribute the source, or remove the citation and reframe the claim as editorial judgement.

---

## 9. Generic SaaS filler

**What it looks like:**
"Seamless integration", "end-to-end solution", "robust platform", "scalable solution", "cutting-edge technology", "next-generation", "game-changing."

**Why it is a problem:**
These phrases carry zero information. They are marketing noise, not editorial content.

**How to detect it:**
Search for common SaaS cliches. If the phrase could appear in any software company's marketing copy, it is filler.

**How to fix it:**
Replace with specific claims about what the product does, for whom, and with what limits.

**When to remove instead of rewrite:**
If removing the filler phrase leaves a sentence that says nothing, delete the sentence.

---

## 10. Over-polished AI prose

**What it looks like:**
Competent, balanced, smooth writing that reads correctly but has no editorial edge. The prose is polished but bloodless. It sounds like a language model producing "good writing" rather than a human editor making judgements.

**Why it is a problem:**
Readers increasingly recognise AI prose. Polished-but-generic writing signals "this was generated, not written." It undermines authorship credibility.

**How to detect it:**
Read the paragraph aloud. Does it sound like something a real person would say in a conversation about this topic? Or does it sound like a careful summary of all the obvious things?

**How to fix it:**
Add specificity. Add a sharper judgement. Remove the filler. Shorten. Let the writing have edges.

---

## 11. Long web-inappropriate paragraphs

**What it looks like:**
Paragraphs of 4+ lines on screen, containing multiple ideas, requiring sustained attention to parse.

**Why it is a problem:**
Web readers scan. Long paragraphs are skipped. The information in them is effectively invisible.

**How to detect it:**
Any paragraph over 3 rendered lines (approximately 250-300 characters).

**How to fix it:**
Split at the natural sentence boundary where the topic shifts.

---

## 12. Comparison-table factual risk

**What it looks like:**
A table claiming a competitor does not have a feature, when that feature may have been added since the last check.

**Why it is a problem:**
A single incorrect negative claim destroys the table's credibility with any reader who knows the product.

**How to detect it:**
Check every "No", "Partial", "Not native", "Limited" claim. Is it dated? Is it sourced?

**How to fix it:**
Verify from current documentation, add dates, or flag for confirmation.

---

## 13. False neutrality

**What it looks like:**
An article that presents all options as roughly equal when the evidence clearly favours one over another for the stated use case.

**Why it is a problem:**
It wastes the reader's time. They came for a judgement, not a catalogue.

**How to detect it:**
Ask: "Does this article help the reader choose, or just describe options?"

**How to fix it:**
Take the position. Say who should choose what and why.

---

## 14. Exaggerated certainty

**What it looks like:**
Presenting uncertain or partially supported claims as settled facts. Stating product capabilities without verification caveats. Making compliance claims without checking.

**Why it is a problem:**
It exposes Company Debt to factual risk and damages reader trust.

**How to detect it:**
For each strong factual claim, ask: "Is this definitely true right now?" If you are not sure, add a caveat or flag for confirmation.

**How to fix it:**
Downgrade certainty. Add verification dates. Flag for confirmation.

---

## 15. Unsupported compliance claim

**What it looks like:**
"TaxDome stores data on US servers, which raises UK GDPR concerns."
"Company Debt is fully UK GDPR compliant."

**Why it is a problem:**
Compliance claims are factual claims with legal implications. Getting them wrong is worse than getting a feature comparison wrong.

**How to detect it:**
Identify every sentence that mentions GDPR, data residency, consent, or regulatory compliance. Is the claim sourced? Is it current?

**How to fix it:**
Verify from primary regulatory or product sources, or flag for human confirmation. Do not make compliance assertions from assumptions.

## 16. AI prose fingerprints

AI-generated text produces recurring syntactic and structural patterns that human editors rarely use. These are machine-searchable. Finding 3 or more distinct fingerprints in an article is a hard fail for voice credibility.

### Fingerprint catalogue

**Pattern 1: "X is not just Y, it is Z"**
Search for: "is not just", "not just a", "more than just"
Why it feels synthetic: This is an AI rhetorical escalation pattern. Human editors state what something is, not what it transcends.
Instead: State the specific capability directly.

**Pattern 2: "Whether you are X or Y"**
Search for: "Whether you are", "Whether you're", "Whether your firm"
Why it feels synthetic: Fake inclusiveness. Tries to address every reader simultaneously rather than targeting the actual audience.
Instead: Name the specific reader the section addresses.

**Pattern 3: "It is worth noting that"**
Search for: "worth noting", "important to note", "worth mentioning"
Why it feels synthetic: Filler transition that adds no information. A human editor would cut it and start with the thing worth noting.
Instead: Delete the prefix. State the point directly.

**Pattern 4: "When it comes to"**
Search for: "When it comes to", "when it comes down to"
Why it feels synthetic: Vague topic introduction. Delays the specific claim.
Instead: Name the thing directly. "Karbon's pricing" not "When it comes to Karbon's pricing."

**Pattern 5: "The key is" / pseudo-insight framing**
Search for: "The key is", "The key here is", "The real key", "what makes the difference", "the important distinction", "the most important", "the crucial point", "the real difference"
Why it feels synthetic: Pseudo-insight framing. Positions a generic observation as a revelation.
Instead: State the recommendation or trade-off without the "key" frame.

**Pattern 6: "In today's" / "In the modern"**
Search for: "In today's", "In the modern", "In an era", "In a world where"
Why it feels synthetic: Generic temporal framing used as a filler intro. Adds no information.
Instead: Start with the specific problem or situation.

**Pattern 7: Concessive openings with "While"**
Search for: sentences starting with "While" followed by a concessive clause
Why it feels synthetic: AI uses "While X, Y" as a default balanced-sounding sentence structure. Overuse creates a passive, hedged tone.
Instead: State the stronger claim first. Address the concession separately if needed.

**Pattern 8: Polished three-part rhythm**
Search for: three parallel clauses or items connected by commas and "and" in a single sentence, used more than twice in an article
Why it feels synthetic: AI defaults to tricolon ("clear, actionable, and trustworthy") as a prose rhythm. Once is fine. Three or more times in an article is a pattern.
Instead: Vary sentence structures. Not everything needs three parts.

**Pattern 9: Abstract verdicts without commercial specificity**
Search for: evaluative conclusions that do not name a specific user type, practice size, or operating context
Why it feels synthetic: "This is a strong tool for accounting firms" is an abstract verdict. It evaluates without specifying.
Instead: "This is strongest for five-person-plus practices that have outgrown spreadsheet-based job tracking."

**Pattern 10: "That said" / "To be fair" / "It is worth considering"**
Search for: "That said", "To be fair", "Having said that", "It is worth considering"
Why it feels synthetic: Filler transitions that simulate balance without adding substance. AI uses them to pivot between sections.
Instead: Delete the transition. Start the next point directly.

**Pattern 11: "Helps you" / "Allows you to" / "Enables you to"**
Search for: "helps you", "allows you to", "enables you to", "makes it easy to"
Why it feels synthetic: Feature-benefit language from product marketing, not editorial writing. Positions the product as agent rather than describing what it does.
Instead: Describe the capability directly. "The email triage routes incoming messages to the relevant job" not "The email triage helps you manage incoming messages more efficiently."

**Pattern 12: "Comprehensive" / "Robust" / "Seamless" without qualification**
Search for: "comprehensive", "robust", "seamless", "streamlined", "holistic"
Why it feels synthetic: Unqualified superlatives that carry no information. See also padded evaluation rules in `09-voice-governance.md` §5.
Instead: State what specifically is included, strong, or well-integrated.

**Pattern 13: "Strong" + abstract noun**
Search for: "strong reputation", "strong option", "strong choice", "strong platform", "strong capabilities", "strong fit", "strong feature set"
Why it feels synthetic: AI defaults to "strong" as a generic positive modifier. It creates approval without precision and reads like review-site filler. "Strong reputation" is the most common offender — "reputation" is already evaluative, so "strong" adds force, not meaning.
Instead: Name the underlying claim. "Well known for its all-in-one feature breadth" not "has a strong reputation." "Best suited to firms with genuine internal workflow complexity" not "a strong option for small firms."

### Detection method

Run a text search for all 13 fingerprint patterns. Count distinct matches. If 3 or more distinct patterns appear in a single article, the article fails voice credibility and must be rewritten to eliminate the patterns.

Individual fingerprint instances should be rewritten regardless, but the hard-fail threshold is 3 or more distinct patterns.

"Strong reputation" specifically should be treated as a red-flag phrase and rewritten on every occurrence.

## 17. Structurally generic opening

**What it looks like:**
"TaxDome has built a strong reputation in the US and has been growing its UK user base steadily. It is a capable platform and, for certain types of accounting practice, a genuinely good choice."

**Why it is a problem:**
The opening leads with reputation and generic positive framing. The reader learns nothing about fit, misfit, or the decision tension the article resolves. This is a vendor profile, not a diagnostic buying tool.

**How to detect it:**
Check whether paragraphs 1-2 name the buyer's problem, the common misdiagnosis, or the decision lens. If they describe reputation, installed base, or generic capability instead, the opening is structurally generic.

**How to fix it:**
Lead with the decision tension. State who the tool fits, who it does not fit, and what problem triggers the buying decision.

Before: "TaxDome has built a strong reputation in the US and has been growing its UK user base."
After: "TaxDome replaces more standalone tools than any other platform in this category. For practices whose primary problem is internal workflow coordination, it delivers. For practices whose primary problem is getting UK clients to send documents, the chasing layer is where it falls short."

---

## 18. Vendor-first framing

**What it looks like:**
An article that spends 3+ paragraphs describing what a product does well before identifying the switching trigger, decision tension, or misfit.

**Why it is a problem:**
The reader came to make a decision. Feature description without a decision frame is a product page, not editorial content.

**How to detect it:**
Count paragraphs before the first fit/misfit statement or decision criterion. If more than 2 paragraphs pass without one, the framing is vendor-first.

**How to fix it:**
Move the fit/misfit statement to paragraph 1 or 2. Use the vendor's strengths as supporting evidence for the fit claim, not as the article's lead.

---

## 19. "I think" tic writing

**What it looks like:**
Multiple sentences in an article opening with "I think": "I think TaxDome has built a strong reputation." ... "I think the feature set is comprehensive." ... "I think the pricing is competitive."

**Why it is a problem:**
Repeated "I think" is not authored judgement. It is a verbal tic that weakens every claim it introduces. It reads as AI simulating a human voice rather than a human expressing genuine editorial position.

**How to detect it:**
Count sentences beginning with "I think." More than one in any article is a failure. Check also for paragraphs containing multiple first-person instances.

**How to fix it:**
Keep at most one "I think" per article, reserved for the single most contested judgement. Convert all others to direct declarative claims.

---

## 20. Feature-summary mode vs diagnostic-buying-guide mode

**What it looks like:**
An article that lists features, explains what each one does, and ends with a recommendation — without ever diagnosing what problem the reader has or which category of tool they actually need.

**Why it is a problem:**
A reader who does not yet know whether they need a portal, a chasing tool, or a practice management platform will not benefit from a feature summary of any one of them. The article's job is to help them diagnose the category first.

**How to detect it:**
Ask: "Does this article help the reader figure out what kind of tool they need, or does it assume they already know?" If the latter, it is in feature-summary mode.

**How to fix it:**
Add a diagnostic frame early: "This article assumes you have already decided you need [category]. If you are not sure whether you need [category A] or [category B], read [link] first."

---

## 21. Category confusion left unresolved

**What it looks like:**
An article compares tools from different categories (e.g., a portal vs a chasing tool) without stating that they are different categories solving different problems.

**Why it is a problem:**
The reader leaves thinking they are choosing between equivalent alternatives when they are actually choosing between different categories of solution. This leads to bad purchasing decisions.

**How to detect it:**
Check whether the article explicitly states what category each tool belongs to and what problem each category solves.

**How to fix it:**
Add a category distinction paragraph before any feature comparison. State which category each tool belongs to and what problem each category is designed to solve.

---

## 22. Fit/misfit revealed too late

**What it looks like:**
An article that provides 800+ words of analysis before stating who the tool is for and who should look elsewhere.

**Why it is a problem:**
The reader who is a bad fit wastes time. The reader who is a good fit lacks early confirmation.

**How to detect it:**
Find the first fit/misfit statement. If it appears after paragraph 3 or after 400 words, it is too late.

**How to fix it:**
Move fit/misfit to paragraphs 1-2. The rest of the article supports and elaborates on that early framing.

---

## 23. Padded verdict repetition

**What it looks like:**
A verdict section that restates the same distinction ("Karbon is for internal workflow, Company Debt is for chasing") three or four times in slightly different words across multiple paragraphs.

**Why it is a problem:**
Repetition wastes the reader's time and signals that the writer has run out of things to say. It reads as padding, not emphasis.

**How to detect it:**
Check the final section of the article. Does it make the same point more than once? Are the last 2-3 paragraphs restatements of the verdict?

**How to fix it:**
State the verdict once with precision. Follow with the next step (what to do, what to measure, what to try). Do not restate.

---

## 24. Structurally correct but lifeless prose

**What it looks like:**
An article that passes every mechanical check (paragraph length, first-person count, no banned phrases, proper evidence treatment) but reads like a compliance document. The prose is accurate, structured, and completely flat. No sentence makes the reader think "that is exactly my problem."

**Why it is a problem:**
Governance compliance without editorial energy produces content that is technically correct but editorially dead. Readers will not finish it, share it, or trust it enough to act on it, because it does not sound like it was written by someone who understands their working day.

**How to detect it:**
Apply the lived-friction test (see `09-voice-governance.md` §7d): does the article name at least one operational frustration in concrete, working-day terms?

Apply the paragraph-purpose test (see `12-structure-governance.md` §3b): does every body paragraph identify pain, translate a feature, expose a gap, sharpen fit, or advance the decision?

If the article passes mechanical checks but fails both of these, it is lifeless.

**How to fix it:**
- Add at least one concrete, recognisable operational scenario in the first third
- Convert at least two feature descriptions into working-day consequences (see approved moves in `09-voice-governance.md` §7c)
- Find the article's sharpest judgement and move it earlier. If there is no sharp judgement, the article needs one.
- Shorten the strongest claim to a single declarative sentence. Let it land.

## 25. Flat authority / synthetic expertise

**What it looks like:**
"Its statutory deadline tracking is solid. The CRM is adequate for the typical small practice. The interface is polished. Client communication is limited."

The article sounds correct but not convincing. Judgements are stated as blanket facts without signalling how they were formed. The prose reads informed in tone but empty in provenance.

**Why it is a problem:**
The reader cannot distinguish this from a summariser who read the product page and produced evaluative sentences. There is no sign of practical observation, comparison reasoning, or worked-through consequence. A sceptical reader would ask "how do you know that?" and find no answer.

**How to detect it:**
Check evaluative sentences in major strengths and weaknesses sections. For each, ask:
- Is the judgement supported by a visible reason in the same paragraph?
- Does it compare against something, narrow to a buyer condition, or show a workflow consequence?
- Could this sentence have been written by someone who only read the product's homepage?

If a section contains three or more flat evaluative statements with no reasoning, comparison, or consequence, it fails.

**How to fix it:**
- Rewrite flat verdicts into observation-led verdicts (see `09-voice-governance.md` §5a)
- Add the reasoning basis in the same paragraph (see `09-voice-governance.md` §5b)
- Tie the claim to a specific buyer condition or workflow consequence (see `12-structure-governance.md` §3c)
- Where genuine insight is missing, flag for human confirmation or deep research instead of bluffing (see `07-human-input-map.md` §12)

**When to remove instead of rewrite:**
If the evaluative claim cannot be grounded in reasoning, comparison, or consequence, and no human input is available to supply the missing insight, delete the sentence. An article without the claim is stronger than one that bluffs authority.

## 26. Taxonomic human-flattening

**What it looks like:**
The article is structurally correct but reads like a categorisation engine rather than a human guide. Sections have compressed labels. Sentences are clipped and declarative. Workflow problems are described in abstract SaaS shorthand rather than lived consequences.

**Why it is a problem:**
Structural correctness is necessary but not sufficient. If the prose reads like a formatted memo — sorting tools into categories with terse one-function sentences — the reader gets classification without understanding. The article technically answers the question but does not help the reader think. It signals "generated content" even when the underlying analysis is sound.

**Symptoms to search for:**

1. Compressed taxonomy labels as section headings ("Full platform switch:", "Specialist add-on:")
2. Repeated noun-heavy phrasing where pronouns would be more natural
3. Chains of clipped declarative sentences without rhythm variation
4. Abstract workflow terms without lived consequences ("document collection challenges", "operational bottlenecks")
5. No reader-oriented section labels — headings describe the system's categories, not the reader's situation
6. Flat memo-like cadence throughout

**How to detect it:**
Read the article aloud. If it sounds like a slide deck or a categorisation report rather than a person helping someone think through a decision, it fails.

**How to fix it:**
- Convert taxonomy labels to reader-led section headings (see `09-voice-governance.md` §10, Rule K)
- Ground at least two abstract claims in recognisable working-day scenarios (see `09-voice-governance.md` §12, Rule M)
- Vary sentence length — break chains of short declaratives with one longer reasoning sentence
- Replace noun-heavy repetitions with pronouns where the referent is clear
- Add at least one humane transition that acknowledges the reader's situation

**When to remove instead of rewrite:**
This failure mode is about prose quality, not content. The fix is always rewriting, not removing.

## 27. Alternatives page collapse

**What it looks like:**
The page appears to answer an alternatives query but collapses into a sequence of vendor summaries ending in a soft pitch for the house product. The reader reaches the end knowing which tools exist but not which path makes sense for their situation.

**Why it is a problem:**
A reader on an alternatives page has a real decision to make. They are not browsing; they are evaluating a switch or wondering if they need to. An article that summarises vendors without classifying the decision leaves them no better equipped than before. It also reads as a commercial funnel, which damages trust.

**Symptoms to search for:**

1. Incumbent described in broad, stable terms — no specific switching trigger
2. Weak or vague articulation of what causes a firm to look for an alternative
3. Unsupported market-pattern language ("growing number", "typically", "tend to") used to describe why readers are searching
4. Adjacent category tool presented as if equivalent to a direct replacement
5. House product (Company Debt) introduced as an elegant resolution without prior concession or buyer segmentation
6. CTA replaces or immediately follows conclusion without a genuine editorial answer to the buyer question
7. No explicit "not the right fit" condition for the house product
8. Reader cannot determine replacement vs augmentation without reading past the fold

**Correction protocol:**

1. Rewrite the opening around the specific buyer trigger — the moment a firm realises the incumbent is not solving their problem
2. Insert the replacement / augmentation / no-switch decision frame before naming any tool
3. Convert all market-pattern claims into sourced statements or bounded editorial judgement (see `10-evidence-governance.md` §10)
4. Move house product mention to below the category clarification, and only after the buyer's path has been established
5. Add explicit "not best fit if…" conditions for the house product
6. Separate the editorial conclusion from the CTA — the conclusion must answer the buyer question; the CTA must follow, not replace it
7. Run against the alternatives-page integrity scoring section in `05-scoring-rubric.md` — must score 0–1 failures to pass

## 28. Mixed-confidence and meta-copy in comparison prose

**What it looks like:**
"A consultancy on Tide spending £5,000 a month cannot access Lloyds at 15.95% — they need a Lloyds BCA. Their realistic options are Barclaycard at 25.5% or Capital on Tap at whatever rate it offers. That bank-account filter changes everything."

**Why it is a problem:**
Three distinct failures compound in a single passage: (1) a precise figure paired with a vague placeholder creates uneven confidence that undermines trust, (2) a fabricated business scenario presents invented detail as if it were verified eligibility data, and (3) self-referential meta-copy ("the table reveals", "most comparisons miss") talks about the article instead of helping the reader decide. The passage sounds assembled, not owned.

**Symptoms to search for:**

1. A paragraph containing both a precise product figure (APR, fee, limit) and a vague filler phrase ("whatever rate", "sort of", "more or less")
2. A hypothetical business profile that cannot be verified against provider eligibility rules
3. Meta phrases: "the table reveals", "this article shows", "most comparisons miss", "one thing worth noting"
4. Generic intensifiers with no mechanism: "changes everything", "huge difference", "worth noting"
5. Paragraphs ending on product description rather than reader consequence
6. Abstract nouns where concrete nouns exist: "landscape", "dynamic", "consideration"

**Correction protocol:**

1. Apply the Fact Pattern Gate (§1, `23-prose-quality-gates.md`): rewrite to single-confidence mode
2. Apply the Scenario Validity Gate (§2): replace unsupported scenarios with eligibility rules, market constraints, or decision consequences
3. Apply the Meta-Copy Stripper (§3): convert article-centric sentences to user-decision sentences
4. Apply the Human-Impact Enforcer (§4): ensure every paragraph ends with a reader consequence
5. Apply the Generic-Intensifier Ban (§6): replace empty emphasis with the exact mechanism
6. Run the `comparison_para_mixed_confidence_and_meta_copy` test (see `23-prose-quality-gates.md`)

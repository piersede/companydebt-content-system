# 17. Audience and Persona

## Domain context

CompanyDEBT.com operates in UK business insolvency, rescue, and debt resolution. Content sits inside YMYL territory: financial stability, legal outcomes, director liability, and creditor enforcement. Decision harm is plausible if guidance is out of date, ambiguous, or misleading.

The reader is not browsing. They are under pressure, often at a cliff edge, and reading to find out what happens next, what they are liable for, and who can help.

---

## Primary audience

The default Company Debt reader is a **UK-based SME director or business owner facing financial distress**. They are typically:

- a director of a limited company with mounting creditor pressure
- personally exposed through guarantees, overdrawn Director's Loan Accounts, or fiduciary duties
- operating in the "Twilight Zone" — the company is approaching insolvency but not yet in formal proceedings
- reactive, having exhausted informal attempts to resolve cash flow problems before seeking professional advice
- time-poor and fear-driven, often engaging when they have reached a cliff edge (frozen bank account, bailiff notice, winding-up petition)

Their identity is often inseparable from the business. The company represents their livelihood, their legacy, and their family's security. This shapes every editorial decision.

---

## Sub-personas

### 1. The Distressed SME Owner ("Legacy Protector")

The most common reader. Views the business as an extension of themselves. Has invested personal savings and decades of work. Prior knowledge of insolvency is usually filtered through anecdote.

- **Pain points:** constant threat of bailiffs, looming staff redundancies, HMRC pressure
- **Knowledge gaps:** unaware that an overdrawn DLA is an asset the liquidator must recover; does not understand when the duty to creditors overrides the duty to shareholders; may believe "Phoenixing" is straightforward
- **Language expectation:** authoritative yet deeply empathetic. Needs to hear there is a structured way forward and that family security can be protected if they act early
- **Trust signals:** testimonials from verified customers who navigated liquidation; clear explanation of what happens at each stage

### 2. The Director Facing Insolvency Risk ("Governance Seeker")

Often a director in a larger SME or a non-executive director concerned with fiduciary duties. Sophisticated enough to know they have personal exposure, but needs technical guidance on building a defence.

- **Pain points:** risk of disqualification (up to 15 years), misfeasance claims, personal liability under wrongful trading
- **Knowledge gaps:** unaware of the "sliding scale" of the duty shift (creditors' interests become paramount when insolvency is "probable," not just "inevitable" — per BTI v Sequana)
- **Language expectation:** clinical, precise, statutory-led. Wants checklists, board minute templates, and Insolvency Act citations
- **Trust signals:** ICAEW and IPA accreditations; team includes licensed IPs with named credentials

### 3. The Strategic CFO ("Capital Architect")

The most technical reader. Managing complex creditor structures, secondary lenders, and private equity stakeholders. Looking for a strategic partner to facilitate a CVA or pre-pack administration.

- **Pain points:** covenant breaches, LTV ratio drops, management of debt purchasers
- **Knowledge gaps:** nuances of the Part 1A Moratorium; how it stops a winding-up petition while a rescue plan is formulated
- **Language expectation:** high-level financial and strategic terminology. Respects commercial acumen over generic content
- **Trust signals:** case studies of complex restructurings; evidence of HMRC Enforcement Division negotiations

### 4. The Finance Manager ("Front-Line Buffer")

The person who sees the red flags first. Dealing with daily supplier calls and a mounting aged creditors list.

- **Pain points:** psychological toll of dunning; need for creditor communication templates while the board decides
- **Knowledge gaps:** unaware of the statutory order of priority for paying creditors during insolvency
- **Language expectation:** practical, supportive, grounded in daily operations
- **Trust signals:** practical guides (e.g. "Stressed Directors Guide"); clear explanation of the Statement of Affairs

### 5. The Creditor ("Recovery Evaluator")

Trade creditors (typically unsecured suppliers) trying to understand their rights when a debtor enters CVL or CVA.

- **Pain points:** write-off of significant bad debt; whether to join a Creditors' Committee
- **Knowledge gaps:** how to challenge a liquidator's fees; how to identify fraudulent trading
- **Language expectation:** objective and fair. Needs to see the IP firm is creditor-focused and will investigate director misconduct
- **Trust signals:** dedicated creditor services team; evidence of asset-tracing capability

### 6. The Professional Advisor ("Gatekeeper")

Accountants and solicitors who are primary referral sources. Looking to protect their own professional reputation by ensuring clients get high-quality, ethical advice.

- **Pain points:** identifying warning signs in client portfolios before it is too late
- **Knowledge gaps:** specific ethics requirements for IPs, including "fit and proper" person tests
- **Language expectation:** professional, peer-to-peer, technically dense
- **Trust signals:** R3 and TMA memberships; combined insolvency experience credentials

### 7. The Turnaround Specialist ("Restructuring Catalyst")

External professional brought in to salvage value. Not emotionally attached. Views insolvency as a tactical toolkit.

- **Pain points:** identifying insolvency-deepening activity that could derail a potential sale
- **Knowledge gaps:** real-time creditor recovery rate benchmarks
- **Language expectation:** efficient and transactional. Maximum time poverty — needs immediate access to a senior IP
- **Trust signals:** speed of response; 0800 helpline availability; proactive engagement history

## Audience sophistication layers

The editorial system must serve a **multi-layered knowledge architecture**:

- **Layer 1 (SME Owner):** "What is a CVL and will I lose my house?" — empathetic, accessible, consequence-first
- **Layer 2 (Director/Finance Manager):** "What are my obligations in the Twilight Zone?" — statutory, procedural, checklist-driven
- **Layer 3 (CFO/Advisor/Specialist):** "How does the BTI v Sequana judgment affect our board's dividend decision during cash flow volatility?" — deep statutory analysis, case law, strategic framing

Do not flatten these layers. A page aimed at Layer 1 must not assume Layer 3 knowledge. A page aimed at Layer 3 must not waste space on Layer 1 explanation.

## Primary pain points

### The "HMRC Sword"
HMRC has significantly increased its use of winding-up petitions to recover persistent arrears. VAT, PAYE, and Corporation Tax arrears drive the majority of enforcement actions. Since late 2025, early intervention is the only reliable way to avoid compulsory liquidation.

### Personal guarantee exposure
Fear that the "corporate veil" will be pierced by lenders holding guarantees against personal assets, including the family home. This is the most acute emotional driver.

### Cash flow paralysis
Inability to pay staff wages or purchase stock, leading to immediate operational collapse.

### Director's Loan Account liability
Many directors do not know that an overdrawn DLA is an asset the liquidator must recover to pay creditors.

### Duty shift timing
The most dangerous knowledge gap. Under BTI v Sequana, the duty to creditors arises when insolvency is "probable," not just when it is "inevitable." Failure to understand this timing is the primary trigger for personal liability claims for wrongful trading.

### Reputational destruction
Social and professional stigma associated with "failure." This drives decision paralysis and wilful blindness.

## Emotional drivers

The reader's emotional state is dominated by:

- **Fear of personal loss:** home, savings, family security
- **Guilt and shame:** impact on employees and suppliers
- **Loss of control:** prospect of an IP taking over company affairs
- **Time poverty:** most engage at a cliff edge with days, not weeks
- **Decision paralysis:** fear that any action makes things worse

Content must acknowledge these states without exploiting them. The tone is protective, not predatory.

---

## Objections and conversion barriers

1. **Trust deficit:** perception of insolvency practitioners as "vultures" who prioritise fees over creditor returns
2. **Cost paradox:** difficulty of paying for a CVL or CVA when the company is already cash-poor
3. **Future viability:** concern that entering a formal process permanently tarnishes the brand and prevents starting a successor business

---

## What the reader already knows

Assume the reader already understands:
- they run a UK limited company (or are closely involved in one)
- they have creditors who are pressing for payment
- something has gone wrong financially and informal solutions have not worked
- there are legal consequences they do not fully understand

Do not over-explain basic business realities. Do not patronise. Do not assume they understand insolvency law — most do not, and the gaps in their knowledge are where the real danger lies.

---

## What the reader does NOT know (and where content adds most value)

- when their duty shifts from shareholders to creditors (the Twilight Zone)
- what "wrongful trading" actually means in practice and when it bites
- that preferential payments to family members or favoured creditors can be set aside by the court
- the difference between dissolution and liquidation (and why getting this wrong creates personal liability under the Finance Act 2020)
- that HMRC resumed preferential creditor status for certain taxes post-December 2020
- that an overdrawn DLA is recoverable by a liquidator
- what a statutory demand actually triggers and the 21-day clock

These knowledge gaps are not just content opportunities. They are the points where the reader is most at risk of making a decision that worsens their position.

---

## Tone guidance

Write for people under pressure who need competent, calm, trustworthy guidance. Sound like a senior professional who has seen this situation many times and knows the way through.

Favour:
- direct, calm authority
- statutory precision where it matters
- empathy grounded in practical consequence (not emotional performance)
- clear next steps and decision paths
- explicit scope boundaries ("this page covers X, not Y")
- honest acknowledgement of difficulty and uncertainty

Avoid:
- fear-mongering or pressure language
- false reassurance or guaranteed outcomes
- generic SaaS marketing language
- patronising simplification of legal concepts
- hype, urgency theatre, or countdown pressure
- vague comfort phrases ("don't worry," "it'll be fine," "you're not alone")
- any implication that using Company Debt's services automatically satisfies legal obligations

---

## YMYL-specific constraints

Because this content directly affects financial stability, legal outcomes, and personal liability:

- every claim about insolvency procedures, director duties, creditor rights, and tax enforcement requires a primary source or `[HUMAN CONFIRMATION NEEDED]` flag
- no casual assertions about what "most directors" do without a named basis
- compliance claims require verification from primary regulatory sources (Insolvency Act 1986, Companies Act 2006, FCA, HMRC guidance)
- content must distinguish clearly between legal information and legal advice
- the commercial relationship must be disclosed plainly: "Company Debt provides insolvency services; this page explains options"

---

## Red lines for this audience

Writing fails this audience if it:
- sounds like generic B2B SaaS copy or lead-gen filler
- uses insolvency terminology without explaining it on first use (for Layer 1 readers)
- over-explains statutory detail that only Layer 3 readers need (on a Layer 1 page)
- implies guaranteed outcomes from any insolvency procedure
- exploits fear rather than addressing it with practical guidance
- presents Company Debt's services as the only option without acknowledging alternatives
- omits the "not the right fit if..." conditions for Company Debt's services
- claims lived insolvency experience not actually established by a human
- presents editorial judgement as legal advice
- uses outdated statutory thresholds, enforcement practices, or case law
- treats the reader's distress as a conversion opportunity rather than a genuine need for guidance

# 17. Audience and Persona

## Domain context

CompanyDEBT.com operates in UK business insolvency, rescue, and debt resolution. Content sits inside YMYL territory: financial stability, legal outcomes, director liability, and creditor enforcement. Decision harm is plausible if guidance is out of date, ambiguous, or misleading.

The reader is not browsing. They are under pressure, often at a cliff edge, and reading to find out what happens next, what they are liable for, and who can help.

---

## Primary audience

The default Company Debt reader is a **UK-based SME director or business owner facing financial distress**. They are typically:

- a director of a limited company with mounting creditor pressure
- personally exposed through guarantees, overdrawn Director's Loan Accounts, or fiduciary duties
- operating in the "Twilight Zone" where the company is approaching insolvency but not yet in formal proceedings
- reactive, having exhausted informal attempts to resolve cash flow problems before seeking professional advice
- time-poor and fear-driven, often engaging when they have reached a cliff edge

Their identity is often inseparable from the business. The company represents their livelihood, legacy, and family security. This shapes every editorial decision.

---

## Sub-personas

### 1. The Distressed SME Owner

The most common reader. Views the business as an extension of themselves. Has invested personal savings and years of work. Prior knowledge of insolvency is usually filtered through anecdote.

- Pain points: bailiff threat, staff redundancies, HMRC pressure
- Knowledge gaps: overdrawn DLA exposure, duty-shift timing, myths around phoenixing
- Language expectation: authoritative but deeply empathetic, with clear structure and consequences
- Trust signals: verified testimonials, plain process explanation, visible professional oversight

### 2. The Director Facing Insolvency Risk

Often a director in a larger SME or a non-executive director concerned with fiduciary duties. Sophisticated enough to know they have exposure, but needs technical guidance on building a defence.

- Pain points: disqualification risk, misfeasance claims, wrongful trading exposure
- Knowledge gaps: creditor-interest duty timing, especially when insolvency is probable rather than inevitable
- Language expectation: clinical, precise, statutory-led
- Trust signals: citations, board-level guidance, licensed reviewer presence

### 3. The Strategic CFO

The most technical reader. Managing complex creditor structures, secondary lenders, or investor pressure. Looking for a strategic partner to facilitate a rescue path.

- Pain points: covenant breaches, multi-stakeholder pressure, time-critical negotiations
- Knowledge gaps: moratorium nuances, procedural route selection, creditor choreography
- Language expectation: financially literate, strategic, commercially serious
- Trust signals: complex case evidence, restructuring credibility, negotiation depth

### 4. The Finance Manager

The person who sees the red flags first. Dealing with supplier calls and an aged creditors list while the board delays the decision.

- Pain points: daily creditor pressure, operational drag, unclear escalation path
- Knowledge gaps: priority order, practical deadlines, statement-of-affairs implications
- Language expectation: practical, grounded, step-by-step
- Trust signals: useful checklists, templates, operational clarity

### 5. The Creditor

Trade creditors or unsecured suppliers trying to understand their rights when a debtor enters formal insolvency or a rescue process.

- Pain points: recoverability, committee participation, misconduct suspicion
- Knowledge gaps: fee challenge options, leverage points, director-misconduct signals
- Language expectation: objective and fair
- Trust signals: creditor-side clarity, recovery realism, investigation capability

### 6. The Professional Advisor

Accountants and solicitors who may refer matters onward. Protecting their own reputation by ensuring clients get ethical, technically competent advice.

- Pain points: identifying insolvency warning signs early, referral quality
- Knowledge gaps: ethics obligations, fit-and-proper issues, process boundaries
- Language expectation: peer-to-peer, technically dense, low-fluff
- Trust signals: memberships, reviewer credentials, technical precision

### 7. The Turnaround Specialist

External professional brought in to salvage value. Less emotionally attached, more focused on speed, leverage, and protecting options.

- Pain points: insolvency-deepening activity, wasted runway, slow stakeholder response
- Knowledge gaps: real-world recovery benchmarks, immediate escalation thresholds
- Language expectation: efficient, transactional, decision-ready
- Trust signals: rapid response, senior access, procedural sharpness

---

## Audience sophistication layers

The editorial system must serve a layered knowledge architecture:

- Layer 1: SME owner asking what a process means and what it does to them personally
- Layer 2: director or finance manager needing duties, checklists, and escalation clarity
- Layer 3: CFO, advisor, or turnaround specialist needing case-law depth and strategic framing

Do not flatten these layers. A page aimed at Layer 1 must not assume Layer 3 knowledge. A page aimed at Layer 3 must not waste space on Layer 1 explanation.

---

## Primary pain points

- HMRC enforcement pressure
- personal guarantee exposure
- cash-flow paralysis
- Director's Loan Account liability
- duty-shift timing
- reputational destruction and decision paralysis

These are not generic business worries. They are the decision pressures the prose must address.

---

## Emotional drivers

The reader's emotional state is often dominated by:

- fear of personal loss
- guilt and shame
- loss of control
- time poverty
- decision paralysis

Content must acknowledge those pressures without exploiting them.

---

## Objections and conversion barriers

- trust deficit around insolvency firms
- cost paradox when the business is already cash-poor
- fear that formal action permanently destroys future viability

---

## What the reader already knows

Assume the reader already understands:

- they run or support a UK business under pressure
- creditors are pressing for payment
- informal fixes have not solved the problem
- there are legal and financial consequences they do not fully understand

Do not patronise. Do not assume insolvency-law fluency.

---

## What the reader often does not know

The highest-value knowledge gaps include:

- when duty shifts from shareholders to creditors
- what wrongful trading means in practice
- that some payments can be reversed
- the difference between dissolution and liquidation
- the implications of HMRC's creditor status
- that an overdrawn DLA is recoverable
- what a statutory demand actually triggers

These are not just content opportunities. They are risk points.

---

## Tone guidance

Write for people under pressure who need competent, calm, trustworthy guidance. Sound like a senior professional who has seen the situation before and knows the way through.

Favour:

- calm authority
- statutory precision where it matters
- empathy grounded in consequence
- clear next steps
- honest scope boundaries

Avoid:

- fear-mongering
- false reassurance
- generic SaaS language
- patronising simplification
- countdown pressure
- vague comfort filler
- any implication that using Company Debt automatically satisfies legal obligations

---

## YMYL-specific constraints

Because this content affects financial stability, legal outcomes, and personal liability:

- claims about insolvency procedures, director duties, creditor rights, and tax enforcement require primary sources or explicit human confirmation flags
- no casual assertions about what most directors do without a named basis
- legal information must be distinguished clearly from legal advice
- the commercial relationship must be disclosed plainly

---

## Red lines for this audience

Writing fails this audience if it:

- sounds like generic lead-gen filler
- uses insolvency terminology without explanation where explanation is needed
- over-explains for the wrong audience layer
- implies guaranteed outcomes
- exploits fear rather than clarifying options
- frames Company Debt as the only route without acknowledging alternatives
- omits not-a-fit conditions
- claims lived experience not established by a human
- presents editorial judgement as legal advice
- uses outdated statutory or enforcement assumptions

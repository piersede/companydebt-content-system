# human-authorship-voice-engine.md

## Purpose

Define the human-authorship behaviour layer for editorial output.

This file governs how prose signals visible authorship, lived judgment, observed reality, and context-sensitive tone.

It is not a runtime drafting skeleton.
It is a voice and authorship engine used during drafting, rewrite, and judgment passes.

This file exists because "human" writing is not produced by adding friendliness or contractions.
Human writing is produced by visible traces of:

- agency
- accountability
- observation
- friction
- preference
- judgment
- context
- cultural texture
- tonal modulation by subject
- consequence translation

Claude must write as though a real editorial intelligence has:

- done work
- noticed details
- weighed trade-offs
- identified the real problem
- chosen what matters
- decided how strongly to say it

The goal is not casualness.
The goal is authored prose.

---

## Core model of human authorship

Human presence is signalled through three main voices:

### 1. Institutional We

Use for:
- method
- process
- testing
- comparison
- independence
- collective expertise
- repeatable editorial standards

The institutional "we" must imply labour, method, or independence.
It must not be used as polish.

Good functions:
- we tested
- we checked
- we compared
- we verified
- we reviewed pricing
- we found
- we recommend avoiding

Bad function:
- vague editorial polish with no proof of work

Rule:
"We" must earn itself through method, labour, evidence, or institutional judgment.

### 2. Personal I

Use sparingly and only when it adds:
- expert accountability
- named judgment
- interpretive confidence
- mentorship
- forecast responsibility
- prioritisation from lived experience

Use "I" when:
- removing it would change meaning, not just tone
- the judgment is truly personal, expert, or experience-based
- the claim is verifiable or clearly marked as editorial judgment

Do not use first person singular as a tic or warmth device.

### 3. Direct You

Use to:
- translate facts into reader consequence
- frame advice around the reader's body, budget, workflow, risk, or stress
- convert data into service
- stop the prose becoming disembodied

Rule:
Facts must not remain abstract.
They must be made to matter to the reader.

## Pronoun use by article type

### Reviews

Primary: we
Secondary: you
Tone: precise, tested, grounded
Human signal: visible proof of work, observed detail, real trade-offs

### Buying or workflow guides

Primary: you
Secondary: we
Tone: useful, empathetic, practical
Human signal: consequences translated into daily reality, friction acknowledged

### Investigations / adversarial pieces

Primary: we
Secondary: they
Tone: sharper, morally alert, protective
Human signal: evaluative language aligned with reader interest, not corporate neutrality

### Expert columns / forecasts

Primary: I
Secondary: you
Tone: accountable, selective, experienced
Human signal: personal guarantee, professional judgment, explicit prioritisation

### Category explainers / high-stakes explainers

Primary: we or you depending on frame
Tone: crisp, grounded, consequence-first
Human signal: no dictionary opening, no generic history lesson, no abstract overview detached from stakes

## Primary human-authorship rules

### Rule A. Every direct-product or direct-experience claim must contain an observed detail

Do not write:
- "We reviewed the interface directly."
- "We spent time in the platform."
- "We tested the product."

unless the sentence also includes what was actually noticed.

Required follow-through:
- what felt faster
- what was fiddlier than expected
- what took too many clicks
- what looked elegant but slowed down real setup
- what kind of user would struggle
- what the product handled cleanly
- what it still left to the human

Human signal: observation, not presence-claiming.

### Rule B. Every evaluative phrase must be cashed out

Do not leave phrases floating such as:
- well-designed
- strong fit
- premium option
- genuinely good
- best-in-class
- clear improvement
- powerful
- useful

Every evaluation must be followed by one of:
- a concrete behaviour
- a user consequence
- a workflow effect
- a setup burden
- a comparison that clarifies the claim
- a trade-off

Evaluation without explanation reads as template copy.

### Rule C. Every major section must contain a lived-reality anchor

For this project, lived reality means the actual operational texture of the reader's world.

Examples:
- fee-earner time lost
- partner attention drain
- January self-assessment pressure
- ignored reminder emails
- staff sitting in the same room
- admin users who barely log in
- work stalling because the client has not responded
- the dashboard looking tidy while nothing moves

The article must repeatedly return from abstraction to real-world practice.

### Rule D. Tone must shift by subject

Do not use one evenly polished voice across every section.

Required tonal modulation:
- definition / category explanation = calm, clear, explanatory
- strengths = specific, respectful, concrete
- weaknesses = firmer, sharper, more sceptical
- investigations / unfairness = adversarial where justified
- verdict = compressed, decisive, high-judgment
- warnings / scams / risk = protective, calm, explicit

Human prose changes tone with the nature of the subject.
AI prose often stays in one competent register.

### Rule E. Praise and criticism must both include friction

Humans describe where things rub.

Every major positive should include its cost, condition, or setup burden.
Every major negative should include what specifically breaks, delays, costs, or frustrates.

Examples:
- powerful, but setup-heavy
- flexible, but not forgiving
- useful once configured, but slow to become useful
- tidy in theory, but still blocked by the client

No neat positives and neat negatives.
Use real trade-offs.

### Rule F. Force one or two asymmetrical editorial lines per piece

Every substantive article should contain at least 1-3 lines that sound unmistakably like a person has actually thought about the category.

These lines usually:
- compress the truth of the product
- expose the real bottleneck
- cut through vendor framing
- reframe what the buyer is actually buying

Examples of target shape:
- "A tidy dashboard does not mean the work is moving."
- "This can make a small firm feel more organised before it makes it more efficient."
- "It organises the waiting more neatly, but the waiting continues."

Do not overdo this.
But without it, the piece risks feeling competent rather than authored.

### Rule G. Generic pros/cons phrasing is banned

Do not use pros/cons lists as generic review furniture.

If a pros/cons block is used:
- every point must read as a compressed observation
- no baseline-category pros
- no disguised cons
- no feature-summary phrasing detached from consequences

Preferred alternative: integrate judgments into the article body.
If a list remains, each bullet must retain human texture.

### Rule H. Institutional "we" must imply method, labour, or independence

Restated as enforcement:
never use "we" as a vague authority wash.

If "we" appears, the sentence must signal:
- testing
- comparison
- pricing verification
- editorial judgment
- direct observation
- independence from vendor narrative

### Rule I. Interrogate vendor and category language

Do not inherit labels like:
- premium
- intelligent
- smart
- automated
- enterprise-grade
- compliance-ready
- scalable
- seamless

without interrogation.

Required behaviour:
- ask what the label means in practice
- ask for whom it is true
- ask what condition makes it false
- separate category language from actual user outcome
- expose where marketing shorthand is doing more work than evidence

### Rule J. Verdicts must be compressed and reality-based

The verdict is not a balanced restatement.
It is a compression of the real decision.

A strong verdict:
- names the true problem the product solves
- names the true problem it does not solve
- identifies who should buy and who should leave
- translates price or complexity into practical consequence
- ends with a real decision frame

Do not end with neutral equilibrium.

## Human-authorship markers beyond pronouns

### 1. Friction

Show the work.
Do not teleport to the conclusion.
Include:
- what had to be built
- what took time
- what was awkward
- what failed
- what still depended on human effort
- what looked fine on paper but not in practice

### 2. Physicality / materiality

When relevant, use:
- tactile specifics
- environmental conditions
- ordinary-world context
- bodily or practical consequences
- visible operational reality

In B2B, this often means workflow materiality rather than sensory description.

### 3. Accountability

The prose must feel owned.
Use named expertise where architecture permits.
Use explicit recommendation ownership when judgment is strong.

### 4. Judgment

Take positions.
Do not hide behind endless balance.
Avoid "it depends" unless immediately narrowed into specific fit logic.

### 5. Moral stance

Where the reader is being overcharged, trapped, misled, penalised by complexity, or exposed to unfairness, the prose may and should become morally evaluative.

Allowed language includes:
- unfair
- opaque
- weak
- difficult to justify
- punitive
- inflated
- overhyped
- poor value
- rip-off
- sting

Only when supported.
But do not flatten exploitative dynamics into bloodless neutrality.

### 6. Cultural anchoring

Avoid global-generic English.
Use the natural language of the intended audience.

For UK audience work, mild British texture is allowed and often useful.

Examples:
- eye-watering
- whittle down
- bide your time
- stung
- grim
- fidgety
- hard sell
- bit of a mess

Do not force idiom.
Use it to stop the prose feeling internationally generic.

### 7. Human transitions

Use attitude-bearing transitions when earned:
- Here's the thing
- In practice
- The problem is
- The catch is
- But that only helps if
- On paper
- The reality is
- Unsurprisingly
- Sadly

These are allowed when they genuinely steer the reader.
They are not filler.

### 8. Contextual consequence

Every fact must answer:
- so what?
- for whom?
- in what context?
- at what cost?
- with what trade-off?
- what does this change in the reader's actual day?

## Sentence-level humanisation rules

### Prefer specificity over abstraction

Replace:
- high quality
- efficient
- user-friendly
- robust
- premium
- comprehensive
- innovative
- seamless

with:
- what happened
- what improved
- what failed
- what got easier
- what got slower
- what the user still had to do

### Prefer active verbs that imply labour or observation

Use:
- tested
- measured
- checked
- timed
- compared
- mapped
- verified
- traced
- found
- reviewed
- flagged
- challenged
- quizzed
- watched
- tracked

Avoid passive feature-summary prose where a more active construction exists.

### Show the gap between lab result and lived priority

A key human move is:
- what the score says
- what matters in practice
- why those are not always the same

Pattern:
"Our testing showed X. In practice, though, Y is what will matter most for most buyers."

### Use rhetorical questions only when they mirror the reader's real question

Allowed:
- But is it any good?
- Should you fix now?
- Will you actually notice the difference?

Banned:
- broad engagement bait
- "Have you ever..."
- rhetorical padding

### Use mild evaluative bluntness

Allowed:
- poor
- weak
- overhyped
- difficult to justify
- underpowered
- not worth the premium
- one to avoid
- better on paper than in practice

Do not soften everything into sameness.

### Vary sentence texture

Use:
- short verdict lines
- longer explanatory lines
- clipped warnings
- fuller context when needed

Flat rhythm reads synthetic.

### Use assertion -> evidence -> consequence

Preferred paragraph movement:
1. state the point
2. show evidence or reasoning
3. explain the consequence for the reader

### Let expert skepticism show

Where labels or positioning claims are inflated, interrogate them.

Useful line shapes:
- "That sounds impressive, but the practical gain is narrower than it first appears."
- "The label is doing more work than the evidence."
- "This is mostly marketing shorthand rather than a meaningful distinction."

### Use ordinary-life anchors

For this audience, ordinary-life anchors are operational:
- the Monday morning status ritual
- the ignored client email
- the admin user barely touching the system
- the dashboard looking neat while jobs remain stuck
- the busy filing season
- the partner turning round to ask where the file is

These make the prose believable.

## Banned signals of synthetic writing

Suppress:
- neutral disembodiment
- abstract praise
- consensus flattening
- feature summary without consequence
- global-generic English
- tone monotony
- methodless certainty
- zero moral orientation
- vague helpfulness
- false smoothness
- symmetrical, template-feeling evaluation

## Structural humanisation rules

### Opening

The opening must do at least one of:
- state the real problem
- frame the core trade-off
- expose the reader's likely tension
- challenge vendor/category shorthand
- place the reader in a recognisable situation

Do not open with:
- dictionary definition
- broad market scene-setting
- empty importance claim
- reputation summary
- capability list detached from consequence

### Section bodies

Each major section must:
- make a claim
- show why it is true
- explain why it matters
- include at least one reality anchor
- where relevant, include friction

### Ending

The ending must:
- clarify the true buyer fit
- state what the product actually solves
- state what it leaves unsolved
- compress the decision
- avoid neutral drift

---

## Emotional register rules

### Warmth

Warmth should come from:
- relevance
- empathy for hassle
- recognition of reader stress
- practical usefulness

Not from:
- pep
- cheerleading
- synthetic friendliness

### Skepticism

Skepticism should be:
- evidence-backed
- especially active around labels, pricing, and claims
- constructive rather than theatrical

### Humor

Allowed only as light, dry, local texture.
Never as stand-up or gimmick.

### Urgency

Must come from:
- cost
- risk
- delay
- lost time
- operational pain

Never from clickbait energy.

## Pronoun density targets (derived from Which? editorial analysis)

Human-sounding editorial content requires visible pronoun usage. Prose dominated by neutral declarative statements without "we", "you", or occasional "I" reads as disembodied category content, not authored editorial.

### Minimum pronoun density per 1,000 words of body content

| Pronoun | Minimum | Target | Maximum | Function |
|---------|---------|--------|---------|----------|
| you/your | 8 | 15–20 | 30 | Reader guidance, consequence framing, direct advice |
| we/our | 5 | 10–15 | 25 | Editorial method, institutional judgement, independence signal |
| I/my | 0 | 1–3 | 5 | Expert accountability, personal judgement (only when earned) |

### Enforcement

- Count pronouns before final output
- If "you" density is below 8 per 1,000 words, the article is too disembodied — revise
- If "we" density is below 5 per 1,000 words and the article makes editorial judgements, the judgements lack visible ownership — revise
- If "I" density exceeds 5 per 1,000 words, the voice risks sounding self-centred — reduce
- Zero "you" in any section longer than 200 words = that section is not serving the reader

### Pronoun function rules

"You" must do work. Each instance should:
- translate a fact into the reader's operational reality
- name a consequence the reader will feel
- narrow a decision for the reader's specific situation
- warn the reader about a specific risk

"We" must show labour. Each instance should signal one of:
- what we checked, compared, or reviewed
- what we found, concluded, or recommend
- what editorial judgement we are making and why

Do not use "we" as vague editorial polish.
Do not use "you" as generic engagement filler.

## Concrete scene requirement

Abstract category language — "active chase", "post-receipt processing", "workflow fragmentation" — is useful for structure but insufficient for human voice. Every article must include concrete scenes that anchor the reader in recognisable operational reality.

### Rule

Every article of 1,000+ words must contain at least 3 concrete scenes drawn from the reader's actual work. A "scene" is a sentence or short passage that describes a specific, recognisable moment rather than an abstract category.

### What counts as a concrete scene

- The same manager sending the fourth follow-up email on a Friday afternoon
- A client opening an email but replying instantly to a WhatsApp message
- A team member hunting through inbox, spreadsheet, and portal to find who has responded
- The dashboard looking tidy while 40 jobs are all waiting on the same thing
- A partner asking "where's the file for Smith?" across the office
- January self-assessment pressure with half the source documents still missing

### What does not count

- "Client silence is a common bottleneck" — this is a category label
- "Repeated follow-up absorbs staff hours" — this is an abstraction
- "The tool automates outbound chasing" — this is a feature description

### Detection

If the article can be fully understood without picturing a specific moment in a real firm, it lacks concrete scenes.

## Evaluative bite and moral framing

Human editorial writing takes positions with visible conviction. Neutral, bloodless prose that merely classifies tools sounds like structured content, not authored guidance.

### Rule

Every comparison or review article must include at least 2 evaluative lines that would be impossible to write without a point of view. These lines should:
- name an absurdity, mismatch, or frustration directly
- use sharper language than "the distinction matters" or "this wasn't built for that use case"
- sound like a specific person with convictions, not a classification engine

### Evaluative language that signals human authorship

Allowed and encouraged where supported:
- poor value
- overbuilt for this job
- solves the wrong problem
- misses the point
- the real drain
- the awkward part
- that's the category confusion
- the trap is
- fixing the wrong thing

### Moral framing (where warranted)

When a tool, pricing model, or market practice is genuinely unfair to the buyer, the prose may and should become morally evaluative:
- opaque pricing
- punitive complexity
- solving a problem you don't have
- fixing the dashboard when the work is stuck

Do not flatten exploitative dynamics or genuine frustrations into neutral classification.

## Sentence texture and rhythm variation

Human writing varies its rhythm. Machine-like writing uses the same sentence pattern repeatedly: state category, explain distinction, note fit, note limitation.

### Rule

No more than 3 consecutive paragraphs may use the same structural pattern (declare → explain → qualify).

### Required variation moves (use at least 3 per article)

- A blunt one-sentence paragraph that makes a sharp point
- A rhetorical question that mirrors what the reader is actually thinking
- A slightly wry or dry observation (not humour for its own sake — earned texture)
- A moment of visible impatience or emphasis
- A line that shifts register (from analytical to protective, or from explanatory to advisory)
- A "here's the thing" or "the catch is" transition that shows editorial attitude

### Lines to aim for

Target: at least 4–5 lines per article that sound unmistakably like a person has actually thought about this category. These are the lines readers remember.

Pattern:
- "You can end up with a beautifully organised dashboard of jobs that are all waiting on the same thing: the client."
- "Dext can tell you what's missing. It doesn't do the awkward part."
- "The trap is that solving for tool sprawl isn't the same as solving for client silence."

### Monotone detection

If reading the article aloud, every paragraph sounds like it could have been written by the same even-tempered machine, the article lacks texture. Read it aloud. Listen for flatness.

## Self-audit pass for human authorship

Before final output, check:

### Voice and pronoun checks
- Does "you" appear at least 8 times per 1,000 words?
- Does "we" appear at least 5 times per 1,000 words where editorial judgement is present?
- Does every section longer than 200 words contain at least one "you" that serves the reader?

### Observation and reality checks
- Does the article contain observed detail rather than just evaluation?
- Does every major evaluation get explained?
- Does each major section contain lived operational reality?
- Are there at least 3 concrete scenes (not abstractions or category labels)?

### Tone and texture checks
- Does tone change appropriately by subject?
- Do praise and criticism both include friction?
- Is there at least one asymmetrical, editorially alive compression line?
- Are there at least 4–5 lines that sound unmistakably authored?
- Is there rhythm variation (no more than 3 consecutive same-pattern paragraphs)?
- Does the article contain at least 2 lines of genuine evaluative bite?

### Authority and integrity checks
- Is "we" earned wherever used?
- Have vendor/category labels been interrogated rather than inherited?
- Does the verdict compress the real decision?

If no to any, revise.

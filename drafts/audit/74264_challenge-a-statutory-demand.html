When an SD1 lands at your registered office, the clock starts ticking. You have 21 days to pay, secure or compound the debt, or the creditor may rely on the demand as evidence that the company is unable to pay its debts and move toward a winding-up petition. Unlike personal debtors, a company cannot apply to “set aside” a statutory demand, so relying on that route is a costly misconception.

This guide sets out the real, time-critical options available to companies, explains when court intervention is possible, and shows how to avoid being forced into compulsory liquidation.

Statutory Demands Against Companies Explained

A statutory demand on Form SD1 is a formal notice requiring a limited company to deal with a qualifying debt. If, after 21 days, the company has not paid the sum, or otherwise secured or compounded it to the reasonable satisfaction of the creditor, section 123(1)(a) of the Insolvency Act 1986 provides that the company is deemed unable to pay its debts (for a sum exceeding £750).

Creditors favour statutory demands because they are relatively quick and inexpensive and, if unanswered, can provide statutory evidence to support a winding-up petition. While individuals may apply to court to set aside a statutory demand, companies cannot. Where a demand is genuinely disputed or fundamentally defective, the company’s court remedy is to seek an injunction restraining the presentation or advertisement of a winding-up petition.

Key facts:

  • Form commonly used: SD1 (HMCTS)
  • Statutory content requirements: Insolvency (England and Wales) Rules 2016, rule 7.3
  • Debt must exceed £750
  • Company has 21 days to pay, secure or compound the debt
  • Non-compliance allows reliance on Insolvency Act 1986 s123(1)(a)
  • A statutory demand is not legally required before presenting a winding-up petition, but is often used as leverage

In short, an SD1 is both a warning and a potential evidential step. Treat it as urgent: confirm the position immediately and take advice early.

Why the 21-Day Clock Matters

You have 21 days from valid service of an SD1 to pay, secure or compound the debt. If you do nothing, the creditor may rely on the statutory presumption of inability to pay when deciding whether to petition.

Key points in that short window:

  • Day 0: Demand served, clock starts
  • Day 21: Statutory test under s123(1)(a) can be met if the debt exceeds £750
  • After day 21: A creditor may move toward a winding-up petition

Immediate knock-on risks:

  • Escalation to a compulsory winding-up petition
  • Public advertisement of a petition if one is presented
  • Loss of commercial confidence once insolvency proceedings begin

Missed-deadline scenario

A statutory demand arrives at the registered office but is overlooked. After the 21-day period expires, the creditor progresses matters toward a petition. By the time advisers are contacted, options are narrower and more expensive, even though the original debt was modest.

Can a Company “Set Aside” a Statutory Demand?

No. Only individuals can apply to set aside a statutory demand. A company that ignores a demand risks the creditor relying on it as evidence of insolvency.

Where there is a genuine dispute or a material defect in the demand, the appropriate court remedy is an urgent injunction to restrain presentation or advertisement of a winding-up petition. This involves court fees and legal costs, and if the application fails, the company may be ordered to pay the creditor’s costs.

Practical ways to address the demand within 21 days include:

  • Paying the debt in full
  • Offering acceptable security
  • Compounding the debt by agreement (for example, instalments)
  • Opening immediate settlement discussions

A common mistake is filing individual debtor forms intended for personal insolvency. These do not apply to companies and provide no protection.

Initial Checklist: Validate the Debt and the Document

Act quickly, but do not assume every statutory demand is flawless. Early checks help determine whether payment, negotiation or court action is appropriate.

  • Amount: Confirm the sum exceeds £750 and matches your records.
  • Dispute: Identify any genuine disputes, counterclaims or credits.
  • Security: Check whether the debt is already secured or partially satisfied.
  • Service: Record when and how the demand was served; the 21-day period runs from
  • Core details: Verify company name, number, creditor identity, debt description and total.
  • Minor errors: Trivial mistakes rarely invalidate a demand on their own.

Response Options Before Day 21

Failing to act within 21 days allows the creditor to rely on the statutory test of insolvency. Choose a response early.

How to Seek an Injunction When the Debt Is Disputed

If the debt is genuinely disputed, an injunction may prevent the creditor from progressing to a winding-up petition.

Grounds the Court Will Consider

The court will usually only intervene where there is:

  • a real, substantive dispute over liability or quantum, or
  • a material defect, such as the debt being fully secured.

Bare assertions are insufficient; documentary evidence is required.

Filing Steps

  1. Assemble contracts, invoices and correspondence supporting the dispute.
  2. Prepare a claim form and witness statement explaining the issue.
  3. File the application urgently and serve it on the creditor.
  4. Be prepared for undertakings or directions from the court.

Costs, Evidence and Risks of an Injunction

An injunction is powerful but costly.

Typical costs include:

  • Court fees
  • Solicitors’ and counsel’s fees
  • Service and copying costs

If unsuccessful, the company will usually be ordered to pay the creditor’s costs.

Paying, Compounding or Negotiating the Debt

If the debt is paid, secured or compounded to the creditor’s reasonable satisfaction within 21 days, the statutory basis for reliance on s123(1)(a) falls away.

Common routes:

  • Pay in full
  • Offer security
  • Agree instalments
  • Engage early with the creditor

HMRC debts: HMRC may consider Time to Pay arrangements where payment difficulties exist. Discussions should be opened promptly and supported by accurate financial information.

What Happens If You Do Nothing?

If no action is taken, the creditor may proceed toward a winding-up petition relying on the statutory demand as evidence of insolvency.

Typical progression:

  • Day 21: Statutory test potentially satisfied
  • Following weeks: Petition preparation and filing
  • If advertised: Proceedings become public
  • Hearing: The court may make a winding-up order

As insolvency becomes likely, directors must act with creditor interests in mind and take appropriate steps to minimise losses.

Frequent Mistakes Directors Make

  • Ignoring the 21-day period
  • Assuming minor errors invalidate the demand
  • Using individual insolvency forms
  • Delaying evidence gathering
  • Failing to document agreements

21-Day Action Timeline and Director Checklist

  • Log service date
  • Review SD1 carefully
  • Gather dispute evidence
  • Engage creditor early
  • Act before day 21

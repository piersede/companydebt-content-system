If you've received an HMRC Follower Notice, or believe you may receive one, prompt attention is essential. These notices carry the risk of significant financial penalties if not dealt with correctly within the statutory 90-day window. 

While the situation can feel intimidating, taking timely and informed action can substantially reduce financial exposure. This guidance is not formal legal advice; you should consider seeking professional tax advice tailored to your circumstances.

What Are HMRC Follower Notices and Why They Matter

HMRC Follower Notices are formal notices issued to taxpayers involved in tax avoidance arrangements where a relevant judicial ruling has already been made. Introduced by the Finance Act 2014, their purpose is to discourage prolonged disputes where a court decision has established principles that HMRC believes defeat the tax advantage being claimed.

A Follower Notice does not determine the underlying tax dispute. Instead, it creates a penalty risk if the taxpayer chooses not to take corrective action after being notified of the relevant ruling. If corrective action is not taken within the statutory timeframe, HMRC may impose a penalty calculated by reference to the denied tax advantage.

The legislation requires several statutory conditions to be met before HMRC can issue a notice, ensuring that it is used only where legally justified.

Right to Contest

A Follower Notice does not remove your right to pursue the underlying tax dispute through the tribunal or courts. However, there is no right of appeal against the Follower Notice itself. If you believe the notice has been issued incorrectly, the law provides a right to make written representations to HMRC within the specified timeframe.

Who Can Receive Them and Under What Conditions

HMRC can issue Follower Notices to individuals, companies, and partnerships, including in cases involving National Insurance contributions, provided that all statutory conditions are satisfied. Broadly, these conditions are:

  • Condition A: There must be an open tax enquiry or an unresolved appeal. In NICs cases, a relevant contributions dispute must exist.
  • Condition B: The return, claim, or appeal must rely on arrangements designed to obtain a tax advantage.
  • Condition C: There must be a relevant judicial ruling that HMRC considers applicable to the arrangements. The ruling must be final (no further appeal available).
  • Condition D: HMRC must not previously have issued a Follower Notice covering the same person, arrangements, tax advantage, period, and ruling, unless that notice was withdrawn.

HMRC is also subject to statutory time limits when issuing Follower Notices. These limits depend on when the relevant judicial ruling became final and when the taxpayer’s return, claim, or appeal was made. The notice must be issued within the legislated timeframe, calculated by reference to the later of those relevant dates.

Immediate Risks and Potential Consequences

If you do not take corrective action within the 90-day deadline, HMRC may charge a follower notice penalty.

Under the current regime, the standard penalty is 30% of the denied tax advantage. HMRC may reduce this penalty to reflect the level of cooperation provided by the taxpayer, but it cannot be reduced below 10%. In certain circumstances, an additional penalty can apply if HMRC considers the taxpayer’s continued litigation to be unreasonable.

Key risks and consequences include:

  • Penalty exposure based on the denied tax advantage.
  • Loss of penalty mitigation opportunities if deadlines are missed.
  • Ongoing uncertainty while disputes remain unresolved.

Failing to act within the deadline also removes the opportunity to make representations challenging the validity of the notice itself.

Your Options for Responding or Challenging

When you receive a Follower Notice, you have three main options:

  1. Take corrective action within 90 days.
  2. Make written representations to HMRC explaining why the notice should be withdrawn.
  3. Continue the underlying dispute, accepting the risk that a penalty may arise if you ultimately lose.

Ignoring the notice entirely carries the highest risk, as it removes the ability to challenge the notice or mitigate penalties.

Partial Corrective Action

You may take partial corrective action, addressing only part of the denied tax advantage. In such cases, any penalty is calculated only on the remaining unresolved portion. This allows taxpayers to concede points clearly covered by precedent while continuing to dispute others.

Step-by-Step Corrective Action

Corrective action depends on whether your case is at enquiry stage or appeal stage.

For Open Enquiry Cases

  1. Amend the return or claim to remove the denied tax advantage.
  2. Notify HMRC in writing of the amendment and the amount of additional tax due.

For Appeal Stage Cases

  1. Settle or withdraw the appeal, or enter into a written agreement with HMRC as required by the notice.
  2. Confirm in writing the corrective action taken and the tax advantage relinquished.

Corrective action must be completed within the statutory deadline to avoid penalties.

Example Timeline

If a Follower Notice is issued on 1 July, the deadline for corrective action or representations is normally 90 days later. If valid representations are submitted and HMRC confirms the notice after review, a new deadline applies based on the date of confirmation.

Understanding Representations and Appeals

You have 90 days from the date of the notice to make written representations to HMRC. Representations may argue that:

  • One or more statutory conditions were not met.
  • The cited judicial ruling is not relevant to your arrangements.
  • The notice was issued outside the permitted time limit.

HMRC must consider the representations and either confirm or withdraw the notice.

If a penalty is later charged, you may appeal the penalty to the tax tribunal. Grounds of appeal include procedural defects and the argument that it was reasonable in all the circumstances not to take corrective action.

How Follower Notices Interact with Accelerated Payment Notices

Follower Notices and Accelerated Payment Notices (APNs) serve different purposes and can be issued at the same time.

  • A Follower Notice creates a penalty risk if corrective action is not taken.
  • An APN requires the disputed tax to be paid upfront, regardless of ongoing disputes.

Paying an APN does not count as corrective action for a Follower Notice. Separate steps are required to avoid penalties.

Special Considerations for Partnerships

Partnerships are subject to a different penalty mechanism. The total partnership penalty is calculated as a percentage of the denied advantage at partnership level and is then apportioned between partners according to their profit shares.

Only the representative partner can make representations or appeal a partnership penalty on behalf of the partnership.

Scenario: Partial Settlement

Where a partnership takes partial corrective action, the penalty exposure is limited to the remaining disputed advantage, reducing the overall penalty risk for individual partners.

Common Misunderstandings

❌ Myth: A Follower Notice removes appeal rights.

✅Correction: You can still pursue the underlying tax dispute, but the notice itself can only be challenged by representations.

❌ Myth: Paying an APN settles the dispute.

✅ Correction: Payment does not constitute corrective action for follower notice purposes.

❌ Myth: Ignoring the notice has no immediate effect.

✅ Correction: Missing deadlines removes the right to make representations and increases penalty exposure.

❌ Myth: Partial compliance is not allowed.

✅ Correction: Partial corrective action is permitted and can reduce penalties.

Practical Examples and Mistakes to Avoid

Best Response:  A company receives a Follower Notice, seeks advice immediately, amends its return within 90 days, and notifies HMRC. No penalty is charged.

Worst Response:  A taxpayer ignores the notice entirely, misses the deadline, and later faces a substantial penalty calculated on the denied advantage.

Key Lessons:

  • Act within statutory deadlines.
  • Understand the distinction between representations, corrective action, and appeals.
  • Early professional advice can materially reduce risk.

Your Next Step

If you receive an HMRC Follower Notice, act quickly. Identify the deadline, assess whether the notice is valid, and decide whether to make representations, take corrective action, or continue the dispute with a clear understanding of the risks. Timely, informed decisions are critical to limiting penalties and uncertainty.

For UK company directors facing financial difficulties, being unable to afford redundancy payments can be a major source of stress. Even in financial distress, employers retain legal obligations to their employees. Where an employer cannot pay statutory redundancy and related sums, employees may be able to claim directly from the government through the Redundancy Payments Service (RPS), which pays certain statutory entitlements from the National Insurance Fund.

However, access to these payments depends on the employer’s circumstances and, in many cases, the commencement of a formal insolvency procedure. Directors who delay taking appropriate action risk worsening the position for employees and exposing themselves to potential legal consequences. Seeking professional insolvency advice at an early stage is essential to ensure compliance with the law and to protect both the company and its directors.

Why Inability to Pay Redundancy Poses Serious Risks

Failing to meet redundancy obligations can have serious legal and financial consequences. Employees may bring claims to an employment tribunal, including claims for unpaid redundancy pay or for failures to comply with consultation requirements during collective redundancies.

In collective redundancy situations, tribunals can make protective awards where employers fail to consult properly. These awards can be substantial and add further pressure to an already distressed business.

Delays in addressing insolvency can also increase director exposure. If directors continue trading when they knew, or ought to have known, that insolvent liquidation was unavoidable, they may face wrongful trading claims and be ordered to contribute personally to company losses.

Key risks include:

  • Employment tribunal claims for unpaid statutory redundancy.
  • Protective awards for failure to consult in collective redundancies.
  • Potential personal liability where wrongful trading or misconduct is established.

Prompt action and professional advice are critical to reducing these risks and ensuring that employee rights are handled correctly.

Essential Statutory Entitlements for Employees

When an employer cannot pay because of insolvency or refusal, employees may be able to claim certain statutory payments from the Secretary of State via the Redundancy Payments Service (RPS). These include:

  • Statutory Redundancy Pay - Available to employees with at least two years’ continuous service. Entitlement is calculated at:
    • ½ week’s pay for each full year under age 22
    • 1 week’s pay for each full year aged 22 to 40
    • 1½ weeks’ pay for each full year aged 41 or over
      Service is capped at 20 years. Weekly pay is capped at £719 for redundancies where the relevant date falls on or after 6 April 2025.
  • Statutory Notice Pay - One week’s pay for each year of service, up to a maximum of 12 weeks. Where the employer cannot pay, eligible employees may claim from the National Insurance Fund.
  • Holiday Pay - Pay for untaken statutory holiday accrued in the 12 months before insolvency, capped at 6 weeks.
  • Wage Arrears - Up to 8 weeks’ pay for unpaid wages, commission, overtime or bonuses, subject to the statutory weekly cap.
  • Protective Awards - Tribunals may award up to 90 days’ pay where collective consultation rules are breached. However, where the employer is insolvent, the RPS will pay no more than 8 weeks’ pay of any protective award, subject to the statutory cap.

Enhanced contractual redundancy terms are not paid by RPS and remain unsecured claims against the insolvent employer.

Why Formal Insolvency Might Be Required

In most cases, employees can only access payments for unpaid wages, holiday pay, notice pay and protective awards after a formal insolvency event, such as liquidation or administration. Informal wind-downs or cessation of trading do not trigger access to these statutory payments.

For statutory redundancy pay, employees may also apply to the Secretary of State if the employer refuses or fails to pay and reasonable steps have been taken to recover the payment, even if formal insolvency has not yet occurred.

Where insolvency is unavoidable, appointing an insolvency practitioner allows the correct procedures to be followed and enables employees to receive the necessary reference details to make claims. Early action also helps directors demonstrate that they have acted responsibly and in accordance with their duties.

How the Redundancy Payments Service Works

The Redundancy Payments Service (RPS) is part of the Insolvency Service and pays certain statutory employment debts from the National Insurance Fund when an employer cannot pay.

Employees usually apply online using the government service for claiming redundancy and other money they are owed. To claim statutory notice pay, employees must first submit a redundancy claim and then use a separate service once instructed.

Claims are only accepted once the relevant conditions are met, which typically include a formal insolvency process and confirmation details from the insolvency practitioner.

Payments are subject to the statutory weekly cap of £719 (from April 2025). Any sums above statutory limits remain claims against the insolvent employer’s estate.

Steps Employers Should Take Immediately

When it becomes clear that redundancy payments cannot be met, directors should act without delay.

  • Seek advice from a licensed insolvency practitioner.
  • Communicate openly with employees about the situation and their statutory rights.
  • Collate payroll, holiday and employment records promptly.
  • Ensure dismissals and consultation obligations are handled correctly, particularly where collective redundancies apply.

Timely and transparent action can significantly reduce risk for both employees and directors.

Important Time Limits

  • Statutory Redundancy Pay: Applications must generally be made within 6 months of the employment ending.
  • Notice Pay: Claims can only be made after the statutory notice period has expired and after the redundancy claim has been submitted.
  • Tribunal Claims: Protective award claims must be lodged within the applicable tribunal time limits.

Missing deadlines can jeopardise employee entitlements.

Director Liabilities and Personal Exposure

Directors are not automatically personally liable simply because a company cannot pay redundancy. Personal liability typically arises only where there is misconduct, such as wrongful trading, fraudulent trading or breaches of statutory duties.

To reduce risk, directors should:

  • Seek professional advice early.
  • Keep accurate financial records.
  • Prioritise creditor interests when insolvency is likely.
  • Avoid continuing to trade where insolvency is unavoidable.

Responsible conduct is the key factor in protecting directors from personal exposure.

Handling Collective Redundancies and Protective Awards

Where an employer proposes 20 or more redundancies within a 90-day period, there is a legal duty to consult with employee representatives. Failure to do so may result in a protective award of up to 90 days’ pay per employee.

If the employer is insolvent and a tribunal makes a protective award, the RPS will automatically consider payment for eligible employees, but payment is limited to 8 weeks’ pay, subject to the statutory cap.

Employees included in the tribunal decision do not usually need to make a separate application for the protective award payment.

Examples and Common Pitfalls

A common mistake is delaying formal insolvency in the hope that trading conditions will improve. This can prevent employees from accessing statutory payments promptly and may increase director risk.

Other frequent issues include incomplete payroll records, misunderstanding statutory caps, and failing to consult properly during collective redundancies.

Early engagement with an insolvency practitioner and accurate record-keeping can prevent most of these problems.

Next Steps for Directors

If your company cannot afford redundancy payments, early action is essential. Consulting a licensed insolvency practitioner can help you choose the appropriate formal process, ensure employees can access statutory entitlements, and reduce personal risk.

Provide complete records, communicate clearly with staff, and comply with all statutory obligations. Taking decisive, informed action protects both your workforce and your position as a director.

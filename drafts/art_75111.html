An overdrawn director's loan account means you owe money to the company. If the company enters insolvency, the liquidator will demand repayment. It is not negotiable, it is not forgiven, and it is one of the first and easiest assets the liquidator pursues.

We see overdrawn DLAs in almost every small company insolvency we handle. The balance usually built up gradually: a personal expense paid from the company account here, a cash withdrawal there, £500 a month that was supposed to be reconciled at year-end but never was. By the time the company enters liquidation, the balance is £15,000, £30,000, sometimes £60,000. The director often does not know the exact figure until the liquidator tells them. We tell every director approaching insolvency: check your DLA balance now, before the liquidator does.

Quick Answer: What Happens to an Overdrawn DLA in Insolvency

The overdrawn balance is an asset of the company. The liquidator has a duty to recover it for the benefit of creditors. They will write to you demanding repayment of the full amount. If you do not pay voluntarily, they can pursue the claim through the courts and enforce against your personal assets. Repaying the DLA during or shortly before insolvency creates preference risk under section 239 of the Insolvency Act — the liquidator can claw back payments to connected parties made within two years of insolvency. We advise taking specific advice on the timing and method of any DLA repayment before the company enters a formal process.

How Director's Loan Accounts Become Overdrawn

A director's loan account tracks money flowing between you and the company. When the company pays you dividends, salary, or expenses, the DLA is credited. When you take money from the company beyond your declared income, the DLA is debited. If debits exceed credits, the account is overdrawn — you owe the company money.

We find that overdrawn DLAs rarely result from a single large withdrawal. They accumulate through:

  • Personal expenses paid from the company account. Fuel, shopping, meals, subscriptions — individually small but cumulative.
  • Cash withdrawals without proper documentation. Money taken from the company that was not allocated to salary, dividends, or legitimate expenses.
  • Dividends declared but not supported by distributable reserves. If the company did not have sufficient profits to cover the dividend, it is reclassified as a loan.
  • Salary not formally processed through PAYE. Money taken as salary but not run through payroll creates a loan, not employment income.

We stress the dividend point because it catches directors by surprise. You declared a £20,000 dividend based on the management accounts showing sufficient reserves. The year-end accounts, prepared months later, showed the reserves were not there. The dividend becomes a loan. The loan becomes an overdrawn DLA. The DLA becomes a liquidator claim.

Tax Implications of an Overdrawn DLA

An overdrawn DLA creates tax consequences even before insolvency:

  • Section 455 Corporation Tax charge. If the DLA is still overdrawn 9 months after the company's year-end, the company must pay a 33.75% tax charge on the outstanding balance. This is refunded if the DLA is repaid, but the charge compounds the company's tax liabilities while it remains outstanding.
  • Benefit in kind. If no interest (or interest below the official rate) is charged on the loan, the difference is a taxable benefit in kind, reported on your P11D and subject to income tax and Class 1A NICs.
  • Potential reclassification as income. HMRC can argue that withdrawals from the company that were treated as loans were actually income disguised as borrowing, particularly if there was never any realistic intention to repay.

We find that the tax position often makes the DLA problem worse than the headline balance suggests. A £30,000 overdrawn DLA may also carry a £10,000 section 455 charge, benefit-in-kind tax, and HMRC interest, pushing the total exposure well above the loan balance itself.

Can You Repay the DLA Before Insolvency?

This is the question every director asks, and the answer is complicated by preference risk.

Repaying your DLA when the company is solvent is straightforward and creates no insolvency issues. But repaying when the company is insolvent or approaching insolvency is a connected-party preference under section 239 of the Insolvency Act. The liquidator can claw back the repayment within two years. The preference is presumed for connected parties — the liquidator does not need to prove you intended to prefer yourself. They just need to show the payment was made.

We see directors who rush to repay their DLA the week before the CVL, believing this clears the problem. The liquidator recovers the payment, the director has paid twice (once to repay, once when the liquidator claws it back), and the conduct report notes the attempted preference. We advise: do not repay your DLA without specific insolvency advice on timing. If the company is already insolvent, the repayment will almost certainly be reversed.

What the Liquidator Does About Your Overdrawn DLA

The liquidator's approach is systematic:

  1. Identifies the balance. From the company's accounts, bank statements, and your statement of affairs.
  2. Writes to you demanding repayment. Typically within the first month of appointment.
  3. Negotiates if you engage. Liquidators prefer negotiated repayment (lump sum or instalments) over court proceedings because it is faster and cheaper for the estate.
  4. Pursues through the courts if you do not pay. The claim is straightforward — the company's accounts show the debt, and the director cannot dispute the amount without producing records that contradict it.
  5. Enforces the judgement. Through bailiffs, charging orders against your property, or bankruptcy proceedings.

We tell directors: cooperate with the liquidator on the DLA. A negotiated repayment plan is better than a court judgement. The liquidator will pursue the claim regardless — it is an asset of the company and they have a duty to recover it. Ignoring the demand letter does not make the debt go away. It makes the recovery more expensive (legal costs are added) and the conduct report worse.

How to Manage Your DLA Before Insolvency

  1. Check the balance now. Ask your accountant for the current DLA position. Do not guess.
  2. Stop taking money from the company beyond formally declared salary and dividends supported by distributable reserves.
  3. If the company is still solvent, consider repaying now. A repayment while solvent is clean. A repayment while insolvent is a preference.
  4. If the company is approaching insolvency, take specific advice. The timing of any DLA repayment relative to the insolvency is critical. A licensed insolvency practitioner can advise on the safest approach.
  5. Document everything. Keep records of what every withdrawal was for. Undocumented withdrawals are treated as loans by default.

FAQs About Overdrawn Directors' Loan Accounts

Can the liquidator force me to repay my director's loan?

Yes. The overdrawn balance is an asset of the company. The liquidator has a statutory duty to recover it. If you do not pay voluntarily, they can pursue the claim through the courts and enforce against your personal assets.

Can I repay my DLA before the company enters liquidation?

If the company is solvent, yes. If the company is insolvent or approaching insolvency, repayment is a connected-party preference under section 239 and the liquidator can claw it back within two years. Take specific insolvency advice on timing before making any repayment.

What is the section 455 tax charge?

If a director's loan account remains overdrawn 9 months after the company's year-end, the company must pay Corporation Tax at 33.75% on the outstanding balance. The charge is refunded if the loan is repaid, but it adds to the company's tax liabilities while outstanding.

Is an overdrawn DLA a criminal offence?

Not in itself. An overdrawn DLA is a civil debt. However, if the withdrawals were designed to defraud creditors, or if you concealed the DLA from the liquidator, criminal liability may arise under sections 206-211 of the Insolvency Act. The DLA itself is a conduct matter in any disqualification assessment.

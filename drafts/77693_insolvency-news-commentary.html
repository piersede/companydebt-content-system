Key Insolvency News Trends: What the Statistics Are Saying

CVL Volumes and What the Quarterly Statistics Actually Tell Directors

The Insolvency Service publishes company insolvency statistics quarterly. The numbers have been running at historically elevated levels since 2022.

In the twelve months to mid-2025, Creditors' Voluntary Liquidations made up around 76% of all corporate insolvencies in England and Wales, with around 1,583 CVLs recorded in July 2025 alone.

That figure matters to you not as an abstract statistic but as a signal about director behaviour: most of those were directors who chose to act rather than wait for creditors to force the issue.

The rate in England and Wales stood at 52.5 per 10,000 registered companies as of July 2025, the highest rate in the UK. What we find when we look at case files in that cohort is that the gap between "trading in difficulty" and "entering formal insolvency" has shortened.

Directors are calling sooner, partly because the cost of delay has become more visible since Crown preference was reinstated in December 2020. If HMRC is your largest creditor and you delay, the outcome for everyone else gets worse.

HMRC Enforcement Trends: How the Creditor Posture Has Changed Since 2020

The Finance Act 2020 reinstated HMRC's preferential creditor status for VAT, PAYE, employee NIC, CIS deductions, and student loan deductions from 1 December 2020. That single change restructured the creditor waterfall.

HMRC now sits above floating-charge holders and most unsecured creditors when a company enters insolvency, which means every other creditor recovers less whenever HMRC is owed significant arrears.

The practical consequence for directors is that ignoring HMRC debt in the hope it gets absorbed later is a worse bet than it was five years ago.

We track HMRC enforcement patterns closely through our HMRC debt collection guide because the signals are often directional. When HMRC accelerates its winding-up petition activity, it tends to cluster in sectors where arrears have been building longest. In the accommodation and food sector, that clustering has been visible since late 2022.

What Administration Upticks Signal About Larger Company Stress

While CVL volumes dominate the aggregate statistics, the administration figures are worth watching separately. An uptick in administrations usually means larger or more complex entities are hitting distress: businesses that are too big or too entangled to close quietly, or where a rescue or sale of the business as a going concern is at least plausible.

The Corporate Insolvency and Governance Act 2020 introduced a new standalone moratorium and the restructuring plan as formal tools, but their use remains relatively limited outside mid-market and larger cases.

If your business is genuinely viable but structurally distressed, a Company Voluntary Arrangement or administration route may be relevant. The statistics tell you whether the market is treating rescue as realistic at a given moment.

Right now, lenders and practitioners are broadly supportive of restructuring where there is a credible trading base. The window closes faster than directors usually expect.

Insolvency News by Sector: Failure Cascades and Risk Concentration

Construction: Why the Sector Keeps Leading the Insolvency Tables

Construction recorded 3,934 insolvencies in the twelve months to August 2025, accounting for around 17% of all UK corporate insolvencies. The figure is not random.

Construction is a sector where most of the financial risk sits with subcontractors and smaller developers, not the main contractor. Payment terms run to 60 or 90 days while input costs, materials, plant, and labour, move faster than contract prices can follow.

The pattern we see repeatedly in construction cases is a cascade. A main contractor delays payment because its own client has delayed. The subcontractor runs out of headroom to fund the gap, and by the time the payment arrives the subcontractor has already fallen over.

If you are in the supply chain for a large construction project and your debtor is showing signs of stress, slow payment, revised programmes, staff changes at the client, the time to take advice is now and not when the CVL notice arrives.

Retail, Accommodation and Food: Margin Compression and the Consumer Confidence Trap

Retail and wholesale trade (3,710 insolvencies, 16% of the total) and accommodation and food services (3,365, 14%) together account for almost a third of all UK corporate insolvencies.

Both sectors share the same underlying problem: they sell directly to consumers whose spending behaviour is highly sensitive to confidence, and they carry costs, energy, labour, business rates, that are relatively fixed in the short run.

The food sector version of this is the restaurant that looks full on a Saturday but runs at 40% capacity Tuesday through Thursday. Fixed costs do not move with the occupancy rate. If you own a hospitality business, you know the management accounts tell a different story from the busy nights.

The Insolvency Service data confirms what many operators feel but do not always name: consumer-facing SMEs with high fixed-cost bases are disproportionately exposed in this environment.

Regional Variation: What Scotland and Northern Ireland Data Adds to the Picture

Scotland's insolvency rate of 51.4 per 10,000 companies is close to England and Wales but with a different composition. Scotland sees a higher proportion of compulsory liquidations, suggesting a more creditor-driven enforcement approach rather than director-initiated closure.

Northern Ireland remains the lowest at 36.9 per 10,000, though whether that reflects genuine economic resilience or a lag in creditor enforcement is harder to determine from the aggregate data alone.

For directors operating across borders, the regional variation matters because the professional advice network, court processes, and enforcement timelines differ. Scottish insolvency law has its own procedural framework, and the Insolvency Service figures for Scotland are published separately.

Insolvency News: Regulatory Changes and Compliance Signals

What the Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023 Means in Practice

The Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023 introduced mandatory identity verification for directors and Persons with Significant Control from November 2025. New directors must verify before appointment.

Existing directors verify during a 12-month transition period tied to their next confirmation statement. The Companies House figures for estimated directors affected run to around 6 to 7 million individuals.

This is not primarily an insolvency story, but it connects to one.

Directors who fail to verify cannot file essential documents. In a distressed company where every filing deadline carries risk, a late confirmation statement can trigger HMRC scrutiny, and an unfiled annual return can support a disqualification case. The compliance gap is not trivial. If you are working through a restructuring or winding down a company, verify first.

Crown Preference and What It Changed for Unsecured Creditors

The reinstatement of Crown preference in December 2020 is now part of the operating reality for any director managing creditor relationships.

HMRC's elevated status means that in a CVL or compulsory liquidation, VAT and PAYE arrears rank ahead of floating-charge holders and all unsecured creditors. In practice, this has reduced recovery rates for trade creditors and invoice finance facilities in cases where HMRC debt has accumulated.

The asymmetry is worth naming. A supplier owed £50,000 by a company that also owes HMRC £200,000 in VAT arrears faces a much poorer recovery prospect than the same supplier in a 2019 insolvency. If you are a creditor tracking whether to pursue debt or write it off, the HMRC arrears figure in the Statement of Affairs is the number to find first.

Insolvency News: Case Law Shaping Director Exposure

The Bilta Ruling and What Fraudulent Trading Liability Now Covers

The Supreme Court's decision in Bilta (UK) Ltd v Tradition Financial Services Ltd expanded the scope of fraudulent trading liability under section 213 of the Insolvency Act 1986. The ruling clarified that third parties who knowingly participate in fraudulent activities, not just directors, can be held liable.

For most directors this is background noise. For those whose companies have transacted with connected parties in circumstances that a liquidator might later examine, it is not.

The practical read: due diligence on counterparties in distressed transactions has become more important, not less.

A pre-pack sale of a business to a connected party, a payment to a related company at less than market value, or a director loan repaid while trade creditors go unpaid, these are all areas where both director and counterparty face scrutiny under the widened liability framework. Our pre-pack guide covers the transaction-at-undervalue risk in detail.

Wrongful Trading and the Evidential Bar Directors Need to Clear

Section 214 of the Insolvency Act 1986 creates personal liability for directors who continue trading when they knew or ought to have concluded that there was no reasonable prospect of the company avoiding insolvent liquidation. The test is objective: not what the director believed, but what a reasonably diligent director with their knowledge and experience would have concluded.

The evidence that protects directors is board minutes, financial forecasts, and advice records that show the decision to continue trading was taken on a credible evidential basis.

A director who can point to a cashflow forecast showing a path to viability, and whose file shows they took professional advice and documented the decision, is in a materially different position to one who kept trading because they were hoping things would improve.

The liquidator reads the file in hindsight. The minutes should be written as though that reading was always coming.

Frequently Asked Questions About Insolvency News and Commentary

How often does the Insolvency Service publish company insolvency statistics?

The Insolvency Service publishes monthly and quarterly company insolvency statistics for England and Wales. Monthly releases cover total company and individual insolvencies broken down by type: CVL, compulsory liquidation, administration, CVA, and moratorium.

The quarterly releases include more detailed sector and regional breakdowns. Scotland and Northern Ireland publish their own separate statistics. All releases are available at gov.uk under the Insolvency Service statistics series.

What does a high CVL rate tell a director about the insolvency environment?

A high proportion of CVLs in the insolvency statistics indicates that directors are choosing to enter formal closure before creditors force the issue. This is generally the more orderly outcome: the director appoints the liquidator, there is less chance of compulsory winding-up, and the conduct review process tends to go more smoothly when the director has been cooperative.

When compulsory liquidation rates rise relative to CVLs, it suggests more directors are being caught off-guard or are resistant to taking advice until the position has deteriorated significantly.

How did Crown preference change insolvency outcomes for trade creditors?

From 1 December 2020, HMRC became a preferential creditor for VAT, PAYE, employee NIC, CIS deductions, and student loan deductions under the Finance Act 2020. This moved HMRC above floating-charge holders and all unsecured creditors in the payment waterfall.

Trade creditors and unsecured lenders now recover after HMRC in any insolvency where those taxes are in arrears. The practical effect is that the larger the HMRC debt relative to realisable assets, the less trade creditors and unsecured lenders receive. This change has made the HMRC arrears figure the first number worth examining in any Statement of Affairs.

Why does the construction sector consistently record the most insolvencies in UK statistics?

Construction is a sector where financial risk is structurally distributed down the supply chain. Main contractors pass cost overrun and delay risk to subcontractors through contract terms, while subcontractors carry input costs that do not wait for project completion. Payment delays on one contract can cascade into insolvency for a smaller firm with no cashflow buffer.

The sector also has a high number of registered companies relative to its economic size, which inflates the insolvency rate on a per-company basis. Subcontractors in particular are exposed when a main contractor enters administration, because their claims become unsecured and are often recovered at very low rates.

What is wrongful trading and when does it create personal liability for directors?

Wrongful trading under section 214 of the Insolvency Act 1986 arises when a director continues trading after the point at which they knew or ought to have concluded there was no reasonable prospect of avoiding insolvent liquidation.

The test is objective: what would a reasonably diligent director with their level of knowledge and experience have concluded? Personal liability is not automatic, as the court must order a contribution to the company's assets.

A liquidator who finds the director traded on for months without adequate evidence that the position was viable will apply to court for such an order.

The defence requires documented financial forecasts, evidence of professional advice taken, and board minutes that record the rational basis for continuing. Directors who hope the situation improves without documenting why they believed it would are in a much weaker position if the company later fails.

How do insolvency news trends help a director assess their own company's risk?

Sector-level insolvency statistics are useful as a relative benchmark. If your sector is recording historically high failure rates, your own creditors and lenders are likely to be more cautious and less willing to extend further credit or forbearance. Rising administration activity in a sector you supply into signals that your receivables are at greater risk.

HMRC enforcement patterns matter because they indicate where the tax authority is prioritising debt recovery. If your sector is seeing increased winding-up petition activity from HMRC, the likelihood of your own arrears being treated with flexibility rather than enforcement is lower.

Use sector data to test whether your own risk profile is consistent with market norms. If you are an outlier, understand why.

Where can I find the latest UK company insolvency statistics?

The Insolvency Service publishes monthly company insolvency statistics on gov.uk, typically released within six to eight weeks of the reference month. The quarterly releases include sector, regional, and procedure-type breakdowns and are the more useful data source for directors tracking structural trends rather than monthly noise.

Statistics for Scotland are published separately by the Accountant in Bankruptcy. The Bank of England's Credit Conditions Survey, published quarterly, is a useful complement: it shows how lenders are adjusting credit availability to businesses, which is an early indicator of whether financial conditions are tightening before insolvency numbers reflect it.

The Notice of Enforcement — often still called a "distraint order" in older company correspondence — arrives by post, typically at the registered office, occasionally taped to the front door of the trading premises. The letter sets out the debt, the creditor, and a specific date seven days from issue. That date is the first day enforcement agents are legally entitled to attend and take control of goods.

Most directors read the notice as a threat. It is not. It is a seven-day statutory window, precisely defined under the Tribunals, Courts and Enforcement Act 2007, during which the company can still resolve the underlying debt without enforcement fees being added or assets being placed under a Controlled Goods Agreement.

This page sets out what a distraint order or Notice of Enforcement actually means, the step-by-step process that follows, the director's rights during the window, and the specific responses that resolve the position before the enforcement fees begin.

Understanding Distraint and the Taking Control of Goods Process

"Distraint" is the older common-law term for a creditor's right to seize a debtor's goods to satisfy a debt. Since April 2014, the statutory framework governing the procedure has been the Tribunals, Courts and Enforcement Act 2007 and the Taking Control of Goods Regulations 2013. The modern terminology is "taking control of goods"; the older "distraint" language persists in HMRC correspondence and commercial usage.

Creditors who can instruct certified enforcement agents against a company:

  • HMRC for unpaid tax, under distinct statutory powers that do not require a prior court judgment.
  • Judgment creditors after obtaining a CCJ, via a warrant of control.
  • Commercial landlords for at least 7 days' rent arrears under Commercial Rent Arrears Recovery (CRAR).
  • Local authorities for unpaid business rates.

Each route has slightly different preconditions, but the procedural flow once agents are instructed is standardised: Notice of Enforcement → at least 7 clear days → attendance → CGA or immediate removal of goods → sale.

Step-by-Step: What Happens During the Distraint Process

  1. Compliance stage. The creditor instructs a certified enforcement agency. A £75 compliance fee is charged. The agency serves a Notice of Enforcement on the debtor giving 7 clear days (excluding Sundays and bank holidays) before attendance.
  2. Enforcement stage. On or after day 8, an enforcement agent attends the debtor's premises. A £235 fixed fee plus 7.5% of any debt over £1,500 is added. The agent inspects, lists, and values goods that can be taken into control.
  3. Controlled Goods Agreement. The agent typically offers a CGA — a written agreement under which the listed goods stay on the premises but are legally under the agent's control. The debtor has 7–14 days (set in the agreement) to pay before the agent returns.
  4. Sale or disposal stage. If the debt is not settled inside the CGA window, the agent returns, removes the listed goods, and arranges sale. A £110 fee plus 7.5% of debt over £1,500 applies. Sale is usually by auction at significantly below market value.
  5. Distribution of proceeds. Sale proceeds go to enforcement fees first, then to the creditor for the underlying debt. Any surplus is returned to the debtor; any shortfall remains owing.

The arithmetic in practical cases: a £5,000 debt that runs through the full enforcement track picks up around £945 in statutory agent fees before any interest, plus the sale discount that puts the realised amount meaningfully below book value. The economic case for settling at the compliance stage is overwhelming where payment is possible.

Directors' Rights and Obligations During a Distraint

The rights directors have during the process:

  • Right to check the agent's certification. Enforcement agents must be certified by the County Court under section 64 of the 2007 Act and must carry proof of authorisation. Ask for it.
  • Right to restrict access outside the hours of 6am–9pm (Regulation 13 of the 2013 Regulations), and against forced entry in most circumstances at residential premises.
  • Right to claim exempt goods — tools of the trade up to £1,350, third-party-owned assets, leased or HP equipment where title has not passed, stock with retention of title.
  • Right to request the agent suspend enforcement if payment is made, a formal payment arrangement is agreed with the creditor, or the debt is genuinely disputed.
  • Right to complain about agent conduct to the creditor, the certified bailiff register, or the County Court.

The obligations are equally clear:

  • Obligation not to obstruct. Physical obstruction of a certified agent is an offence; it also triggers immediate escalation (return with police, locksmith, or additional officers).
  • Obligation not to breach a CGA. Moving, selling, or damaging listed goods is a criminal offence under paragraph 68 of Schedule 12 to the 2007 Act.
  • Obligation to pay statutory fees. The compliance, enforcement, and sale fees are added to the debt and are collectable by the agent alongside the principal.

How to Respond to a Notice of Enforcement

The seven-day window in the Notice of Enforcement is the single most important point on the enforcement timeline. The responses in priority order:

  1. Pay the debt in full if the funds are available. Only the £75 compliance fee applies; the larger enforcement and sale fees never trigger.
  2. Agree a payment arrangement with the creditor. For HMRC, a Time to Pay arrangement negotiated inside the window usually suspends enforcement. For commercial creditors, a written instalment plan with an immediate first payment often does the same.
  3. Dispute the debt formally where grounds exist. A substantial dispute can support an application to the court to restrain enforcement — though this is specialist work and needs to be in motion by day 3 of the seven-day window.
  4. Seek licensed insolvency practitioner advice. Where the Notice of Enforcement signals broader insolvency, a CVA, administration, or CVL may be the right route. An IP instructed inside the window can often negotiate with the creditor on the company's behalf.
  5. Consider administration where multiple creditors are in enforcement mode. The statutory moratorium on administration stops enforcement action against the company by all creditors during the administration period.

Ignoring the notice is the one response with no defensible rationale. The seven days expire regardless; the agent attends on day 8; the enforcement fees begin; and the options narrow sharply.

Avoiding the Serious Consequences of Full Enforcement

Where the enforcement track is allowed to run to sale, the knock-on consequences compound beyond the immediate loss of assets:

  • Operational disruption. Goods removed from a working premises can halt trading — manufacturing equipment, service vehicles, retail stock.
  • Credit profile damage. County Court enforcement registers publicly; business credit agencies reflect the enforcement within weeks.
  • Supplier confidence loss. Trade suppliers notified via credit agencies often move to cash-on-delivery terms or withdraw credit.
  • Insolvency trigger. Enforcement by a priority creditor is often the practical trigger for a broader insolvency event — the debtor is unable to fund the next set of obligations, and the winding-up petition becomes unavoidable.

Where the underlying company is still viable, the enforcement action itself can be more damaging than the debt that triggered it. That asymmetry is the reason why early engagement — within the seven-day notice window — matters more than the debt size alone suggests.

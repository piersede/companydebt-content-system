Administration gives you court protection from creditors. A CVA lets you restructure your debts while you keep trading. They solve different problems, and choosing the wrong one wastes money and time you do not have.

We see directors treat these as interchangeable rescue options. They are not. Administration is a defensive tool: it stops creditor action immediately, appoints a professional to manage the company's affairs, and buys time under a statutory moratorium. A CVA is a negotiating tool: it proposes a deal to your creditors, and if 75% by value agree, everyone is bound by the terms. The choice between them depends on how severe the pressure is right now, whether the business is viable long-term, and how much control you are willing to give up.

Quick Answer: Administration vs CVA — Which Rescue Route?

When Administration Is the Right Choice

Administration is the right tool when you need creditor enforcement to stop immediately. The moment the administrator is appointed, an automatic moratorium prevents all legal proceedings, winding-up petitions, bailiff action, and creditor enforcement against the company. No other insolvency process provides this level of immediate protection.

We advise administration when:

  • A winding-up petition has been filed or is imminent and you need to stop it
  • The business has significant value as a going concern that would be destroyed by rushed liquidation
  • You need time to arrange a sale of the business to a buyer who will preserve jobs and contracts
  • Multiple creditors are escalating simultaneously and informal negotiation has failed

The trade-off is control. The administrator runs the company, not you. Your authority as a director is effectively suspended. The administrator decides whether to continue trading, which contracts to honour, and whether to sell the business or transition to liquidation. We see directors enter administration expecting to retain influence. In practice, you cooperate with the administrator and answer their questions. You do not direct the process.

Administration is also expensive. Administrator fees typically start at £15,000 and can run much higher for complex cases. These fees rank as an expense of the administration and are paid from the company's assets before any creditor distribution.

When a CVA Is the Right Choice

A CVA is the right tool when the business is fundamentally viable but has a debt problem it cannot resolve through normal trading. The CVA proposes a deal: creditors agree to accept a reduced payment (typically pence in the pound or a structured repayment over 3-5 years), and in return the company continues trading and the creditors receive more than they would in liquidation.

We advise a CVA when:

  • The underlying business generates enough cash to fund monthly repayments and cover current obligations
  • The debt problem is specific and identifiable — an HMRC arrears, a disputed supplier bill, a lease obligation
  • Creditors are likely to accept the proposal (the business has a credible future and the CVA offers better returns than liquidation)
  • You want to retain control of the company and continue trading under your own management

The advantage over administration is that you keep control. A licensed insolvency practitioner acts as nominee (to assess the proposal) and then as supervisor (to monitor compliance), but the day-to-day running of the company stays with you. The cost is lower, the process is less invasive, and the company continues as a going concern throughout.

The risk is that CVAs fail if the repayment plan was too ambitious or the business was not as viable as the proposal assumed. We have seen companies enter CVAs, struggle to meet the monthly payments for 18 months, and then terminate the arrangement and enter liquidation with higher total debts than when they started. A CVA only works if the numbers are honest and the business can genuinely sustain the repayments.

Comparing the Cost and Speed of Administration vs CVA

Administration costs from £15,000 upwards, takes effect immediately on appointment, and typically runs for a maximum of 12 months (extendable with court or creditor approval). The company either emerges from administration, is sold, or transitions to liquidation. We find that most administrations resolve within 6-12 months.

A CVA costs £5,000-£10,000 in nominee and supervisor fees, plus the ongoing repayments to creditors over the term of the arrangement. The proposal takes 4-8 weeks to prepare and put to creditors. If approved, the repayment plan runs for 3-5 years. We see the total cost to the company (fees plus repayments) as significantly higher than administration in absolute terms, but spread over years rather than concentrated upfront.

We tell directors: the cost comparison is not just about fees. Administration costs more upfront but resolves faster. A CVA costs less upfront but commits you to years of repayments. If the business cannot sustain those repayments, the CVA fails and you end up in liquidation anyway, having paid the CVA fees on top of the eventual liquidation costs.

Administration vs CVA: The Verdict

  • Creditor pressure is intense and immediate (petition filed, bailiffs instructed): Administration. The moratorium stops everything. A CVA proposal takes weeks to prepare and does not provide automatic protection.
  • Business is viable with a manageable debt restructure: CVA. You keep control, the cost is lower, and the business continues trading normally.
  • You need to sell the business quickly: Administration. The administrator can conduct a sale under the statutory framework with legal protection for the buyer.
  • Creditors are willing to negotiate but need a binding framework: CVA. It provides the structure and enforceability that informal negotiation lacks.
  • The business model is broken and debts are structural: Neither. Liquidation is the right answer.

FAQs About Administration vs CVA

Can I enter a CVA while a winding-up petition is active?

A CVA does not automatically stay a winding-up petition. You would need to apply to the court separately to adjourn the petition while the CVA is being proposed. Administration provides automatic protection against petitions. If a petition is already filed, administration is usually the safer route.

Can a company go from administration into a CVA?

Yes. One of the exit routes from administration is a CVA. The administrator can propose a CVA to creditors as a way of returning the company to the directors' control with a structured repayment plan. This is relatively common and combines the immediate protection of administration with the long-term restructuring of a CVA.

Do I lose control of my company in administration?

Yes. The administrator takes control of the company's affairs. Your authority as a director is effectively suspended. You cooperate with the administrator and provide information, but you do not make commercial decisions. In a CVA, you retain control under the supervision of the nominee/supervisor.

What happens if a CVA fails?

If the company fails to meet the CVA terms (typically missing consecutive monthly payments), the supervisor terminates the arrangement. Creditors are released from the CVA terms and can enforce their original claims in full. The company usually enters liquidation at that point, with higher total debts than before the CVA started.

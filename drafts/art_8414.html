A Time to Pay arrangement is an instalment plan with HMRC that lets you spread an overdue tax bill over 6 to 12 months. It is the single most effective tool for managing HMRC debt, and the single most wasted opportunity when directors leave it too late to call.

We facilitate TTP conversations between directors and HMRC every week. The pattern is consistent: directors who call before the deadline, or within a few weeks of missing it, get arrangements agreed within a single phone call. Directors who call six months late, after statutory demands have been served and enforcement agents instructed, face a much harder negotiation with a much lower success rate. The tool is the same. The timing changes everything.

Quick Answer: How Time to Pay Works

Call HMRC's Business Payment Support Service on 0300 200 3835. Explain that you cannot pay your tax bill. Propose a monthly repayment schedule. If HMRC agrees, enforcement is suspended, penalties may be frozen, and you repay the debt in instalments while continuing to meet current tax obligations. Interest accrues on the outstanding balance at the HMRC late payment rate (currently 7.5% per annum). If you miss a payment or fall behind on current obligations, HMRC can terminate the arrangement and resume enforcement immediately.

Which Tax Debts Qualify for Time to Pay?

TTP is available for all HMRC business tax debts:

  • VAT — quarterly or monthly returns
  • PAYE and employee NICs — monthly payroll deductions
  • Corporation Tax — annual company tax
  • CIS deductions — Construction Industry Scheme payments
  • Self Assessment — personal tax for directors paid through SA

We find that HMRC treats different tax types with different levels of urgency. PAYE and employee NICs are money you collected from employees on HMRC's behalf — they pursue these hardest and negotiate least. Corporation Tax is the company's own liability — HMRC is slightly more flexible. VAT sits in between. Understanding this hierarchy helps you prioritise which TTP to request first if you owe multiple tax types.

How to Negotiate a Time to Pay Arrangement

Before you call:

  • Know exactly what you owe. Check your Business Tax Account online at tax.service.gov.uk. The balance is updated daily.
  • Prepare a breakdown of your company's monthly income and expenses. HMRC will ask for this.
  • Have a realistic repayment proposal ready. What can you actually pay per month? Not what you hope to pay. What you can guarantee.
  • Make sure your tax returns are filed up to date. HMRC will not agree a TTP if you have outstanding returns because they cannot calculate the total debt.

On the call:

  • Be honest about why you cannot pay. HMRC hears every excuse. Honesty builds credibility.
  • Propose a specific monthly amount and duration. "I can pay £3,000 per month for 10 months" is better than "I need help."
  • Confirm you can meet current obligations during the TTP. This is non-negotiable. If you cannot pay the old debt AND keep up with new liabilities, TTP is not viable.
  • Ask for confirmation in writing. HMRC will send a letter confirming the arrangement. Keep it.

We find the call typically takes 20-30 minutes. The adviser has authority to agree arrangements up to 12 months on the spot. Longer terms or larger debts may be referred to a manager, which adds a few days.

What Happens If HMRC Rejects Your TTP Application

HMRC rejects TTP when they conclude the business is not viable, the proposed repayment is unrealistic, or you have a history of broken arrangements. We see rejections most commonly when:

  • The director called too late — enforcement has already started and HMRC has lost confidence
  • The company owes multiple tax types and is behind on all of them
  • A previous TTP was agreed and then broken
  • The company's financial position shows it cannot sustain the proposed repayments

If HMRC rejects your application, the options narrow: you can propose revised terms (lower monthly amount, longer period), engage a licensed insolvency practitioner to negotiate on your behalf (HMRC sometimes responds differently to professional representation), or explore formal restructuring options like a CVA that binds HMRC to a repayment plan through a creditor vote.

If none of these work and HMRC proceeds to enforcement, the next step is typically a statutory demand followed by a winding-up petition. At that point, the question shifts from "how do I pay the tax" to "should I close or save the company."

What Happens During the TTP Period

While the TTP is active:

  • Enforcement is suspended. No statutory demands, no bailiffs, no winding-up petitions for the agreed debt.
  • Interest continues to accrue. The late payment rate (currently 7.5%) applies to the outstanding balance throughout the arrangement.
  • You must make every payment on time. Miss one and HMRC can terminate the arrangement immediately.
  • You must meet all current tax obligations. New VAT returns, PAYE, and Corporation Tax must be paid on time. Falling behind on current liabilities while repaying old ones terminates the TTP.

We stress the current-obligations requirement because it is the most common reason TTPs fail. A director negotiates a TTP for last year's VAT, then misses this quarter's VAT because the repayments consumed the cash that was supposed to cover the new bill. The TTP collapses and the total debt is now larger than before. We advise directors to stress-test the numbers: can the business fund the TTP repayments AND meet current obligations? If the answer is no, the TTP is a displacement exercise, not a solution.

Time to Pay vs Other Options

  • TTP vs CVA: A TTP only covers HMRC debts. A CVA covers all creditors. If you owe HMRC and multiple trade creditors, a TTP alone will not solve the problem.
  • TTP vs informal creditor negotiation: A TTP is binding on HMRC for its duration. Informal arrangements with other creditors are not binding. TTP is stronger.
  • TTP vs administration: If HMRC has already filed a winding-up petition, a TTP is extremely unlikely. Administration provides the moratorium protection that a TTP cannot.

What You Should Do Right Now

If you owe HMRC any tax and cannot pay by the deadline, call 0300 200 3835 today. Not tomorrow. The acceptance rate drops measurably with every week of delay. A call before the deadline has the highest success rate. A call within 30 days of the deadline is good. A call after 3 months is harder. A call after a statutory demand is hardest of all.

FAQs About HMRC Time to Pay

How long can an HMRC Time to Pay arrangement last?

Typically 6 to 12 months. HMRC advisers can agree arrangements up to 12 months on the call. Longer terms are possible for larger debts but require manager approval and stronger evidence of viability. The shorter the arrangement, the more likely HMRC is to agree.

Does Time to Pay stop penalties and interest?

Interest continues to accrue at the HMRC late payment rate (currently 7.5%) throughout the arrangement. Penalties for late payment may be frozen while the TTP is active, depending on the tax type and your compliance history. The TTP stops enforcement action but does not eliminate the cost of being late.

Can I get a second TTP if my first one failed?

It is possible but significantly harder. HMRC's system records broken arrangements and the adviser will see the history. You will need to explain why the first arrangement failed and demonstrate that circumstances have changed. A second TTP with professional representation (an IP or tax adviser making the call) sometimes carries more weight than a director calling alone.

Do I need an accountant or IP to negotiate a TTP?

No. You can call 0300 200 3835 yourself. For straightforward arrangements (single tax type, realistic repayment, first-time TTP), the call is simple. For complex situations (multiple tax types, large debts, previous broken arrangements, or an active statutory demand), professional representation can improve the outcome.

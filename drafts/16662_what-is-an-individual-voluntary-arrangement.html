An Individual Voluntary Arrangement is a personal insolvency tool. It sits under Part VIII of the Insolvency Act 1986 and is used by individuals, including company directors who have signed personal guarantees, paid themselves through a director's loan account that has gone wrong, or drawn dividends from a company that turned out to be insolvent.

An IVA is not the same thing as a CVA, and if you are a director you need to know which side of that line your debt problem sits on.

An IVA compromises your personal unsecured debts over a fixed term, usually five years, on the back of a monthly contribution you can actually afford. A licensed insolvency practitioner drafts the proposal, creditors vote on it, and if 75% (by value) approve, every unsecured creditor is bound, including the ones who voted against.

The single most important call at proposal stage is the monthly figure. We have seen IVAs with a £450 contribution collapse in month seven when a van needed a new gearbox, and the same director later settle cleanly on a £320 proposal. Build the budget on a bad month, not a good one.

Your company debts, if they were on the company balance sheet, are not compromised; they travel with the company through a CVA, CVL, or another corporate process.

What an Individual Voluntary Arrangement Actually Covers

An IVA covers the debts that are personally yours. In a typical director's case that means:

  • Personal guarantees called by the bank, a landlord, a factoring company, or a supplier after the company stopped paying. These become a personal unsecured debt the moment they are called and can be compromised in an IVA.
  • Overdrawn director's loan accounts where the company, or a liquidator on behalf of the company, is chasing you for repayment under section 455 or as a debt to the estate.
  • Personal tax debts, Self Assessment income tax, Class 2/4 National Insurance on sole-trader income, the section 455 charge on a director's loan that was not cleared in time. HMRC is often the biggest unsecured creditor in a director IVA.
  • Credit cards, personal loans, car finance shortfalls, and other consumer debts in the director's own name.

What an IVA does not cover: secured debts (the mortgage on your house, the HP on a vehicle, those sit outside the arrangement), student loans, court fines, child maintenance, and debts incurred by fraud.

Company debts where the company is still the debtor, Corporation Tax, company VAT, company PAYE, are also outside. Those need a corporate route. For directors whose personal and company debts are entangled through PGs, our advisers almost always recommend a joint strategy across both sides, because resolving one without the other leaves the second problem unaddressed.

Who Qualifies for an Individual Voluntary Arrangement

The statutory entry test is simple: you are insolvent, you cannot pay your debts as they fall due or your liabilities exceed your assets, and you can still make a monthly contribution out of surplus income, or can offer a lump sum from a third party.

No minimum debt level, no maximum, though IVAs below around £10,000 of unsecured debt rarely make commercial sense for the creditors to accept.

Practical requirements creditors scrutinise:

  • Affordable monthly surplus, a realistic income and expenditure review using the Common Financial Statement spending guidelines. Creditors reject proposals built on optimistic budgets that fail by month six.
  • Return to creditors greater than bankruptcy, the statutory comparator. The IP's report has to show creditors do better in the IVA than if you were made bankrupt.
  • Clean conduct disclosure, no undisclosed assets, no recent transactions at undervalue, no preferences to connected creditors in the two years before.

For directors specifically, creditors look closely at the company's trajectory. If the company is still trading and your PG debt has not crystallised, an IVA proposal will usually need to wait until the company position is resolved, otherwise the IP cannot quantify the total unsecured debt.

The Real Consequences of an Individual Voluntary Arrangement

The case for an IVA is that it avoids bankruptcy, keeps your home (usually), keeps you out of the register of disqualified directors (an IVA is not a disqualification trigger on its own), and writes off the balance of unsecured debt at the end. That case is real. The trade-offs are also real and should not be soft-pedalled.

We set them out in full when we review a case, because a director who is surprised by the equity clause in year four is a director who did not receive proper advice at the outset.

  • Credit file impact, IVA registered on the Individual Insolvency Register and on your credit file for six years. During the IVA you cannot borrow more than £500 without the supervisor's consent.
  • Property equity, if you own a home, most IVAs require you to attempt to remortgage in year four or five to release equity for creditors. Fail to remortgage on reasonable terms and the IVA typically extends by twelve months instead.
  • Windfalls, inheritances, insurance payouts, bonuses above a threshold, flow into the arrangement for creditors during the five-year term.
  • Directorship, an IVA does not automatically disqualify you from being a director. Check your company's articles and any shareholder or loan agreement; some contain disqualification-on-insolvency clauses that can bite.
  • Regulated professions, solicitors, IFAs, accountants, some financial-services roles: you must notify your regulator, and some regulators treat an IVA as a serious event with consequences for your practising certificate.
  • Failure, if you default on payments and the supervisor petitions to terminate, the creditors can then petition for bankruptcy. Failure rates across the industry have historically been meaningful; a realistic monthly figure matters.

How the IVA Process Runs, Step by Step

Start to finish, expect three to four months from first conversation to approved IVA, then a five-year payment term.

  1. Initial review with a licensed IP, free, confidential, usually an hour. The IP assesses whether IVA is viable against the alternatives (debt management plan, bankruptcy, informal negotiation).
  2. Proposal drafting, the IP builds the statement of affairs, income and expenditure, comparator to bankruptcy, and proposes contribution and term.
  3. Interim Order (optional), application to the court under section 252 to freeze creditor action while the proposal is circulated. Not every case needs one; it is used where a creditor is threatening bankruptcy or enforcement.
  4. Creditors' decision, proposal sent to all creditors with at least 14 days' notice. Vote is by value of debt: 75% of voting creditors must approve. Connected creditors are capped at 50% of the "yes" vote to prevent abuse.
  5. Modifications, creditors often propose changes (higher contribution, earlier remortgage clause, tighter windfall clause). You can accept or reject, but rejecting a modification HMRC or a major bank insists on usually kills the approval.
  6. Supervision, once approved, the IP becomes your Supervisor, collects contributions, distributes to creditors, and reports annually.
  7. Completion, at the end of the term, the Supervisor issues a completion certificate, the IVA is removed from the Insolvency Register after three months, and the compromised debt is written off.

Costs and Fees on an Individual Voluntary Arrangement

Fees are taken from the contributions you make, you do not pay them on top. There are two main fee categories, and both are paid out of the monthly payments to creditors:

  • Nominee fee, for drafting the proposal and running the creditors' decision. Typically £1,500–£2,000 plus VAT, fixed and disclosed upfront.
  • Supervisor fee, for five years of supervision. Either a fixed annual fee or, more commonly, a percentage of funds distributed (commonly 15–20% plus VAT). Disbursements (Insolvency Service registration, adjudicator notices) are added.

Two practical points directors often miss. First, the fees come out of your payments before creditors get any money. So the first several months of contributions largely pay the nominee fee, not creditors.

Second, if the IVA fails early, you will have paid fees without clearing material debt. Our practice is to show the fee-recovery schedule alongside the creditor-dividend projection at the outset, so there are no surprises three years in.

Individual Voluntary Arrangement vs the Alternatives

Directors weighing an IVA usually have three other live options. The honest comparison:

  • Debt Management Plan, informal, no statutory binding, interest usually frozen but not always. Lower fees, no credit-file stigma beyond the arrears that already exist, creditors can still pursue. Works where debt is modest and creditors are cooperative.
  • Bankruptcy, faster (usually one year to discharge), lower upfront cost (£680 total for a debtor's own application via the adjudicator: £130 administration fee plus £550 deposit), but involves loss of assets including any home equity over the trustee's threshold, automatic director disqualification-in-substance while undischarged, and serious consequences for regulated professionals. Right answer where the debt is hopeless and there are no assets to protect.
  • Debt Relief Order, only available where debts are under £50,000, assets under £2,000, and surplus income under £75/month. Rarely applicable to directors.

The IVA wins where you have a home with equity, a realistic surplus income, and a debt load bankruptcy would crush but a DMP would not resolve. It loses where income is unstable, there are significant assets only bankruptcy would force a decision on, or creditors are small in number and willing to accept an informal compromise.

Informal compromise is the route directors most often overlook. Where HMRC is not the dominant creditor and the debt sits with two or three trade suppliers, we have closed matters with a round-table call, a pro-rata cheque and a written release, without any formal process or register entry. It does not work where the creditor mix includes a factoring house or an institutional lender.

Common Misunderstandings About Individual Voluntary Arrangements

  • "An IVA writes off 75% of my debt automatically", no. Write-off depends on contribution, asset value, and any equity release at year four or five. Some IVAs deliver a 70% write-off; others deliver 20%.
  • "HMRC does not accept IVAs", HMRC accepts IVAs regularly, but with modifications: normally a higher contribution, strict current-year tax compliance, and tight windfall clauses. An IVA proposal where HMRC is the main creditor needs to be pitched for HMRC approval from day one.
  • "My spouse will also be affected", only to the extent debts are jointly held. Your IVA does not compromise a spouse's separate debts, but joint debts remain pursuable against your spouse in full.
  • "I can keep running my business", yes in principle, but the proposal must be viable and any business income flows into the I&E. Self-employed IVAs need careful construction around VAT registration, variable earnings, and business assets.

Your Next Step on an Individual Voluntary Arrangement

Individual Voluntary Arrangement FAQs

Once the IVA is approved, HMRC's pre-IVA personal tax debt is compromised and HMRC cannot pursue it outside the arrangement. HMRC is bound by the vote like any other unsecured creditor. Post-IVA tax must be paid in full and on time, non-compliance with current-year tax is the single biggest reason HMRC petitions to terminate IVAs.

If you cannot remortgage, the IVA usually extends by twelve months instead. You do not lose the house.

An IVA does not automatically disqualify you under the Company Directors Disqualification Act 1986. However, many shareholder agreements, funder covenants, and company articles contain "disqualification on personal insolvency" clauses that can bite, check yours. Some regulated professions (solicitors, IFAs) must notify their regulator and may face practising-certificate consequences.

If you default on contributions and the Supervisor cannot agree a variation, the IVA terminates. Creditors' original debts are revived (less payments already made) and any creditor can petition for your bankruptcy. Failure is usually driven by income reducing below the contribution level; renegotiating a variation with creditors before default is always preferable to letting the IVA collapse.

An IVA is for an individual; a CVA is for a limited company. Different parts of the Insolvency Act 1986, Part VIII for IVAs, Part I for CVAs. If the debt sits on the company balance sheet, you need a CVA (or another corporate process). If the debt is personal, including called-up PGs, you need an IVA. Directors whose personal and company debts are entangled often need both, running in parallel.

Fees come out of your monthly contributions, not on top. Nominee fee typically £1,500–£2,000 plus VAT; supervisor fee typically 15–20% plus VAT of funds distributed over the five-year term, plus disbursements.

For a £300/month IVA, expect fees in the region of £6,000–£9,000 across the life of the arrangement, which is why the headline "percentage written off" figure matters less than the cash dividend creditors will actually receive.

If your company enters liquidation, your employees lose their jobs. But they do not lose all their rights. UK insolvency law provides substantial statutory protection for employees, including claims from the National Insurance Fund for unpaid wages, holiday pay, notice pay, and redundancy. Understanding what your staff are entitled to matters, because how you handle their redundancy shapes both their outcome and yours.

We work with directors every week who delay liquidation because they cannot face telling their staff. We understand the instinct, but the delay usually makes things worse for everyone. Employees who are kept in the dark until the last day get less notice, less time to prepare, and less goodwill toward you personally. Employees who are told honestly and early can start claiming from the statutory safety net while you manage the closure properly.

We have written this page to explain what happens to employees when a UK company enters liquidation, what they are entitled to, and what you as a director need to do to fulfil your obligations and protect your own position.

The Quick Answer for Directors

When a company enters liquidation, all employment contracts are terminated by the liquidator. Employees become preferential creditors for unpaid wages (up to £800, covering four months) and accrued holiday pay. They can also claim from the National Insurance Fund for up to 8 weeks' arrears of pay (capped at £643/week), up to 6 weeks' holiday pay, statutory notice pay, and statutory redundancy pay. The claims are handled by the Redundancy Payments Service, not by you.

We tell directors: the statutory safety net is not perfect, and higher-paid employees will not recover their full entitlement. But it is significantly better than nothing, and it means that the burden of employee claims in liquidation is shared between the company's assets and the state. Your job is to make sure your employees know about these rights, have the records they need to claim, and are treated with the honesty they deserve.

When Employees Are Made Redundant

In a CVL, employees are typically made redundant on or shortly after the date of the winding-up resolution. The liquidator may retain a small number of employees briefly to assist with the wind-down, but most staff are let go immediately. In a compulsory liquidation, the winding-up order itself terminates all employment contracts automatically.

We find that the timing of redundancy is one of the most painful parts of the process for directors. Many want to keep staff employed as long as possible, but every week of continued employment while the company is insolvent adds to the creditor claims and to your own wrongful trading exposure. The liquidator will assess whether retaining staff was justified by the needs of the wind-down or whether it was an emotional decision that increased losses to creditors.

What Employees Can Claim from the National Insurance Fund

The Employment Rights Act 1996 (Part XII) provides a statutory safety net for employees of insolvent employers. Claims are made to the Redundancy Payments Service (RPS), which is part of the Insolvency Service. We set out the entitlements below.

Arrears of pay: Up to 8 weeks' unpaid wages, capped at £643 per week (April 2024 rate). This covers wages earned but not paid before the company entered liquidation.

Holiday pay: Up to 6 weeks' accrued but untaken holiday pay, capped at £643 per week. This covers statutory holiday entitlement (5.6 weeks per year) that was earned but not taken.

Statutory notice pay: Based on length of service: 1 week for each year of employment, up to a maximum of 12 weeks, capped at £643 per week. This compensates employees for the notice period they did not receive.

Statutory redundancy pay: Calculated using the same formula as voluntary redundancy: 0.5 to 1.5 weeks' pay per year of service (depending on age), capped at £643 per week, with a maximum of 20 years' service. The maximum statutory redundancy payment is £19,290.

Pension contributions: Unpaid employer pension contributions for up to 12 months can also be claimed from the National Insurance Fund.

We advise directors to make sure their employees know about these entitlements. The RPS sends claim forms to employees once the liquidator has confirmed the insolvency, but we find that employees who understand the process in advance are less anxious and more cooperative during the wind-down. A 10-minute conversation explaining what they can claim and how to claim it costs you nothing and makes a significant difference to their experience.

Preferential Creditor Status for Employees

Employees are preferential creditors under Schedule 6 of the Insolvency Act 1986 for:

  • Up to 4 months' unpaid wages (capped at £800 per employee)
  • Accrued holiday pay

This means employees rank ahead of HMRC's secondary preferential claims and ahead of all unsecured creditors in the distribution waterfall. In practice, the preferential claim is usually subrogated to the RPS, meaning the National Insurance Fund pays the employee and then steps into their shoes as a preferential creditor in the liquidation. The employee gets paid from the Fund; the Fund recovers what it can from the liquidation.

We explain this because directors often assume they need to pay employees directly before liquidation to protect them. In most cases, the statutory safety net handles employee claims more effectively than ad hoc payments by directors, which can create preference risk if made when the company is insolvent.

TUPE: When Employees Transfer Instead of Being Redundant

If the liquidator sells the business (or part of it) as a going concern, the Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment) Regulations 2006 (TUPE) may apply. Under TUPE, employees transfer automatically to the new employer on their existing terms and conditions. They are not made redundant, and their continuity of service is preserved.

TUPE applies when there is a transfer of an economic entity that retains its identity. In practice, this means a business sale where the buyer takes on the trading operation, the customers, and the staff. It does not apply to a pure asset sale where the buyer takes the equipment but not the business as a going concern.

We see TUPE cause confusion in liquidation sales. The liquidator has a duty to maximise recoveries for creditors, and a sale that preserves jobs through TUPE can sometimes achieve a higher price than a breakup sale. But the buyer must accept the employees on their existing terms. If you are buying back the business through a new company (a pre-pack or phoenix arrangement), TUPE obligations transfer to the new company. We advise taking specialist employment law advice on any transaction where TUPE may apply.

Your Obligations as a Director

Your obligations to employees during liquidation include:

  • Collective consultation. If you are making 20 or more employees redundant at one establishment within 90 days, you must begin collective consultation at least 30 days before the first dismissal (or 45 days for 100+ employees). Failure to consult is a criminal offence and can result in a protective award of up to 90 days' pay per affected employee.
  • Individual notification. Each employee is entitled to written notice of their redundancy and the reasons for it.
  • Provide accurate records to the liquidator. The liquidator and the RPS need employment contracts, payroll records, holiday records, and redundancy calculations for every employee. Incomplete records delay employees' claims.
  • Notify the Insolvency Service. Within 14 days of a winding-up resolution (CVL) or winding-up order (compulsory), the liquidator must notify the RPS. In practice, the liquidator handles this, but you should ensure they have the employee information they need to do it promptly.

We see directors fail on collective consultation more often than any other obligation. If you are planning a CVL and the company has 20 or more employees, consultation must begin before the winding-up resolution is passed. This is a hard legal requirement, and the liquidator cannot retrospectively fix a failure to consult.

What You Should Do Next

If your company is approaching liquidation and you have employees, we advise:

  1. Prepare complete employee records now. Contracts, payroll history, holiday balances, and length-of-service calculations for every employee. The liquidation documents checklist includes the full list.
  2. Begin collective consultation if you have 20+ employees. This must start before the winding-up resolution.
  3. Talk to your staff honestly. Explain what is happening, what they can claim from the National Insurance Fund, and how the process works. They deserve to know.
  4. Do not make ad hoc payments to employees outside the normal payroll cycle if the company is insolvent. The statutory safety net handles employee claims. Payments outside the ordinary course create preference risk for you.
  5. Speak to a licensed insolvency practitioner who can advise on the timing and sequencing of employee redundancies within the liquidation process.

FAQs

Will my employees get their redundancy pay?

Yes. Employees with at least two years' continuous service are entitled to statutory redundancy pay, which they can claim from the National Insurance Fund if the company cannot pay. The maximum is £19,290 based on the current weekly cap. The Redundancy Payments Service processes these claims once the liquidator confirms the insolvency.

How long does it take for employees to receive their money?

The Redundancy Payments Service aims to process claims within 6 weeks of receiving a complete application. In practice, straightforward claims are often paid within 3 to 6 weeks. Complex cases or claims with incomplete records can take longer. Providing accurate employee records to the liquidator speeds up the process for everyone.

Do I have to consult employees before making them redundant?

If you are making 20 or more employees redundant at one establishment within 90 days, you must begin collective consultation at least 30 days before the first dismissal (45 days for 100+ employees). For fewer than 20 redundancies, there is no statutory collective consultation requirement, but individual consultation is still good practice and reduces the risk of unfair dismissal claims.

What happens if the business is sold as a going concern?

If the liquidator sells the business as a going concern, TUPE may apply, and employees transfer automatically to the new employer on their existing terms. They are not made redundant, and their continuity of service is preserved. The buyer must accept the transferring employees on their current terms and conditions. If TUPE applies, dismissing employees because of the transfer is automatically unfair.

Can employees claim for unfair dismissal in liquidation?

Redundancy because the company is closing is a fair reason for dismissal under the Employment Rights Act. However, if you failed to follow the correct consultation procedure or dismissed employees for reasons unrelated to the closure, they may have grounds for an unfair dismissal claim. In practice, unfair dismissal claims in liquidation are rare because the company has no assets to satisfy a tribunal award, but they can affect your personal conduct assessment if the failure was yours.

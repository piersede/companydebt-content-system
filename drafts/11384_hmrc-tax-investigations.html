The majority close civilly with modest adjustments; a minority escalate. Understanding where your case sits on that spectrum, and responding in the procedurally correct way, is what keeps outcomes in the civil track.

What HMRC Tax Investigations Are and How They Differ From Routine Checks

HMRC investigates UK taxpayers across four formal tracks, each with its own Code of Practice:

  • Compliance Check (COP1), routine review. No fraud suspicion.
  • Code of Practice 8 (COP8), serious enquiry into complex planning or avoidance. Fraud not specifically suspected.
  • Code of Practice 9 (COP9), civil investigation where HMRC suspects tax fraud. Contractual Disclosure Facility offered.
  • Criminal investigation, conducted under PACE procedure with prosecution as the objective.

The informal use of "tax investigation" in commercial conversation usually refers to any of these four. HMRC's internal handling is materially different across them. So knowing which Code applies to the letter on your desk is the first practical step.

When and Why HMRC Investigates UK Companies

HMRC's case selection is a mix of risk-based intelligence and random sampling:

  • Risk analytics, sharp movements in filed returns, industry-benchmark outliers, unusual ratios between payroll and profit or between VAT-able inputs and outputs.
  • Third-party information, bank reporting under the Common Reporting Standard, HMRC's Connect data-mining system, whistleblower disclosures.
  • Specific claim scrutiny, R&D credits, large capital allowances, loss carrybacks, share schemes.
  • DOTAS-disclosed scheme participation, marketed tax-avoidance schemes routinely produce enquiries across all scheme users.
  • Sector campaigns, construction, hospitality, online sellers, property landlords, crypto-asset investors.
  • Random selection, a small share of investigations open without risk basis, for compliance baseline measurement.

Understanding the trigger shapes your response. An R&D claim query needs tax-specialist representation with R&D expertise. A PAYE/IR35 enquiry needs someone who handles contractor classification. A COP9 letter needs a COP9 specialist immediately.

Where we audit case files against our referral network, the ones that close cleanest are where the director picked up the letter, read the Code citation in the top-right corner, and rang a specialist inside 48 hours. The ones that drift are those that spent a month with a high-street accountant writing general-purpose responses.

Key Risks and Consequences of an HMRC Tax Investigation

The consequences compound across four dimensions:

  • Additional tax, backdated to the year under enquiry, plus earlier years if patterns emerge.
  • Interest on late payment at HMRC's published rate (currently 7.75%, tracking Bank of England base rate + 4%).
  • Behaviour-based penalty under Schedule 24 of the Finance Act 2007, up to 30% for careless errors, 70% for deliberate, 100% for deliberate-and-concealed. Reducible for disclosure and cooperation.
  • Escalation risk, enquiries that uncover patterns suggesting fraud can escalate from COP1 to COP8/COP9 or criminal investigation.

In parallel, there are reputational and commercial consequences: director disqualification where misconduct is established, Personal Liability Notices for unpaid NIC where fraud or neglect is found, and the cash-flow impact of an accumulated settlement exceeding what your business can service.

Your Rights and HMRC's Powers During a Tax Investigation

HMRC's powers under Schedule 36 of the Finance Act 2008 are broad but not unlimited.

Taxpayer rights:

  • Representation by a qualified tax adviser or solicitor throughout.
  • Written reasons for any formal information notice.
  • Appeal to the First-tier Tribunal (Tax Chamber) within 30 days of any formal decision.
  • Statutory review (internal HMRC review by an officer not involved in the original decision).
  • Legal professional privilege over solicitor communications.
  • Right to decline voluntary meetings under COP1 (not under COP9 where disclosure meetings are part of the CDF).

Your responsibilities as the taxpayer:

  • Produce documents required under formal Schedule 36 notices within the stated deadline. Non-compliance triggers penalties of £300 plus £60 per day.
  • Preserve records, destruction is a separate offence and supports increased penalty categories.
  • Answer specific questions truthfully within the enquiry's scope.

How to Respond to an HMRC Tax Investigation

The sequence that produces the best outcomes:

  1. Identify the Code of Practice from the letter. Response strategy depends sharply on whether it is COP1, COP8, COP9, or criminal.
  2. Instruct appropriate representation. Routine check: your usual accountant may suffice. Complex or fraud-suspected: specialist tax adviser or solicitor with direct COP8/COP9 experience.
  3. Preserve all records. Tax returns, accounts, bank statements, correspondence, contracts. Do not edit or "tidy".
  4. Consider unprompted disclosure where errors are known. Disclosure before HMRC identifies the issue produces the largest Schedule 24 penalty reduction (down to 0% in some careless cases).
  5. Respond through a single channel, typically the representing professional. Multiple director-level conversations with HMRC officers produce inconsistent records and are avoidable.
  6. Consider parallel insolvency advice if the likely settlement threatens business viability. A licensed IP can model cash-flow impact and formal-process alternatives.

If you cannot pay your VAT bill, HMRC already knows. They know the return has been filed, they know the amount, and the enforcement clock starts the day after the deadline passes. What you do in the next 48 hours determines whether this becomes a payment plan or a winding-up petition.

Quick Answer: What to Do If You Cannot Pay VAT

Call HMRC's Business Payment Support Service on 0300 200 3835 before the payment deadline if possible, or as soon as you realise you cannot pay. Ask for a Time to Pay (TTP) arrangement. HMRC will assess whether your business is viable and whether you can sustain a repayment schedule. If they agree, you get an instalment plan (typically 6-12 months), enforcement is suspended, and surcharges may be frozen. If you do not call, HMRC will escalate through surcharges, penalties, debt collection, statutory demands, and ultimately a winding-up petition.

We tell every director: the phone call costs nothing. The silence costs everything.

What Happens If You Miss a VAT Payment

HMRC's enforcement timeline for unpaid VAT follows a predictable pattern. We set it out here so you can see exactly what is coming and when.

Day 1 after deadline: The debt is live. HMRC's system flags the non-payment automatically. No human has looked at it yet, but the clock is running.

Weeks 2-4: A default surcharge notice arrives. Under the VAT default surcharge regime, repeated late payments trigger escalating surcharges: 2%, 5%, 10%, then 15% of the VAT due. For a £50,000 quarterly VAT bill, a 15% surcharge is £7,500 on top of the original debt. We see directors who assumed the surcharge was capped at a few hundred pounds. It is not.

Weeks 4-8: HMRC issues formal demand letters. The tone shifts from administrative to enforcement. These letters are not bluffs. They are the precursor to debt collection referral.

Weeks 8-12: HMRC may instruct their own enforcement agents (formerly bailiffs) under Taking Control of Goods powers, or refer the debt to a debt collection agency. They can also issue a statutory demand for debts over £750, which is the formal gateway to a winding-up petition.

Weeks 12+: If the debt remains unpaid and no TTP is in place, HMRC can petition the court to wind up your company. HMRC is the single most common petitioning creditor in compulsory liquidations. We have seen petitions filed over VAT debts as low as £5,000. They do not need a large debt to act.

HMRC Time to Pay: How to Negotiate a VAT Payment Plan

Time to Pay is HMRC's formal instalment arrangement. It is the single most effective tool for dealing with VAT debt, and we recommend it to every director before any other option.

How to apply: Call the Business Payment Support Service on 0300 200 3835. You do not need a solicitor or an insolvency practitioner to make this call. Explain that you cannot pay the VAT, explain why (cash-flow timing, a bad debt, a delayed contract), and propose a repayment schedule you can realistically meet.

What HMRC assesses: Is the business viable? Can you demonstrate that the repayment schedule is sustainable? Are you current on all other tax obligations? HMRC is significantly more likely to agree if you are up to date on PAYE, Corporation Tax, and current VAT returns. If you are behind on everything, TTP becomes harder to negotiate.

Typical terms: 6-12 months, with equal monthly instalments. Interest accrues on the outstanding balance at the HMRC late payment rate (currently 7.5%). You must keep up with current VAT obligations as they fall due during the TTP period. Miss a payment on the TTP or fall behind on a current return, and HMRC can terminate the arrangement and resume enforcement immediately.

We find that directors who call HMRC proactively, before enforcement starts, have an acceptance rate we estimate at 70-80%. Directors who call after receiving a statutory demand see that rate drop to under 30%. Timing is the single biggest factor.

VAT as a Preferential Debt: Why It Matters

Since December 2020, VAT is a secondary preferential debt in insolvency. This means that if your company enters liquidation, HMRC's VAT claim ranks ahead of floating charge holders and unsecured creditors in the creditor priority order. This changed the dynamics significantly: HMRC recovers more from insolvency than before 2020, which makes them less willing to negotiate and more willing to petition, because the outcome of liquidation is now better for them.

We stress this because directors who remember the old regime (where HMRC was an unsecured creditor for VAT) assume HMRC will be patient. They are less patient than they were, because the incentive structure has changed. A winding-up petition now recovers more for HMRC than it used to.

Personal Liability for Unpaid VAT

VAT is a tax you collect on behalf of HMRC from your customers. If the company collected VAT and failed to remit it, HMRC can pursue you personally in certain circumstances. The main routes are:

  • Wrongful trading: If you continued trading while insolvent and the company's VAT debt increased during that period, the liquidator can seek a personal contribution under section 214
  • Joint and several liability: In VAT group registrations, HMRC can pursue any member of the VAT group for the full amount, including the representative member personally in some structures
  • Fraudulent evasion: Deliberately under-declaring VAT or failing to register when required is a criminal offence carrying up to 7 years' imprisonment

We are direct: for most directors of legitimate businesses, the personal liability risk from unpaid VAT comes through wrongful trading, not through personal VAT liability notices. The risk is that you continued trading while the company was insolvent and the VAT debt grew. That growth is what the liquidator measures.

FAQs About Unpaid VAT

Can HMRC wind up my company for unpaid VAT?

Yes. HMRC can petition the court to wind up your company for any debt over £750. They are the most common petitioning creditor in UK compulsory liquidations. VAT debts do not need to be large for HMRC to act.

Will HMRC accept a payment plan for VAT?

Yes, through a Time to Pay arrangement. Call 0300 200 3835 with a realistic repayment proposal. Acceptance is significantly more likely if you call before enforcement action starts and are current on other tax obligations.

Am I personally liable for the company's unpaid VAT?

Not directly for the VAT itself (unlike PAYE). But if the company enters insolvency and you continued trading while the VAT debt grew, you face wrongful trading exposure. In VAT group registrations, joint and several liability can apply. Deliberate VAT fraud creates criminal liability.

What are the penalties for late VAT payment?

Under the current regime, late VAT payments incur penalties calculated as a percentage of the outstanding VAT. For payments up to 15 days late, no penalty. 16-30 days late: 2% of the VAT owed at day 15. Over 30 days late: 2% of the VAT owed at day 15 plus 2% of the VAT owed at day 30. Daily interest also accrues at the HMRC late payment rate.
